# Two Layers of Instability in Causal Estimation 

Alexis Bellot 

## Abstract 

There is a precise sense in which drawing causal inferences from observational data is hard , even when identifiability is assumed. In particular, Robins and Ritov (1997); Robins et al. (2003) showed that causal effects can be discontinuous as a function of the data distribution: two arbitrarily close data distributions might correspond to different causal effects. This is a fact independent of the choice of estimator; however, not all estimators are equally unstable. Our contribution is to surface a second layer of instability that depends on the choice of estimator. We show that many standard point estimates can be read as point summaries of multimodal distributions over the space of structural causal models. As such, estimators can jump discontinuously in the data distribution. This defines a taxonomy of estimators that admits a decision-theoretic reading: stability depends on whether the implicit loss function an estimator optimizes is aligned with the causal effect itself. Specifically, inverse propensity weighted estimators and regression estimators are examples of discontinuous summaries, while explicit posterior means and medians are shown to be continuous. 

## 1 Introduction 

For many practical purposes we want to know what would happen were we to act upon a given system. The answers to such questions depend not just on the data distribution, but also on deeper features of underlying data-generating models or mechanisms (Pearl, 2009). This statement surfaces a structural concern: the causal effect may not be reducible to a functional of the data distribution, in general, unless some additional knowledge such as the (partial) dependency structure encoded in a causal diagram or equivalence class is assumed. In the literature, precise conditions have been derived to determine when such a reduction is possible (Tian and Pearl, 2002; Bareinboim et al., 2022; Shpitser and Pearl, 2006; Shpitser et al., 2012; Pearl and Bareinboim, 2022; Huang and Valtorta, 2008). On the other hand, there is a statistical concern that asks how to estimate an identified causal effect from finite samples of the data distribution when available, see e.g. (Jung et al., 2021; Kennedy, 2023). A causal effect being identifiable, however, does not necessarily entail well-posed estimation as in general two arbitrarily close distributions P and Q may correspond to different causal effects even when the causal effect is identifiable. This property makes causal estimation discontinuous in the data distribution. This problem has parallels in the nonparametric regression literature (Stone, 1980) but can be significantly more severe in the context of causal estimation. Versions of this result were first shown by Robins and Ritov (1997); Robins et al. (2003) and have been revisited more recently by Schulman and Srivastava (2016); Gordon et al. (2021); Aronow et al. (2021); Maclaren and Nicholson (2019), who also highlight the advantage that experimental data can confer even when queries are identifiable from observational data (experimental data leads to a more stable estimation problem). The contribution we make in this paper is to show that the choice of estimator can add a second layer 

of instability that is independent of the identification map P Þ Ñ ΨpP q :“ EP rYxs itself. We argue that many standard point estimates can be read as ˆΨpP q “ T pΠP q, where ΠP is a distribution over

86.1 Is effect-aligned summarization more stable? 

The claim is justified in the sense that effect-aligned summaries such as T “ E are Lipschitz in ϵ for fixed n, δn, and prior hyperparameters (Corol. 2 and Prop. 2), while model-selective summaries are not (Thm. 3). However, several caveats are worth surfacing. Firstly, model-selective and effect-aligned summaries typically target different estimands rather than being different estimators for the same estimand. As such they strike different bias-variance tradeoffs and our discussion can be equally framed within the classical theory of regularization of ill-posed inverse problems (Evans and Stark, 2002; Maclaren and Nicholson, 2019). Whether one estimand is preferred over the other depends on our relative tolerance for bias and variance. For example, T “ E

trades off potentially infinite variance in non-parametric model spaces for non-zero bias, while some instances of model-selective reporting are unbiased but involve larger variance. Secondly, in many practical settings, the difference between selection strategies collapses. For example, in a strongly-convex log-likelihood, identifiable, parametric regime, the posterior is asymp-totically Gaussian and unimodal, and mode and mean concentrate on the same value at rate n´1{2.The setting that is problematic is the nonparametric, multimodal regime that arises under imperfect overlap and rich SCM classes. The taxonomy this paper analyzes is thus informative in classes broad enough that one cannot rule out multimodality a priori .Thirdly, our results study the question of stability as robustness to noise, not consistency. That is, we are interested in bounding how much the estimate moves under small perturbations of P ; but do not bound how far the estimate is from the true effect (which connects to the bias-variance tradeoff). A misspecified prior or restricted hypothesis class produces a stably wrong estimator. We restrict to point-identified targets throughout; the same T -taxonomy applies whenever one reports a point summary of SCM uncertainty. Finally, given that we quantify uncertainty over the causal effect through a posterior distribution over SCMs, uncertainty from instability and uncertainty from under-identification of the causal effect can both be expressed with the same framework. An explicitly Bayesian approach (if a reasonable prior can be defined) has the potential to honestly quantify a wide range of inherent difficulties in the process of drawing causal inferences from observational data. 

## 7 Conclusion 

Causal inference from observational data can be unstable even when a causal effect is point-identified. A first layer of difficulty is that causal effects can be discontinuous as a function of the data distribution: two arbitrarily close data distributions might correspond to different causal effects (Robins and Ritov, 1997; Robins et al., 2003; Maclaren and Nicholson, 2019). This is a property of the identification map itself, before any reporting rule or estimator is chosen. We show that a second layer of difficulty can be defined as “ summarization instability ” that is a consequence of collapsing a multimodal distribution over the space of structural causal models to a single number. Many standard point estimates can be read as such summaries. Among them we define a class of “model-selective” summaries that can jump discontinuously in P when compatible SCMs tie, independently of the discontinuity in the identification map. These include the posterior mode, plug-in estimators, and more generally any rule that reports the causal effect under a single selected SCM. On the other hand, there is a class of “effect-aligned” summaries that includes the posterior mean, median, and quantiles, shown to be continuous in P under regularity conditions. Further, this taxonomy can be explained through a decision-theoretic reading that shows that stability depends on whether the implicit loss of the estimator is aligned with model identity or with the causal effect.

12 A Related Work 

A large body of work studies when causal quantities can be expressed as functionals of the ob-servational (or experimental) data distribution, given graphical or algebraic assumptions about the data-generating process (Tian and Pearl, 2002; Shpitser and Pearl, 2006; Shpitser et al., 2012; Huang and Valtorta, 2008; Bareinboim et al., 2022; Pearl and Bareinboim, 2022). Complementary to these structural results is a statistical literature on how to estimate identified effects from finite samples, including semiparametric efficiency theory, double machine learning, and doubly robust estimation (Robins and Ritov, 1997; Jung et al., 2021; Kennedy, 2023). Our focus is orthogonal to both: we take identifiability as given and ask when estimation from the data distribution is well-posed in the sense of continuity in P . As the introduction notes, identifiable causal functionals need not be continuous in P ; closeness of distributions does not imply closeness of effects. That gap between identification and stable estimation is the starting point for the present work. The discontinuity of causal estimation has been documented in several settings. Robins et al. (2003) show that in sufficiently rich DAG model classes there need not exist uniformly consistent estimators or valid confidence intervals for causal effects, even when effects are identifiable. Robins and Ritov (1997) and Stone (1980) connect related difficulties to curse-of-dimensionality phenomena and optimal nonparametric rates. More recent work makes the instability explicit as a sensitivity of the identification map: Schulman and Srivastava (2016) give illustrative examples where tiny changes in the data distribution flip the implied causal answer; Gordon et al. (2021) bound condition numbers for causal inference; and Aronow et al. (2021) argue that nonparametric identification alone does not deliver reliable inference without randomized evidence on relevant parts of the distribution. These results concern the causal functional Ψ and the map from P to the set of compatible SCMs. Our Sec. 2 (extrapolation gap and overlap) situates this paper in that line by quantifying how off-support regions of P drive discontinuity. The tension between uniqueness (identifiability) and continuity (stable estimability) is classical in inverse problems and robust statistics. Hadamard’s notion of a well-posed problem requires existence, uniqueness, and stability of the solution; identifiability corresponds to injectivity of a forward map, which does not guarantee a continuous inverse. Maclaren and Nicholson (2019) develop this perspective for causal inference explicitly, distinguishing “what can be identified” from “what can be estimated” and relating intrinsic barriers to estimability to ill-posedness. Evans and Stark (2002) and Horowitz (2014) review parallel ideas in statistics and econometrics, where irregular identification and ill-posed inverse problems arise routinely. Regularization—penalizing complexity of solutions or aggregating over a set of compatible models—is the standard remedy for ill-posedness (Tikhonov and Arsenin, 1977). We connect to that tradition in Sec. 6: effect-aligned summaries can be read as regularized targets (bias–variance tradeoff) relative to model-selective plug-in rules, but our emphasis is on continuity in P as a property of the summarization rule T , not only on finite-sample risk. Related work on partial identification (Manski, 1990; Balke and Pearl, 1994; Zhang et al., 2022; Bellot, 2023) studies bounds and posterior uncertainty when point identification fails. Our focus is complementary: we take point identification as given and ask how different point summaries T pΠP q

behave under perturbations of P .Bayesian approaches to causal inference already maintain a distribution over compatible models, mechanisms, or identified sets. Partial-identification methods represent ambiguity via posterior mass over effect ranges (Zhang et al., 2022; Jalaldoust et al., 2024); flexible outcome models integrate over uncertain response surfaces (Hill, 2011; Hahn et al., 2020); and structure-learning pipelines maintain posteriors over graphs or equivalence classes (Claassen and Heskes, 2012). These contributions primarily address what uncertainty to represent . Our focus is orthogonal: given a causal posterior 

Ψ#ΠP , different point summaries T have different stability profiles under perturbations of the observational distribution P . In particular, many Bayesian workflows are only partially aggregative at the reporting stage. A pipeline that is Bayesian over graphs or mechanisms but reports the MAP structure and a plug-in effect remains model-selective and inherits the discontinuities of Thm. 3, even when ΠP itself is computed exactly. Conversely, effect-aligned summaries—posterior means, medians, quantiles, and other weakly continuous functionals on PpRq—inherit continuity from Prop. 2. Flexible Bayesian estimators such as BART are stable insofar as they report expectations over the causal functional; instability re-enters when implementation collapses to selecting one fitted mechanism or hyperparameter configuration (Hill, 2011; Hahn et al., 2020). 13 B Proofs
