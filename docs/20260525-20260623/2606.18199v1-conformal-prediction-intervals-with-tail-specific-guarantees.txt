Abstract 

This paper extends classical conformal frameworks for constructing prediction intervals with global marginal coverage 1 вҲ’ Оұ to intervals that provide explicitly calibrated guarantees for the upper and lower tails separately. Focusing on split conformal prediction, we first construct lower and upper one-sided conformal intervals that achieve marginal validity, and then derive the induced two-sided interval by intersection. Theoretical results prove both tail-specific and global marginal coverage of the induced two-sided interval. Results are presented first for the exchangeable setting, where coverage has finite-sample guarantees, and then for non-exchangeable data, where guarantees are asymptotic. Simulation studies show that the proposed approach achieves improved directional calibration relative to classical two-sided intervals, especially relevant in skewed data. Finally, the benefit of the proposed framework is showcased in a financial application, where one aims for return maximization while seeking strict control on the left tail. 

Keywords: Conformal prediction for skew data, Finite-sample tail coverage, Reliable risk management, Robust conformal prediction, Uncertainty quantification

# 1 Introduction 

Consider a set of n training data Dn = (Xi, Y i)ni=1 , where each i-th pair consists of a feature vector Xi вҲҲ Rp, p вүҘ 1, and an associated scalar response Yi вҲҲ Y вҠҶ R. Let Xn+1 

denote a (new) test feature. Having observed Dn and Xn+1 , the objective is to construct a prediction interval Cn+1 := C(Xn+1 ) вҠҶ Y that is likely to contain, or вҖң cover вҖқ, the true value of the unseen test response Yn+1 . Conformal prediction (CP) offers a valid yet flexible distribution-free framework for this predictive inference problem, under the sole assumption of exchangeability (Vovk et al. 2005, Lei et al. 2018). Specifically, if (X1, Y 1), . . . , (Xn+1 , Y n+1 )

are exchangeable, a CP interval is guaranteed to satisfy 

P (Yn+1 вҲҲ C n+1 ) вүҘ 1 вҲ’ Оұ, Оұ вҲҲ (0 , 1) , (1) where the probability is taken over the joint distribution of the training data and the test observation, in line with the frequentist philosophy. For a comparison with prediction intervals under the Bayesian paradigm, see Deliu & Liseo (2026). This frequentist guarantee is often referred to as marginal coverage or statistical validity . Importantly, this property holds in finite samples , without assumptions on the form of the underlying distribution. Moreover, it remains compatible with arbitrarily complex predictive models, including modern regression models as well as machine learning (ML) algorithms. The versatility of CP has contributed to its growing popularity as a tool to quantify uncer-tainty in modern statistical practice. Yet, standard CP methods are not without limitations. For example, the marginal guarantee in Eq. (1) does not extend to conditional coverage given Xn+1 and exact conditional coverage is impossible without further (distributional) assumptions (Lei & Wasserman 2014, Barber et al. 2021). The most practical remedies rely on adaptive nonconformity scores, such as conformalized quantile regression (CQR; Romano et al. 2019), which produce intervals that are more responsive to local heteroscedas-ticity. Another challenge concerns the exchangeability assumption, which fails in many 2realistic settings, ranging from covariate shift (Tibshirani et al. n.d., Qin et al. 2025) to temporal dependence (Gibbs & Candes 2021); more general forms of distribution drift and corresponding remedies are discussed in Barber et al. (2023). The challenges outlined above all concern a global or average form of coverage of the entire response range, with no regard for direction. In this work, we identify and address a complementary challenge: in many real-world prediction and decision problems, the two tails of the predictive distribution are inherently asymmetric and carry different consequences. Two paradigmatic examples illustrate this point. 

Example 1. In portfolio management, an investor seeks to maximize returns while requiring explicit protection against extreme losses. Both positive and negative deviations from the expected return must be characterized, but the downside carries far more severe consequences, demanding stricter probabilistic guarantees. Value-at-Risk formalizes this asymmetry by focusing on the left tail of the distribution (McNeil et al. 2015). Two-sided risk measures extend this idea to jointly consider the two tails (Chen & Wang 2008, Farinelli et al. 2008). 

Example 2. A similar asymmetry arises in medicine: when monitoring a patientвҖҷs blood pressure, a clinician must guard against both extremes, which reflect hypotension and hypertension; yet, the emphasis often has a direction. In post-surgical hypertensive patients, for instance, the primary concern is to detect dangerous spikes above a safety threshold, as values that are too high may trigger stroke. Analogously, in early-phase clinical trials, a new treatment must satisfy simultaneous constraints on toxicity and efficacy, where the two thresholds carry inherently different stakes (Thall & Cook 2004). In all such settings, the decision-maker must account for both tails, but each demands its own probabilistic guarantee. 

Common methods for constructing prediction intervals, including the CP framework, do not directly respond to this two-directional objective. They distribute coverage across both tails, seeking for the marginal guarantee in Eq. (1) , which ensures that the prediction 3interval contains the true response with high probability on average, but provides no explicit control over individual tail probabilities. In particular, it does not distinguish between coverage failures in the lower tail and those in the upper tail, leaving directional calibration unaddressed. This motivates the development of more robust prediction intervals that deliver separate and explicitly calibrated guarantees for each tail, allowing practitioners to assign stricter coverage requirements where the cost of error is more severe. 

1.1 Specific Contributions and Organization 

This work proposes a CP framework that strengthens classical marginal coverage with explicitly calibrated guarantees for the upper and lower tails of Yn+1 separately. Focusing on split CP (Vovk et al. 2005), this is achieved by constructing one-sided CP intervals for each tail and recovering a two-sided interval through their intersection. The specific contributions of this work are fourfold. 

C1 We introduce a stronger notion of marginal validity with tail-specific coverage (Section 2) and propose a general CP procedure that allows explicit control on the left and right tails with user defined miscoverage rates ОұвҲ’ and Оұ+, respectively (Section 3). We present a variation of the quantile score that retains the sign of the residual relative to the estimated quantile, in contrast to the truncated version used in standard CQR. 

C2 In Section 4, we establish theoretical guarantees for the proposed CP framework, proving finite-sample tail-specific and marginal coverage under exchangeability (Section 4), and asymptotic counterparts under non-exchangeable data (Section 4). 

C3 We illustrate the empirical benefits of the proposed framework through simulation studies (Section 5), demonstrating improved directional calibration relative to classical two-sided CP intervals, with particular gains in skewed and heavy-tailed settings, and show the advantage of the signed quantile score over its truncated counterpart. 4C4 In Section 6, we showcase the framework in a financial application ( Example 1 ), where the asymmetry between downside risk and upside return motivates strict probabilistic control over the left tail alongside a robust indication of potential gains. 

# 2 Preliminaries 

2.1 Problem Statement 

Given a training set Dn = (Xi, Y i)ni=1 and a test feature Xn+1 , our goal is to construct a prediction interval for the new response Yn+1 , say Cn+1 вҠҶ Y , that not only satisfies the marginal coverage guarantee of Eq. (1) , but also provides explicitly calibrated guarantees on each tail of the distribution of Yn+1 . Assuming Cn+1 takes the form of a single interval Cn+1 =[Ln+1 , U n+1 ] вҖ“ which holds whenever the response variable has a unimodal distribution, but also under less restrictive conditions that will be discussed in Section 3 вҖ“ the joint set of guarantees we seek is 

P (Yn+1 вҲҲ [Ln+1 , U n+1 ]) вүҘ 1 вҲ’ Оұ, (marginal coverage) 

P (Yn+1 > U n+1 ) вүӨ Оұ+ and P (Yn+1 < L n+1 ) вүӨ ОұвҲ’, (tail-specific coverage) with ОұвҲ’ + Оұ+ = Оұ вҲҲ (0 , 1) . The tail-specific coverage condition is strictly stronger than marginal coverage: it decomposes the total miscoverage Оұ into directional components, ensuring that no tail is systematically under-protected. This objective relates to a classical distinction between equal-tailed intervals , which allocate 

Оұ/ 2 to each tail, highest density regions , which yield the shortest interval at a given coverage level (Hyndman 1996, Deliu & Liseo 2024), and intervals symmetric around a point predictor. The tail-specific intervals proposed here occupy a different point in this landscape: they target directional reliability, allowing the assignment of asymmetric miscoverages, ОұвҲ’ and 

Оұ+, to reflect the asymmetric consequences of errors in each tail. This is particularly 5valuable when the response is skewed or heavy-tailed, settings where a single global coverage conceals meaningful asymmetries between the two tails. In what follows, we first review the standard split CP framework in both the exchangeable and non-exchangeable setting, before developing the proposed tail-specific construction. 

2.2 Background on Split Conformal Prediction 

There are two main approaches to implement conformal prediction: full CP and split CP (Vovk et al. 2005, Fontana et al. 2023). While the former is computationally demanding and consequentially rarely applicable in many practical settings, split CP proposes a computationally efficient procedure that decouples model training and calibration in a similar flavor to the validation-set approach (James et al. 2021). The most common approach for implementing CP is known as the split CP (Vovk et al. 2005), which can be summarised as follows. The available data are partitioned into two disjoint subsets, 

Dnt , a proper training set of cardinality nt on which a fitted model МӮ fnt is obtained, and 

Dnc , a calibration set of size nc which is used to compute the so-called calibration or conformity scores. The central feature of CP, compared to, e.g., asymptotic prediction intervals or resampling methods, is, in fact, the use of a (non-)conformity score function ,say s : X Г— Y вҶ’ R, that quantifies the (dis-)similarity or (non-)conformity of any point 

(X, Y ) to an observed sample D. # 6 An Application to Financial Forecasting 

As motivated in Example 1 , financial return forecasting presents a canonical problem of two-sided asymmetry: an investor simultaneously cares about downside risk (extreme losses) and upside potential (large gains), yet the left tail carries far more severe consequences and demands stricter probabilistic control. Value-at-Risk (VaR) formalizes the left-tail objective, quantifying the maximum loss that an investment can experience with a specified probability over a specific period of time (McNeil et al. 2015). Its accurate estimation is critical for regulatory compliance, capital allocation, and financial risk management. Despite being central, a framework focused exclusively on VaR foregoes characterization of the upper tail, which carries its own financial relevance, giving explicit information on potential gains and guiding portfolio optimization (Chen & Wang 2008, Farinelli et al. 2008). This paper develops a conformal prediction methodology designed to control lower- and upper-tail prediction errors separately, rather than only enforcing the usual global marginal coverage requirement at level 1 вҲ’ Оұ. We establish coverage guarantees for each tail and derive the corresponding global coverage properties of the resulting interval. The analysis covers both the exchangeable case, where the guarantees are finite-sample, and the non-exchangeable case, where adaptive procedures yield asymptotic validity, as well as short-term regret bounds. The simulation results indicate that the proposed construction substantially improves tail-specific calibration compared with standard two-sided conformal intervals, particularly in skewed and heavy-tailed environments. A financial application further confirms these findings: only the intersection-based procedures are able to deliver reliable tail-specific coverage, at the cost of slightly less efficient prediction intervals. Several directions for future research emerge naturally from the present work. First, while the proposed intersection-based construction provides explicit tail-specific coverage guarantees, it does not recover the tight finite-sample global validity achieved by classical two-sided conformal prediction. Extensions may consider coupled-tail calibration procedures beyond the independent treatment of the two tails adopted here. Second, extending the framework toward weighted conformal inference schemes (Tibshirani et al. n.d., Schmitt 2024) appears particularly promising in non-stationary settings, where adaptive weighting mechanisms may allow the calibration procedure to respond more effectively to evolving distributional regimes and covariate shifts over time. Third, integrating the proposed tail-specific construction within a Bayesian conformal prediction framework (Deliu & Liseo 2026) represents another promising direction. By incorporating prior and posterior uncertainty into the calibration mechanism, such an extension may lead to more informative prediction intervals and potential efficiency gains. 28 References
