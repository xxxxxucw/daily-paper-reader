# Conformal Prediction for Dyadic Regression Under Complex Missingness 

## Robert Lunde 1, Minjie Yang 1, Elizaveta Levina 2, and Ji Zhu 21Department of Statistics and Data Science, Washington University in Saint Louis 

2Department of Statistics, University of Michigan 

Abstract 

We develop a framework for conformal prediction in dyadic regres-sion problems under complex missingness mechanisms. At the theoretical level, we develop general technical tools for establishing finite-sample va-lidity of conformal prediction under distributional invariance conditions weaker than exchangeability. A key result handles the case where the sample itself is a random subset of the index set, a setting not covered by existing theory, via a novel bijection argument that constructs an ex-plicit measure-preserving correspondence between events. In addition, we propose conformal prediction procedures for jointly exchangeable ar-rays, including full conformal, split conformal, a row-column approach exploiting similarities within rows and columns, and a selective conformal procedure achieving mask-conditional validity. For missing elements, we establish asymptotic validity of a weighted conformal procedure under a nonparametric graphon model for the missingness mechanism. We fur-ther establish conditional validity results for both continuous and discrete responses; to the best of our knowledge, this is the first formal proof of asymptotic conditional validity for weighted conformal prediction under a missing-not-at-random assumption. The proposed methods are illustrated on synthetic and real network data. 

Keywords— Conformal Prediction, Dyadic Regression, Missing Data, Joint Exchangeability, Beyond Exchangeability, Network Data, Graphon 

# 1 Introduction 

A fundamental problem in network analysis is dyadic regression, where the goal is to predict the value of a response variable Yij using summary statistics computed from the array and covariate information. One important subproblem is link prediction, where Yij is binary. Link prediction methods have been used to predict interactions in protein-protein interaction networks [47], model 1

> arXiv:2606.11136v2 [math.ST] 16 Jun 2026

interactions between different drug treatments [1], and predict associations in criminal networks [8], to name a few applications. Dyadic regression also arises in modeling trade networks between countries in economics [24] and may also be used to model pairwise comparisons data. While many approaches have been developed to generate predictions in dyadic regression problems, uncertainty quantification for such problems has proven to be challenging for several reasons. Similarly to other regression prob-lems involving network data, array elements typically exhibit a complex depen-dence structure. In addition, with dyadic regression, missing data is often the norm rather than the exception, and the missingness mechanism itself is usually highly complex. For example, in link prediction problems, missingness may de-pend on the underlying probability of edge formation [55]. In these cases, even the choice of test point may depend on whether the observation was missing or not, which leads to additional complications for uncertainty quantification. For network-assisted regression problems, Lunde et al. [39] showed that con-formal prediction remains finite-sample valid under a joint exchangeability as-sumption and mild conditions on network summary statistics. Joint exchange-ability is a natural invariance assumption for network data, which, at a high level, asserts that the distribution is invariant under relabeling of the nodes. This assumption is also natural in the dyadic regression setting. In this paper, we study the properties of conformal prediction for dyadic regression under a joint exchangeability assumption on an appropriate array, where the array also includes the matrix that encodes missingness. When the non-conformity scores themselves have an array structure, there are several possibilities when it comes to which scores to use for calibration. For instance, one may consider using all of the observed upper triangular elements for symmetric arrays. On the other hand, the Aldous-Hoover theorem [2, 29] suggests that elements in the same row or column share a latent position and, therefore, these elements may be more similar to each other compared to arbitrary array elements. In the jointly exchangeable array setting, we pro-pose three ways of constructing conformal prediction sets that, in some form or another, make use of all of the sampled elements and establish finite-sample validity for all methods. Along the way, we develop general theoretical machin-ery for conformal prediction under distributional invariance conditions weaker than exchangeability. The key challenge is that existing proof strategies fail when observations can leave the sample under group transformations; we address this via a novel bijection argument that constructs an explicit measure-preserving correspondence between events. While prediction sets for sampled elements of the array that are valid for any sampling rate are highly valuable, in certain applications, one wants to predict array elements that are known to be missing based on observed data. If the miss-ingness mechanism is not known, one cannot expect to construct finite-sample valid prediction intervals in this setting. # 2 Related Work 

## 2.1 Conformal Prediction and Predictive Inference 

Conformal prediction, pioneered by Vovk and colleagues in the late 1990s, has become a widely used tool for uncertainty quantification in statistics and ma-chine learning; see, for example, Vovk et al. [54] and Angelopoulos and Bates [3] for overviews of the topic. Conformal prediction has recently been consid-ered in various problems involving network information. Huang et al. [30] and Zargarbashi et al. [57] consider node prediction problems, where the test set is randomly selected from a potential pool of calibration and test points. Since they do not assume exchangeability, their guarantees differ from the usual finite-sample marginal coverage guarantees available under exchangeability. More specifically, their results do not guarantee coverage for a fixed pre-specified test point; these arguments do not directly extend to situations where test points are chosen adaptively, which we consider in Section 6. Lunde et al. [39] con-sider a form of exchangeability for such problems, which is satisfied for a wide 3range of data-generating mechanisms, and establish guarantees that are anal-ogous to those for iid data. Lunde [38] further extend these results to various settings involving non-uniform network sampling, including snowball sampling and random walk sampling. Some work has also appeared related to conformalized link prediction and matrix prediction. In Section 6, we consider a weighted conformal prediction procedure that is similar to the one studied by Gui et al. [26]. These au-thors consider matrix completion in a setting where the underlying matrix is deterministic and entries of the matrix are revealed via independent Bernoulli sampling. Since no form of exchangeability is imposed on the underlying ma-trix, the authors weight the non-conformity score for the test point using a worst-case probability over all entries, which can be conservative in settings where additional structure is available. Liang et al. [37] further develop this approach, establishing simultaneous group-level guarantees under the assump-tion of a fixed underlying matrix with known missingness probabilities. Their weighted conformal procedure achieves finite-sample validity but requires exact knowledge of the missingness structure, and because the underlying data are treated as fixed, the analysis does not use the symmetry structure available under joint exchangeability. In contrast, our framework handles unknown miss-ingness mechanisms through the joint exchangeability structure and establishes validity for each individual test point, which is crucial for the adaptively chosen test points we consider in Section 6. Shao and Zhang [50] consider conformal prediction with a jointly exchange-able matrix A and a masking matrix M . Although they claim that the masking matrix M is arbitrary, they also assume that the test index, which is assumed to be missing, does not depend on A. We consider situations where M can depend on A, as well as cases where the test point is chosen adaptively. Furthermore, the authors impute values for all missing entries and include the corresponding non-conformity scores in the calibration set, leading to more noise in the cali-bration step. Du et al. [20] consider conformal link prediction under graphon models with the goal of FDR control for multiple missing links. Their theoreti-cal guarantees again rely on a uniform missing rate assumption, which excludes heterogeneous missingness settings of the kind we consider. Moreover, their work does not study the conditions under which super-uniformity holds under random sampling mechanisms and joint exchangeability, which is the basis for the finite-sample guarantees we establish. Marandon [41] consider conformalized link prediction under a missingness mechanism that is one special case of the jointly exchangeable structure we study when the response is exchangeable. It should be noted that the exchangeability required in their paper is present only when the data are fixed and the data points are sampled at random; when the underlying graph is jointly exchange-able, the scores are jointly exchangeable, but not exchangeable. Luo et al. [40] consider the detection of anomalous edges under the assumption of edge exchangeability. Edge exchangeability can be a natural assumption in some problems, but joint exchangeability is often a more natural notion of symmetry for array prediction tasks that subsumes many commonly used models; see Ex-4ample 3 for further discussion. Zaffran et al. [56] consider conformal prediction sets with missing covariates. Although this problem setting differs from ours, it is worth mentioning that they discuss a notion of mask-conditional validity, which requires conformal prediction sets to be valid given M . The authors es-tablish finite-sample validity in cases where the response does not depend on M .In contrast, we study cases where the response can depend on M , but we do not condition on M ; in general, this is too much to ask for with jointly exchangeable arrays, which have less structure than exchangeable data. Our work is also related to recent work on predictive inference under sym-metry assumptions. In particular, our results imply that one can directly use conformal prediction in various settings with group structure that were stud-ied in Dobriban and Yu [19]. These authors further develop randomization tests to construct prediction sets in settings with group structure. As noted by Ritzwoller et al. [48], when group invariance holds, randomization tests offer finite-sample validity. Randomization tests involve comparing a statistic com-puted on the observed data to statistics computed on data transformed by a group action; such tests do not directly use knowledge about the distribution of rank statistics. In fact, distributional invariance over a group does not by itself imply super-uniformity. When super-uniformity is known, one can avoid recomputing the test statistic over different group actions. Finally, it should be noted that their procedure does not appear to be able to handle the ran-dom sampling mechanisms and evaluation functions that we consider and, in the case of array data, requires enumeration of automorphism groups to con-struct a prediction region. Shen and Yu [51] extend the SymmPI framework to near-conditional coverage under group symmetries using a multi-accuracy for-mulation; their framework does not address the random sampling mechanisms, network covariate structure, or missing-element setting we consider. Paul and Kuchibhotla [46] develop probability inequalities for order statis-tics under violations of the iid assumption. While their framework handles approximate violations without structural assumptions, our results exploit the joint exchangeability structure to obtain exact finite-sample guarantees in the array prediction setting. Barber and Tibshirani [7] develop a unified frame-work for conformal prediction methods based on partial information revelation, recovering and extending several existing results. Their framework provides a conceptual unification of methods including weighted and nonexchangeable conformal prediction; however, jointly exchangeable arrays and random sam-pling mechanisms fall outside the scope of their analysis. Huang [31] proposes a bootstrap framework for confidence interval construction from approximately invariant transformations, recovering conformal prediction under exact cyclic group invariance as a special case; their approximate invariance results rely on Gaussian universality conditions that do not apply to conformal prediction, and their framework does not address the random sampling mechanisms or missing element setting we consider. 52.2 Dyadic Regression and Link Prediction 

Dyadic regression problems frequently arise in the social sciences, where these models have been used to model trade flows [53], international relations [45], and cooperation in hunter-gatherer groups [4], among other applications; see Graham [24] for an overview of the topic. In recent years, some authors have investigated the theoretical properties of kernel regression for nonparametric dyadic regression problems [25], and others have extended classical regression inference to this setting [42]. One special case of dyadic regression is link prediction, which has received attention in a variety of disciplines; see, for example, Mart´ ınez et al. [43] for an overview of the area. Many classical approaches to link prediction involve constructing measures of node similarity and ranking the likelihood of an edge based on the similarity of the two nodes involved. Many existing methods do not directly take into account the fact that observed entries may not be repre-sentative of the underlying population. Some authors in the matrix completion and prediction literature have considered estimation in the presence of this non-representative sampling. Bhattacharya and Chatterjee [9] establish consistency in mean squared error when the underlying matrix is low rank. These authors assume that the entries of the missingness matrix are independent, whereas in Section 6 we assume conditional independence given latent positions. Gaucher and Klopp [22] derive oracle inequalities for maximum-likelihood-based graphon estimation in the presence of non-uniform sampling, where the upper bound in-volves a notion of distance between the true underlying probabilities and the closest approximation given the missingness structure. In the special case of a stochastic block model, they propose a computationally efficient procedure based on a variational approximation.

## 4.3 Conformal Prediction Sets that Exploit Similarities Within Rows/Columns 

While directly using all sampled non-conformity scores to compute prediction set is natural in many ways, in some sense, the proposal in the previous section does not take full advantage of the structure present in exchangeable arrays. ## 4.4 Selective conformal prediction conditional on ranks of network summary statistics 

In many link prediction problems, certain columns or rows in the matrix may be more likely to contain missing links. By focusing on such rows or columns, it is possible to substantially increase the rate at which such missing links are detected. On a related note, since elements in a given column or row may be more similar to each other, prediction sets focusing on a row or column may be narrower than those that involve the entire array. Several comments are in order. Although our approach is asymptotic in na-ture, it is very robust to the choice of selection rule. In fact, the selection rule does not need to be known or estimated, which differs from existing methods such as Bao et al. [5]. Therefore, our approach has fewer “researcher degrees of freedom,” which is an important practical consideration in selective inference 24 problems. Our approach also makes full use of the calibration set and does not discard points that are not chosen by the selection rule, leading to potentially shorter widths. However, it should be noted that our approach does not guar-antee validity for certain selection rules such as the sample maximum for ˆY , for which the approach of Bao et al. [5] also fails. Our proposition provides an average coverage guarantee over “interesting” points, which is desirable but differs from false discovery rate (FDR) control. FDR control for p-values from conformal prediction was previously studied by Jin and Candes [32]. ## 7.2 Asymptotic Validity for Missing Elements 

In this section, we consider the setting where the test point is a missing entry and evaluate the graphon-weighted conformal prediction procedure proposed in Section 6. Our empirical focus is primarily dedicated to evaluating the finite-sample performance of the proposed graphon-weighted conformal procedures under network dependence. We deliberately isolate these marginal properties rather than heuristically choosing the multi-dimensional tuning parameters re-quired for the conditional validity framework, where one must simultaneously optimize over parameters for the conditional regression estimators ˆY and the IPW-weighted conditional CDFs. We leave optimal data-driven tuning param-eter selection for this conditional regime to future work. We consider two data-generating processes that differ in the dimension of the latent covariates used for the response and the masking matrix. For the first model, the response is given by 

Yij = sigmoid( Xi1Xj1 + Xi2Xj2) + c1ξiξj + c2ϵij ,

where c1 and c2 are as defined in Section 7.1.1. The masking matrix is generated as 

Mij ∼ Bernoulli( ρnω(ξi, ξ j )) ,

where ρn controls the sparsity level. The graphon model uses one-dimensional latent positions ξi with ω(ξi, ξ j ) = 1 − | ξi − ξj |, which captures similarity be-tween latent positions. The second is the two-dimensional inner product model described in Section 7.1.1. For both models, the response Yij and the masking matrix Mij share a complex dependent structure through ξ, and the conditional distributions of Yij | Mij = 0 and Yij | Mij = 1 differ. 28 As discussed in Section 6, constructing the weighted conformal prediction interval of the missing elements requires estimating of the probability matrix P .We consider two estimation methods to solve this task. The first is the Universal Singular Value Thresholding (USVT) method of Chatterjee [11], which estimates ˆP USVT by retaining singular values of the masking matrix above a pre-defined universal threshold. While the original method assumes independent entries, the authors note that it extends to dependent entries with minor adjustments to the threshold. In our experiments, we set the threshold to be 3 .03 √n, which is universal across all settings. The second is the Neighborhood Smoothing (NS) method of Zhang et al. [58], which estimates each entry of P by averaging neighboring entries of the masking matrix under a smoothness assumption on the graphon that is naturally satisfied in our setup.

Our simulation results for n = 500, α = 0 .1, and 300 iterations are presented in Figure 1, where coverage and width are plotted as functions of the sparsity level ρn, ranging from 2 n−0.8 to min {2n−0.1, 1}. For ρn ≥ 2n−0.3, coverage is close to the nominal level across all methods; however, for sparser graphs, coverage deviates from the nominal level. Comparing the estimation meth-ods, the USVT method tends to stabilize around nominal coverage at smaller sparsity levels than the NS method, possibly because the USVT method pro-vides more accurate estimates of the probability matrices for sparser graphs. 29 Even for sparse graphs beyond our regularity conditions, USVT still appears to provide valid albeit conservative coverage. It is possible that USVT-based weighted conformal prediction exhibits mild upward bias in sparse regimes since the singular value thresholding tends to underestimate connection probabilities when the signal-noise ratio is low. A precise theoretical understanding of how the bias properties of graphon estimators interact with the validity of weighted conformal procedures is an interesting direction for future work. On the other hand, for moderate to dense graphs ( ρn ≥ n−0.7), the NS method consistently produces narrower prediction intervals than the USVT method across both graphon models, and further analysis is provided in Ap-pendix A.5.3. Finally, the random forest model produces narrower conformal prediction intervals than the linear model as in the previous subsection, and both graphon models exhibit similar patterns across all settings. 

## 7.3 Example: Predicting Common Citations 

In this section, we illustrate our split conformal prediction procedure on the Cora dataset (McCallum [44]), which consists of 2708 machine learning publi-cations and 10556 citations between papers. Each paper is classified into one of 7 categories and is associated with a 1433-dimensional binary feature vector, indicating the presence or absence of specific words. We consider the task of pre-dicting similarity between papers, which is of practical interest in applications such as paper recommendation systems. An intuitive measure of similarity is the number of common citations between two papers, with a larger count suggesting a stronger connection. In practice, data collected from real networks is often curated in complex and unknown ways. Certain pairs may be more likely to be observed based on structural or covariate information that is difficult to fully characterize. We represent this curation mechanism using a graph neural network trained on the observed citation structure and node features, which produces a masking matrix 

M reflecting pairs that are predicted to share common citations. Since the cura-tion mechanism is not known, standard approaches to uncertainty quantification that require explicit knowledge of the missingness mechanism are not directly applicable. Our framework is well-suited to this setting since finite-sample va-lidity holds under joint exchangeability regardless of the specific form of the curation mechanism, provided the jointly exchangeable assumption is satisfied. Let A denote the (weighted) adjacency matrix representing the true citations between papers. The response matrix is given by 

Y = AA T ,

so that Yij counts the number of papers cited by both paper i and paper j. Since the citation network is unknown and sparse, we construct a masking matrix M

to focus on pairs of papers that are more likely to share common citations. To this end, we estimate a probability matrix ˆP from A and the node features using 30 a graph attention network, then threshold at 0 .5 to obtain 

Mij = 1(ˆ pij > 0.5) .

The resulting masking matrix has a density of 0 .12. Details of the graph atten-tion network architecture [10] and training procedure are provided in Appendix A.5.4. We consider three splitting methods: node splitting, edge splitting and selected column splitting. For node splitting, nodes are partitioned with a 0.6/0.3/0.1 split, and the observed edges within each subgraph serve as the training, calibration, and test sets. For edge splitting, the observed edges are randomly partitioned with the same 0 .6/0.3/0.1 ratio. For the selected column method, the column with the highest degree is selected, and its entries are split into calibration and test sets with a 0 .9/0.1 ratio, with all remaining entries used for training. We fit a random forest model on the training set to predict Yij for observed pairs ( Mij = 1) using node-level features constructed from Xi and Xj , where X

is the word list of each node, and the class labels of each node. These features include similarity measures (Jaccard similarity, Cosine similarity, Hadamard product sum) of Xi and Xj , word counts (number of words in Xi and Xj , num-ber of common words and number of distinct words between Xi and Xj ), and class information (the categories of node i and j, and whether the two nodes share the same category). Conformal prediction intervals are constructed using the absolute residual nonconformity score, and coverage is computed by averag-ing over all test points. We set α = 0 .05 and present results over 100 iterations in Table 3. The coverage on the test set is 0 .9500 for node splitting and edge splitting, and 0 .9488 for selected column method, which is consistent with the nominal level of 0 .95. It should be noted that the masking matrix is constructed from a fitted model rather than a known generative process. Nevertheless, the coverage remains close to the nominal level across all three methods. Addition-ally, the average widths are small for all three methods, suggesting that the prediction intervals are reasonably informative for this task. Among the three splitting methods, the selected column method produces narrower intervals, which may be attributed to both the larger training set for the fitted random forest model, and the similarity between calibration and test entries that share a column, as suggested by the Aldous-Hoover theorem. In this paper, we have developed a framework for conformal prediction in dyadic regression problems under complex missingness mechanisms. Our contributions operate at two levels: a general theoretical framework for conformal prediction beyond exchangeability, and specific methodology for array prediction problems under joint exchangeability. At the theoretical level, we established super-uniformity of conformal pre-diction under distributional invariance conditions weaker than exchangeability. The key result, Theorem 5.1, handles the case where the sample itself is a ran-dom subset of the index set, a setting not covered by existing theory. The proof introduces a novel bijection argument that works directly with the joint distri-bution of scores and inclusion indicators, circumventing the failure of standard rank-identity arguments when observations can leave the sample under group transformations. We believe these results are of substantial independent interest beyond the array prediction setting considered here. Several directions for future work are worth highlighting. First, establish-ing simultaneous coverage guarantees for groups of missing entries under the jointly exchangeable framework would extend the individual coverage results of Section 5 in a practically useful direction. Second, the beyond-exchangeability framework of Section 4 may find applications in other settings where group invariance holds but full exchangeability does not, including time series with cyclic stationarity, spatial data with rotational invariance, and tensor-valued observations. Finally, extending the weighted conformal results of Section 5 to handle unbounded graphons and heavier-tailed missingness mechanisms would broaden the applicability of the framework.
