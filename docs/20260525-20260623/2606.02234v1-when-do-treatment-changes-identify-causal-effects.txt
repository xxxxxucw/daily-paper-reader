# When Do Treatment Changes Identify Causal Effects? 

## Martin Huber 

University of Fribourg, Department of Economics 

## June 2, 2026 

Abstract: This paper clarifies the identifying assumptions underlying causal inference based on treatment changes rather than treatment levels, and their relationship to conventional identification strategies. We characterize two distinct structural models, with non-nested identifying assump-tions, under which treatment-change identification is valid conditional on observed covariates. We demonstrate that the identifying assumptions relying on treatment changes are generally not nested with those of methods relying on treatment levels, such as selection-on-observables strategies that control for past outcomes, treatments, and covariates, or difference-in-differences approaches that difference outcomes rather than treatments over time. We show, however, that under a random-walk restriction on the treatment process, conditioning on treatment changes is equivalent to conditioning on treatment levels given lagged treatment. This and other equivalence results motivate overidentifi-cation tests by jointly considering methods based on treatment levels and changes. Beyond these tests, the non-nesting results carry a structural double robustness implication: an estimator that differences both the outcome and the treatment over time, such as two-way fixed effects regression, remains consistent if either the treatment-change assumption or the parallel-trends assumption holds, without requiring both simultaneously. We characterize the causal models consistent with each method, investigate finite-sample behavior in a simulation study, and present an empirical application to cigarette demand. 

Keywords: treatment effects, treatment changes, conditional independence, overidentification.

# 1 Introduction 

Identifying causal effects in observational data remains a central challenge in empirical research. In the absence of random assignment, applied work frequently relies either on selection-on-observables assumptions, which require treatment assignment (such as a medical treatment dose or participation in a training program) to be independent of potential outcomes conditional on observed covariates, or on difference-in-differences (DiD) designs, which exploit repeated observations over time and assume parallel trends in potential outcomes, possibly conditional on covariates. Both approaches impose identifying assumptions with respect to the treatment level that are non-nested, as discussed in Weber, van der Laan, and Petersen (2015), Chab√©-Ferret (2017), Xu (2023), and Huber and Oe√ü (2024). There exists a third practice that is not uncommon in applied work but rarely made theoretically explicit: assessing the effect of changes in a dynamically evolving treatment rather than of specific treatment levels. Examples include studies of tax reforms that change marginal rates over time, labor market regulations that adjust minimum wages, education policies that shift compulsory schooling ages, and medical dosage protocols that modify treatment intensity. In such settings, researchers often implicitly assume that treatment changes rather than levels are quasi-randomly assigned conditional on observed covariates, without articulating precisely what structural conditions this requires or how it relates to conventional identification strategies. A common example is recoding the treatment as one in the case of a treatment increase and as zero if the treatment remains constant over time. Card and Krueger (1994), for instance, apparently a classical DiD study, define the treatment group as observations experiencing an increase in the minimum wage (in the US state of New Jersey) and the control group as those where the minimum wage remains constant but is nonzero (in Pennsylvania). This paper provides a systematic analysis of identification based on treatment changes, addressing the following issues. We clarify the conditions under which variation in treatment changes (rather than levels) identifies causal effects and what must be ruled out for this identification strategy to be valid. We then discuss scenarios in which these conditions are distinct from those underlying identification based on treatment levels via DiD or selection-on-observables strategies. Finally, we investigate causal models in which treatment-change and level-based strategies are jointly valid and how this joint validity can be exploited to construct overidentification tests or to obtain doubly robust identification, implying consistent estimation when one but not another set of identifying assumptions holds. As a main contribution of this paper, we characterize two structural models under which treatment-change identification is valid, even though the two models are not nested in terms of 1assumptions. Both models require that, conditional on covariates, any unobserved confounder in the treatment equation that also affects the outcome is an additively separable time-invariant fixed effect, so that first-differencing removes the fixed effect from the treatment change. In this case, treatment changes may add identifying power relative to level-based approaches, since unobserved fixed effects that jointly determine treatment levels and outcomes do not contaminate the treatment change. The first model (Model A) additionally rules out dynamic treatment effects of earlier treatments on later outcomes and, in general, on later covariates, conditional on earlier covariates. The second model (Model B) imposes a stronger restriction on the treatment process, requiring that the time-varying unobserved shocks driving treatment changes are serially uncorrelated conditional on covariates, so that the treatment follows a random walk. In return for this stronger assumption on the treatment side, the second model accommodates dynamic treatment effects. We formally show that the identifying assumptions relying on treatment changes are generally not nested with those of methods relying on treatment levels, such as selection-on-observables strategies that control for past outcomes, treatments, and covariates, or DiD approaches that difference outcomes rather than treatments over time while also controlling for past treatments and covariates. We establish, however, that if the treatment follows a random walk as in Model B, conditioning on treatment changes is equivalent to conditioning on treatment levels given the lagged treatment, so that differencing the treatment adds no identifying power beyond what is already achieved by conditioning on levels. Similarly, when both treatment-change and DiD assumptions hold simultaneously (namely when fixed effects are additively separable in both the treatment and outcome equations and there are no time-varying unobserved confounders) the resulting causal parameters coincide. Such equivalence results also permit the construction of Hausman (1978)-type overidentification tests by jointly exploiting methods based on treatment levels and changes, which we discuss in both parametric and nonparametric settings. Beyond the overidentification tests, the non-nesting results carry a double robustness (DR) implication, see Robins, Rotnitzky, and Zhao (1994), for estimators that rely on multiple identification strategies simultaneously. Consider, for instance, DiD and treatment-change-based identification, which aim to difference out fixed effects in the outcome and treatment equations, respectively. An estimator that differences both the outcome and the treatment, as the classical two-way fixed effects (TWFE) estimator does in a linear setting, remains consistent if either identifying assumption holds, that is, if confounding is due to additive fixed effects in the treatment equation or in the outcome equation, without requiring both simultaneously. This result is related to the findings on doubly robust identification in Arkhangelsky and Imbens (2022) and Arkhangelsky, Imbens, Lei, and Luo (2021), who 2consider different structural models and estimation approaches than we do. We investigate the finite-sample behavior of the different identification strategies in a simulation study, which confirms the non-nesting results: each strategy can fail while the others remain valid, and no single set of assumptions dominates the others. As a further result, the simulations also confirm that TWFE satisfies a DR property in the sense that it remains consistent whenever either the treatment-change assumption or the parallel-trends assumption holds, even when the other fails. As an empirical illustration, we estimate the price elasticity of cigarette consumption using the state-level panel of Baltagi and Levin (1986) covering 46 US states over the period 1963-1992. Applying different identification strategies and the associated Hausman overidentification tests, we find that the treatment-change strategy yields a substantially larger price elasticity in absolute value than the DiD, treatment level-based selection-on-observables, and TWFE strategies, which are more similar to each other. Correspondingly, the overidentification tests reject the treatment-change strategy against these alternatives, refuting the joint validity of treatment-change- and treatment-level-based approaches. When estimating the models nonparametrically using the causal forest approach of Wager and Athey (2018) and Athey, Tibshirani, and Wager (2019) in addition to OLS, the estimates broadly confirm this picture, providing evidence against the treatment change-based strategy when taking the DR property of TWFE into account. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the causal framework, effect of interest, and the assumptions for identification based on treatment changes. Section 3 presents two non-nested structural models satisfying these assumptions, with illustrations using causal graphs. Section 4 compares the identifying assumptions based on treatment changes to those underlying DiD and selection-on-observables strategies, including proofs of non-nesting in general and equivalence results for specific models. Section 5 discusses overidentification tests and DR identification results. Section 6 presents a simulation study and Section 7 an empirical application to the price elasticity of cigarette demand. Section 8 concludes.

In such settings, ‚ąáDt plays the role of the shift-share instrument, 

Dt is the endogenous treatment, and ňÜőĽ is the first-stage coefficient. The exogeneity of ‚ąáDt

# 7 Empirical Application: Cigarette Demand 

To illustrate the four identification strategies and the overidentification tests developed in Sections 2‚Äď5, we estimate the price elasticity of cigarette demand using the panel data previously analyzed by Baltagi and Levin (1986) covering 46 US states over the period 1963‚Äď1992. The data set covers 1,380 state-year observations (or 1,334 after first-differencing) and is available in the plm package by Croissant and Millo (2008) for the statistical software 

R. The outcome Yit is log per-capita cigarette sales (packs per year), the treatment Dit is log real cigarette price (nominal price deflated by the CPI), and the covariates Xit consist of log real per-capita disposable income and log population aged 16 and over. Cigarette demand is an interesting testing ground, as real prices vary substantially across states and over time owing to differences in state excise taxes, and the literature offers a well-established benchmark price elasticity of approximately ‚ąí0.3 to ‚ąí0.5, as discussed in Chaloupka and Warner (2000). We implement four empirical strategies, namely CIA-‚ąáD, CIA-‚ąáY , CIA-D, and TWFE. In line with our previous discussion, for any strategy in which Dit enters in levels, Di,t ‚ąí1 is added as an additional control variable, and whenever Yit enters in levels, Yi,t ‚ąí1 is added as an additional control variable. The continuous controls Xit enter at both the current 26 level Xit and the lagged level Xi,t ‚ąí1 as separate regressors. Year fixed effects are included in all four specifications. State fixed effects are never included explicitly, but are eliminated by outcome differencing when relying on CIA-‚ąáY and TWFE, while Yi,t ‚ąí1 is controlled for instead when relying on CIA-‚ąáD and CIA-D. A potential reason for a violation of CIA-‚ąáD might be that states experiencing large year-to-year price increases may differ systematically in ways that past outcomes and covariates do not fully capture, plausibly because tax-driven price changes reflect state-level political and fiscal conditions that can also be associated with cigarette consumption. 

# 8 Conclusion 

This paper has clarified when conditioning on treatment changes rather than levels identifies causal effects, how these conditions compare to those underlying conventional identifica-tion strategies based on treatment levels such as selection-on-observables and difference-in-differences, and under which circumstances the different strategies are jointly valid so that overidentification tests can be constructed. Treatment-change identification is valid under at least two distinct structural models with non-nested identifying assumptions: one that requires the absence of dynamic treatment effects and one that imposes a random-walk restriction on the treatment process. In the latter case, all three identification strategies - based on treatment changes, treatment levels, and outcome differences - coincide and can be exploited jointly in overidentification tests. When the strategies are not jointly valid, the non-nesting results carry a structural double robustness implication: under specific structural assumptions, the TWFE estimator, which differences both the outcome and the treatment, remains consistent if either the treatment-change 29 assumption or the parallel-trends assumption holds, without requiring both simultaneously. These results have practical implications for empirical work. Researchers exploiting treatment changes should be explicit about it and also about which structural conditions they rely on ‚ÄĒ in particular, whether dynamic treatment effects are permitted or treatment shocks are assumed to follow a random walk. The overidentification tests considered here provide a valuable diagnostic for whether data are consistent with more than one strategy in terms of identifying assumptions, and are best combined with falsification tests on pre-treatment periods. When the estimates based on different strategies disagree, the double robustness of TWFE offers a degree of protection against misspecification of either the treatment or outcome equation, even though this does not substitute for careful examination of the underlying structural assumptions. 30 References
