Abstract 

Under the canonical setting of observational studies for causal inference, we show that the average treatment effect under the overlap weight, the weight that is propor-tional to the conditional variance of the treatment given the covariates, is bounded between the average treatment effects on the treated and control, under a monotonic relationship between the propensity score and the conditional average treatment ef-fect. We further extend the result to weighted local average treatment effects, under the canonical setting with a binary instrumental variable and a binary treatment. We also extend the results to other weights. Based on the theory, we recommend the “CP-plot” of the estimated conditional average treatment effect against the estimated propensity score. 

Keywords: Beta weight; Instrumental variable; Observational study; Matching weight; Potential outcome; Propensity score. 

# 1 Introduction 

Causal inference with observational studies is critical in many disciplines. Under the potential outcomes framework, the average treatment effect (ATE) can be identified under two assumptions. The first assumption is unconfoundedness that the treatment is inde-pendent of the potential outcomes given the observed covariates. The second assumption is the overlap assumption that the propensity score, defined as the conditional probability

of the treatment given the observed covariates, is not degenerate (Rosenbaum and Rubin, 1983). While the majority of the literature focuses on the ATE, other weighted average treatment effects are also of scientific and policy relevance. For instance, the average treat-ment effect on the treated (ATT) measures the effect on the treated population, the average treatment effect on the controls (ATC) measures the effect on the control population, and the average treatment effect under the overlap weight (ATO) measures the effect on the population weighted by the conditional variance of the treatment given the observed co-variates (Li et al., 2018). ATO has attracted growing interest because of its robustness to extreme propensity scores (Li et al., 2019) and its connection with various regression-based estimators (Angrist, 1998; Wallace and Moodie, 2015; Ding, 2021; Lee, 2018). Despite its popularity, the relationship between ATO and the more traditional ATT and ATC has not been characterized, in general. We fill this gap with a simple ordering result under a monotonicity assumption and develop several extensions. First, we show that ATO lies between ATT and ATC whenever the conditional expec-tation of the conditional average treatment effect (CATE) given the propensity score is monotonic. This includes a recent result by Humphreys (2025) as a special case, which focuses on fixed-effects regression with a discrete covariate under a more stringent mono-tonicity assumption. Second, we extend the bracketing result to the instrumental variable (IV) setting with a binary IV and a binary treatment (Angrist et al., 1996; Imbens and Angrist, 1994), known as the local average treatment effect (LATE) framework. Most of this literature focuses on the LATE, defined as the ATE among compliers, whose treatment status is changed by the IV. Recently, Choi (2024) introduced a general class of weighted LATEs. In particular, the overlap-weighted LATE has appeared frequently in the recent IV literature (Bugni and 2Gao, 2023, Section 4); see also Borusyak and Hull (2024), Zhao, Ding and Li (2026) and Jiang et al. (2026). We show that the overlap-weighted LATE is bracketed by the LATEs under the treated and control populations whenever the conditional expectation of the CATE among compliers given the propensity score of the IV is monotonic. Third, we extend the results to a beta-weight family (Matsouaka and Zhou, 2024) indexed by two parameters, of which ATE, ATT, ATC, and ATO are special cases. We further obtain ordering results that go beyond beta weights, including matching weights and a general framework based on a one-dimensional ordering index. Fourth, based on this theory, we recommend a practical diagnostic “CP–plot” of the estimated CATE against the estimated propensity score. We revisit several observational studies and an IV study. The rest of the paper proceeds as follows. Section 2 studies propensity-score weighted causal estimands and establishes the bracketing relation between ATO, ATT, and ATC. It then discusses identification under ignorability and connects the overlap-weighted estimand to regression-based estimands. Section 3 studies propensity-score weighted local causal estimands in a binary IV setting and establishes the corresponding bracketing relation for the overlap-weighted local estimand. It then connects the overlap-weighted local estimand to the population two-stage least-squares (TSLS) coefficient. Section 4 provides empirical illustrations. Section 5 develops extensions to beta weights and other weighting schemes. Section 6 concludes with a discussion. 32 Bracketing and Regression Interpretation of the Overlap-Weighted Estimand 

## 2.1 A bracketing result for the overlap-weighted estimand 

We work in the standard potential-outcomes framework. For each unit, Z ∈ { 0, 1}

indicates whether the unit receives treatment ( Z = 1) or control ( Z = 0). Let Y (1) denote the outcome that would be observed under treatment, and Y (0) the outcome that would be observed under control. The vector X collects observed pre-treatment covariates. The conditional average treatment effect (CATE) is defined as 

τ (X) = E{Y (1) − Y (0) | X},

which summarizes the average treatment effect among units with the same covariate profile 

X. Population-level causal estimands are then obtained by averaging τ (X) under different weighting schemes, with different weights corresponding to different target populations. Let e(X) = P(Z = 1 | X) denote the propensity score (Rosenbaum and Rubin, 1983). Common estimands include ATE, ATT, and ATC:

Unlike e(X) or 1 − e(X), the overlap weight e(X){1 − e(X)} vanishes at both endpoints of [0 , 1]. Hence, τATO emphasizes covariate regions where both treatment arms are well represented and downweights regions with extreme propensity scores. This makes ATO a natural and stable target estimand for the overlap population, especially when treatment and control groups have limited overlap. Because ATO targets a different population from ATT and ATC, it is important to understand how these estimands are related. We pursue this comparison under a monotonicity condition on the conditional mean of the CATE given the propensity score. Our theory relies on the following P-CATE function

# 4 Empirical illustrations 

We provide two sets of empirical illustrations. The first examines the relationship between estimated CATE and estimated propensity scores in several observational applica-tions. The second illustrates the analogous local bracketing relation in a conditionally valid IV setting using the 401(k) application of Abadie (2003). These analyses provide empirical evidence on the monotonicity conditions and the resulting bracketing relationships among weighted average effects. 

## 4.1 Observational studies 

To complement our theoretical results in Section 2, we revisit eight empirical set-tings. One uses the Right Heart Catheterization (RHC) data (Conners Jr et al., 1996; Harrell, 2026). For this application, the treatment variable ( swang1 ) indicates whether a patient received right-heart catheterization, and the outcome is in-hospital death ( death ). This paper studies how monotonic relationships between treatment effects and propen-sity scores induce ordering relations among weighted causal estimands. In unconfounded observational studies, we show that the overlap-weighted estimand is bracketed by ATT and ATC. In the IV setting, we establish analogous results for weighted local average treat-ment effects, including the overlap-weighted LATE. We further extend these results to a general beta-weight family and to weighting schemes beyond beta weights. The results suggest a practical diagnostic and reporting strategy. In observational stud-ies, analysts can plot an estimated conditional treatment effect ˆ τ (X) against the estimated propensity score ˆ e(X); in IV studies, the analogous plot uses a modeled conditional Wald estimate ˆ τ c(X) against the estimated IV propensity score ˆ e(X). Such plots should be in-terpreted as descriptive diagnostics rather than formal tests of monotonicity. The most relevant pattern is the trend over the region where the data are well supported, especially the central overlap region, because overlap weights downweight observations with extreme propensity scores. When the smoothed relationship is approximately increasing over the relevant region, the theory predicts τATC ≤ τATO ≤ τATT , and analogously for the local IV estimands. When the relationship is approximately decreasing, the ordering reverses. When the relationship is visibly nonmonotone in the well-supported region, the bracketing relation should be interpreted with caution rather than used as a substantive conclusion. We therefore rec-ommend reporting the effect–propensity score diagnostic together with ATT, ATO, and ATC, or their local IV analogs, so that the sensitivity of causal conclusions to the target population is made explicit. 27 References
