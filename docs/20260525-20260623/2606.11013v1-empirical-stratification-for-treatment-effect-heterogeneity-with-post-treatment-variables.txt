Abstract 

Post-treatment variables (PVs), such as treatment noncompliance, behavioral re-sponses, intercurrent events, often modify the ultimate treatment effect on the pri-mary outcome. However, existing methods provide limited tools for studying treat-ment effect heterogeneity with respect to PVs. Conventional heterogeneous treatment effect estimands condition on baseline covariates. However, similarly conditioning on the observed PV can induce endogenous selection bias for the treatment effect estima-tion. Principal stratification offers a rigorous framework for studying principal causal effects across principal strata, but principal strata are latent and their identification often requires stringent assumptions. This paper develops an assumption-lean empirical stratification framework for characterizing treatment effect heterogeneity with respect to PVs. We define empiri-cal scores using the predicted potential PV responses based on baseline covariates, and use the empirical scores to construct empirically accessible subgroups. The resulting empirical-stratum treatment effects (ETEs) are identifiable under standard causal as-sumptions. We connect the proposed framework to principal stratification by showing that the average ETE recovers principal causal effects under the principal ignorability assumption, but remains informative under violations of this assumption. We further introduce projected ETE curves and develop efficient influence function-based esti-mators for the semiparametric inference. We illustrate the proposed framework with two real-world applications. 

Keywords: heterogeneous treatment effects, empirical stratification, empirical-stratum treat-ment effects, intercurrent events, efficient influence function, semiparametric inference 1

# 1 Introduction 

Heterogeneous treatment effects (HTEs) are central to many scientific and policy ques-tions. Most existing HTE methods focus on variation in treatment effects across baseline covariates (e.g., Wager and Athey, 2018; Semenova and Chernozhukov, 2021; Kennedy, 2023; Chernozhukov et al., 2025). In many studies, investigators are interested in a dif-ferent type of heterogeneity about how treatment effects vary with individuals’ response to post-treatment variables (PVs). Examples of the PV include treatment noncompliance (Angrist et al., 1996), behavioral responses to treatment (Manski, 2013), intercurrent events in health research (Kahan et al., 2024), and secondary outcomes of scientific interest. These PVs reveal how individuals respond after treatment initiation and may therefore explain important variation of treatment effect on the final outcome. Studying HTEs with respect to PVs is challenging because PVs are themselves affected by treatment. Directly conditioning on the observed PV can induce endogenous selection bias (Elwert and Winship, 2014; Montgomery et al., 2018), so conventional HTE methods targeting on baseline covariates are generally invalid. Despite the prevalence of PVs in randomized trials and observational studies, there is no general assumption-lean statistical framework for characterizing treatment effect heterogeneity with respect to PVs. To motivate our methodology, we consider the World Health Organization’s Large Anal-ysis and Review of European Housing and Health Status (WHO-LARES) study (Shenassa et al., 2007), an observational study of the relationship between housing conditions and health outcomes among residents in eight European cities. Let Z, M , Y , and X denote the treatment, PV, outcome, and a set of baseline covariates, respectively. Following the analysis in Cheng and Li (2025), we are interested in studying the effect of residing in damp housing ( Z = 1: yes and Z = 0: no) on depression ( Y = 1: yes and Y = 0: no), but some individuals develop dampness-related diseases after treatment ( M = 1: yes and 

M = 0: no). The motivating question is to understand how the effect of damp housing 2on depression varies with individuals’ dampness-related disease responses. This question cannot be answered by simply comparing individuals with and without dampness-related disease status, because such disease status is affected by treatment. Principal stratification (Frangakis and Rubin, 2002) provides a rigorous causal frame-work for studying treatment effects within subpopulations defined by the joint potential values of a PV, G = [ M (1) , M (0)] ⊤. Such subgroup treatment effect is referred to as the principal causal effect (PCE). In the WHO-LARES study, the PCEs can help address the following question: “ How does the effect of damp housing on depression vary among individ-uals who would or would not develop dampness-related disease under alternative treatment conditions? ” However, principal strata are latent subpopulations, making it difficult to determine stratum membership and translate PCEs into actionable policy insights (Cheng and Li, 2025; Mattei et al., 2013; Pearl, 2009). Moreover, identification of PCEs often requires additional structural assumptions, such as monotonicity, exclusion restrictions, principal ignorability, or parametric modeling assumptions (Angrist et al., 1996; Ding and Lu, 2017; Liu et al., 2024; Vansteelandt and Van Lancker, 2025). These assumptions are useful in some settings but are typically problem-specific and unverifiable. We propose an empirical stratification framework for studying treatment effect hetero-geneity with respect to PVs. Specifically, we define subgroups, referred to as empirical strata, based on the conditional expectation of principal-stratum variable given baseline covariates, E[G | X]. The empirical stratum summarizes individuals’ predicted responses to the PV using pretreatment information. Building on this idea, we introduce a new class of HTE estimands, termed empirical-stratum treatment effects (ETEs), to quantify treatment effects within these empirical strata. In the WHO-LARES study, ETEs can help address the question: “ How does the effect of damp housing on depression vary among in-dividuals who are more or less likely to develop dampness-related disease under alternative treatment conditions? ” Therefore, shifting from the PCE to the ETE changes the inter-3pretation of the causal analysis; nevertheless, both estimands are meaningful with different inferential goals. Specifically, the new ETEs characterize treatment effect heterogeneity with respect to individuals’ predicted potential PV responses based on covariates, whereas PCEs characterize treatment effect heterogeneity with respect to individuals’ realized coun-terfactual potential PV responses. Studying ETEs offers two advantages. First, ETEs are identifiable under the standard causal assumptions commonly used in observational studies (Rosenbaum and Rubin, 1983). Second, because empirical strata are functions of baseline covariates, stratum membership can be determined once covariates have been collected, thereby enhancing policy relevance. Moreover, we show that under the principal stratifi-cation setting where principal scores are used to define empirical strata, the average ETEs can be viewed as a robust approximation to the PCE. In particular, it coincides with the PCE under the additional causal assumption of principal ignorability (Jo and Stuart, 2009; Ding and Lu, 2017; Feller et al., 2017; Forastiere et al., 2018); when principal ignorability is mildly violated, it can still serve as a good approximation to the PCE. This paper further develops a semiparametric inference theory for the efficient estima-tion of ETEs. When baseline covariates are high-dimensional or include some continuous variables, we typically have infinitely many empirical strata, making the estimation of ETEs a challenging task. Following previous projection-based approaches (e.g., Lu et al., 2026; Ye et al., 2023; Kennedy et al., 2019; Robins et al., 2000), our proposed method projects the ETE onto a prespecified working model to obtain a low-dimensional approximation to the underlying true ETE curve. We refer to the resulting estimand as the projected ETE curve (projETE). When the working model is correctly specified, the projETE recovers the true ETE; when the working model is misspecified, it remains a well-defined causal summary given by the best least-squares projection of the true ETE onto the chosen working model. We develop a semiparametric estimator of the projETE that combines efficient influence functions (EIFs) with flexible, data-adaptive nuisance estimation (Chernozhukov et al., 4X Z

# 2 The empirical stratification framework 

## 2.1 Notations and setup 

Consider that we observe n independent and identically distributed data vectors Oi =

{Xi, Z i, M i, Y i}, where Xi ∈ X ⊆ Rp×1 is a set of baseline covariates, Zi ∈ { 0, 1} is a binary treatment (1 for treated; 0 for control), Mi ∈ R is a PV, and Yi ∈ R is an outcome of interest. The n copies of data may be drawn from a randomized trial or an observational study. We also allow the PV to be either continuously or discretely distributed. Figure 1 show the causal relationship among these variables, where we do not place any restriction on the causal structure between the outcome and PV. Under the counterfactual outcomes framework, we use Yi(z) to denote the counterfactual value of outcome when setting Zi =5z ∈ { 0, 1}, and similarly write Mi(z) as the counterfactual value of PV when setting 

Zi = z. Throughout, we require the Stable Unit Treatment Value Assumption (Rubin, 1986) to connect counterfactual and observed values by Yi = Yi(1) Zi + Yi(0) {1 − Zi} and 

Mi = Mi(1) Zi + Mi(0) {1 − Zi}. In addition, we assume the following two structural assumptions to identify the proposed estimands: 

Assumption 1. (Treatment ignorability) {Y (1) , Y (0) , M (1) , M (0) } ⊥ ⊥ Z | X.

Assumption 2. (Positivity) c < P(Z = 1 |X) < 1 − c for some 0 < c < 0.5.

Both assumptions are usually satisfied in randomized trials. They are the standard set assumptions for causal inference in observational studies (Rosenbaum and Rubin, 1983). In observational studies, Assumption 1 requires that, conditional on baseline covariates, treatment assignment behaves “as if randomized,” and Assumption 2 requires that every individual has a positive probability of receiving each treatment condition. The average treatment effect, ATE = E[Y (1) − Y (0)] , is commonly employed to measure the overall treatment effect for the entire study population, but it cannot characterize treatment effect heterogeneity. We next introduce the empirical stratification framework to characterize the treatment effect heterogeneity with respect to PVs. 

## 2.2 Defining PV-induced subgroups via empirical scores 

The principal-stratum variable G = [ M (1) , M (0)] ⊤ describes how an individual’s PV would respond under treatment and control, and therefore provides a natural basis for defining PV-induced subgroups. However, G is generally latent because only one of M (1) and M (0) can be observed for each individual. We therefore use a covariate-based prediction of G

to construct empirically accessible subgroups. Specifically, for z ∈ { 0, 1}, let sz (X) = 

E[M (z) | X] be the expected PV response under treatment level z based on covariates 

X. Then, the vector E[G | X] = [ s1(X), s 0(X)] ⊤ ∈ R2 summarizes the predicted PV response profile under both treatment conditions. In many applications, investigators may 6be interested in a particular feature of this predicted response profile rather than the full two-dimensional vector. To this end, we define the empirical score as 

τ (X) := r{s1(X), s 0(X)} ∈ R,

where r(x, y ) : R×R → R is a user-specified transformation function that maps {s1(X), s 0(X)}

to a scalar value τ (X). The empirical score τ (X) is then used to define empirical strata, namely subgroups formed by values or ranges of τ (X). Note that τ (X) is easily identified based on observed data. Specifically, under Assumptions 1–2, we have sz (X) = E[M (z) |

X] = E[M | Z = z, X], so that τ (X) is identified by 

τ (X) = r{h1(X), h 0(X)}1,

where hz (X) := E[M | Z = z, X]. By construction, the choice of the transformation function r(x, y ) determines the interpretation of the empirical score. We highlight three example choices and clarify their interpretation in the WHO-LARES study. Web Ap-pendix 1.1 provides three additional data examples illustrating how empirical scores can be interpreted in the applications when the PV denotes a treatment noncompliance status, a behavioral response to treatment, and a secondary outcome.

More general definitions of empirical score are also possible. For example, when M is continuously distributed, one may define empirical scores using features of the conditional counterfactual distributions P[M (1) ≤ m1 | X] and P[M (0) ≤ m0 |

In settings where the principal score, eg(X) := P(G = g | X), is identifiable, one may also set τ (X) = eg(X), where g ∈ G is a fixed principal stratum. Then, the empirical stratum {τ (X) = t} corresponds to individuals with a t×100% predicted probability of belonging to principal stratum g. In this case, the corresponding ETE has a connection to the principal causal effects; see Section 3.2 for further discussions.

(1) By definition, the ETE characterizes how the treatment effect on the primary outcome varies across individuals with different predicted PV responses, as summarized by τ (X). ## 3.1 Connection to prediction-based stratification methods 

The empirical stratification framework is related to several prediction-based stratification methods that conduct subgroup analysis using covariate-based predictions of the PV. One closely related approach is the predicted-risk method of VanderWeele et al. (2012), which studies how the effect of maternal smoking ( Z) on infant mortality ( Y ) varies by the occurrence of low birth weight ( M = 1: yes; M = 0: no). Outside of the causal inference framework, Approach 1 of VanderWeele et al. (2012) conducts a regression analysis of Y on 

{Z, X} with subgroup infants stratified by the predicted risk of low birth weight, risk( X) = 

P(M = 1 | X). Under Assumptions 1–2, this predicted-risk score can be rewritten as risk( X) = s1(X)π1(X) + s0(X)π0(X), where sz (X) = E[M (z) | X] and πz (X) = P(Z =

z | X). Thus, risk( X) is a covariate-based summary of predicted PV responses, averaged over the treatment-assignment mechanism. If we choose τ (X) = risk( X), then their analysis relates to studying ETE( t) = E{Y (1) − Y (0) | risk( X) = t}, that is the effect of maternal smoking on infant mortality among infants with a t × 100% predicted risk of low birth weight. We extend this method to a formal causal inference framework. The empirical stratification framework also nests compliance-score subgroup analyses as an important special case. In randomized trials with noncompliance, the treatment receipt status ( M ∈ { 0, 1}) may differ from the treatment assignment status ( Z ∈ { 0, 1}). Prior work studies subgroup intention-to-treat effects by stratifying individuals according to the compliance score, P(G = [1 , 0] ⊤ | X), which is the principal score measuring the probability of belonging to the complier stratum given baseline covariates (Follmann, 2000; Joffe et al., 2003; Hu et al., 2022). This estimand is sometimes referred to as the compliance-score ad-justed causal effect (Hu et al., 2022). Therefore, their analysis corresponds to studying ETE by choosing τ (X) as the compliance score. Our framework generalizes compliance-score 10 subgroup analysis to broader classes of PVs and, in addition, develops semiparametrically efficient inference procedures for the resulting ETEs. Relatedly, Kennedy et al. (2020) also use compliance scores to define identifiable subpopulations, but target the effect of treat-ment receipt on the final outcome rather than intention-to-treat effects, thereby addressing a distinct causal question. Finally, our framework is related to endogenous stratification (Abadie et al., 2018), which studies subgroup treatment effects by stratifying individuals according to the level of E[Y (0) | X]. Thus, the stratification criterion is based on a covariate-based prediction of the untreated potential outcome. Similarly, our empirical stratification approach forms subgroups based on the covariate-based prediction of the potential PVs, E[G | X], thereby enabling the study of treatment effect heterogeneity with respect to the PVs. 

## 3.2 Connection to principal stratification 

The principal stratification framework focuses on the PCE within a target principal stratum 

g = [ m1, m 0]⊤ in the support of G = [ M (1) , M (0)] ⊤. Specifically, the PCE is defined by PCE g = E[Y (1) − Y (0) | G = g].

In this section, we establish a general relationship between the PCE and ETE in the special case when we set the empirical score τ (X) in (1) as the principal score eg(X) := P(G =

g|X). Therefore, the ETE we consider here is ETE g(t) = E[Y (1) − Y (0) | eg(X) = t]. (2) In this case, we can interpret ETE g(t) as the treatment effect among subgroup individuals with a t×100% predicted probability of membership within principal stratum g. Generally, the principal score cannot be identified based on Assumptions 1–2 only. In the binary PV setting with M ∈ { 0, 1}, if the monotonicity assumption ( Mi(1) ≥ Mi(0) for all individuals) is plausible, then eg(X) is identified by eg(X) = h0(X), h1(X)−h0(X), and 1 −h1(X) for 11 g ∈ { [1 , 1] ⊤, [1 , 0] ⊤, [0 , 0] ⊤}, respectively, while the stratum g = [0 , 1] ⊤ does not exist (An-grist et al., 1996). If M is continuous, identification of eg(X) generally requires additional structural assumptions, such as a copula model, and can involve sensitivity parameters; see Zhang and Yang (2025) and Lu et al. (2026) for details. Our discussion in this section will focus on the scenario where the principal score is identifiable. Note that ETE g(t) and PCE g are not directly comparable because they target on dif-ferent subpopulations, where the former targets a empirical stratum but the latter targets a principal stratum. To unify the subpopulation, we compare the PCE to an average ETE over individuals in the principal stratum. Specifically, given an individual with covariates 

X from the target principal stratum, let ETE g{eg(X)} be the individualized ETE when the principal score is set to the value under observed individual covariates. Then, we define the average ETE among individuals in principal stratum g as AvgETE g = E [ETE g{eg(X)} | G = g] .

# 6 Two applications 

## 6.1 The WHO-LARES study with an incurrent event 

We apply the proposed methodology to the WHO-LARES study ( n = 5 ,882) to study how the effect of living in damp housing on depression varies with individuals’ vulnerability to dampness-related disease. We define the empirical score as τ (X) = s1(X) = P{M (1) = 1 |

X}, so that individuals with larger values of τ (X) are those predicted to be more vulner-able to dampness-related disease if exposed to damp housing. We focus on the projected ETE curve under the linear working model so that projETE( t) = βdiff ,0 + βdiff ,1t. The nuisance parameters {πz (X), h z (X), μ z (X)} are estimated using either parametric mod-25 els or machine learning methods, leading to two EIF-based estimators, \projETE eff −par 

(t)and \projETE eff −ml 

(t). The nuisance-model specifications follow those used in Section 5. To make the treatment ignorability assumption more plausible, we consider the following base-line covariates: sex, age, marital status, education, employment, smoking, home ownership, home size, crowding (number of residents per room), heating, and natural light. Before presenting the empirical treatment effect analysis, we first examine how the es-timated empirical score relates to baseline covariates. Web Table S3 in the Supplementary Material summarizes baseline characteristics for individuals below and above the median of the estimated empirical score, where bτ (X) is obtained using the machine learning nuisance estimator. This descriptive comparison characterizes which covariates influence individu-als’ vulnerability to dampness-related disease. As shown in Web Table S3, a particularly notable pattern is the sharp difference in gender composition across the two groups: the lower-score group is composed mostly of men, whereas the higher-score group is composed predominantly of women. In addition, individuals in the higher-score group tend to be older, less likely to be married or employed, and more likely to smoke. They are also less likely to own their homes and more likely to live in crowded dwellings. ## 6.2 The National Job Corps study with noncompliance 

The National Job Corps Study is a randomized trial of the Job Corps training program for disadvantaged youths in the United States (Schochet, 2001). In the study, participants were randomly assigned either to have immediate access to Job Corps ( Z = 1) or to a control condition in which access to Job Corps was restricted for three years later ( Z = 0). We study the effect of assignment to Job Corps on participants’ weekly earnings during the fourth year after randomization ( Y ). Treatment noncompliance is substantial in the study (Frumento et al., 2012; Chen and Flores, 2015). We define M = 1 if an individual participated in Job Corps within one year after randomization and M = 0 otherwise. Among the 9,240 participants, 15% of individuals assigned to the treatment group did not participate in Job Corps, while about 51% of individuals assigned to the control group 28 participated in the program. We define the empirical score as τ (X) = s1(X) − s0(X), so that τ (X) summarizes the extent to which an individual’s treatment receipt is affected by treatment assignment. In the National Job Corps Study, the monotonicity assumption is plausible, because the Job Corps program is generally considered beneficial and defiers are unlikely to exist (Chen and Flores, 2015). As discussed in Section 3, τ (X) is the compliance score under monotonicity. Therefore, we interpret ETE( t) as the compliance-score adjusted causal effect, measuring the treatment effect among individuals with a t × 100% predicted probability of belonging to the complier stratum. Although the National Job Corps Study is a randomized trial, we consider a rich set of baseline covariates when estimating the nuisance parameters (see Web Table S4 in the Supplementary Material for the list of covariates). The covariates are used to improve the prediction of treatment receipt behavior in order to construct a more informative compli-ance score. To describe how compliance scores relate to baseline covariates, Web Table S4 in the Supplementary Material summarizes baseline characteristics across two subgroups with individuals below and above the median of the compliance score distribution. Individ-uals in the higher-score group appear to have a better socioeconomic status, who are older, more educated, and more likely to have a high school degree or GED degree. They also have higher baseline earnings and are more likely to report smoking or drinking experience. This paper proposes an empirical stratification framework for studying treatment effect heterogeneity with respect to PVs. The framework can be viewed as a complement to the principal stratification analysis. Specifically, principal stratification is most natural when the scientific goal is to understand treatment effects within subpopulations defined by the joint potential values of the PV, G. The questions are intrinsically about counter-factual response types and are therefore well aligned with principal stratification analysis, although identification of PCEs often requires additional assumptions. In contrast, em-pirical stratification is designed for settings in which the goal is to characterize treatment effect heterogeneity across identifiable subgroups constructed from baseline covariates. By stratifying individuals according to predicted PV responses, the proposed framework tar-gets subgroups that can, in principle, be described before treatment assignment and may therefore be more directly useful for policy and practice. Several limitations and extensions remain. First, as in most causal inference methods, identification of the ETE relies on treatment ignorability assumption, which may not hold in observational studies. In applications such as the WHO-LARES study, where treatment is not randomized, then the analysis may be biased due to unmeasured confounding. Sen-sitivity analyses for violations of treatment ignorability are a useful extension. Second, the projected ETE curve summarizes treatment effect heterogeneity through a prespec-ified working model. While this improves stability and interpretability, it may obscure more complex nonlinear patterns in the underlying ETE curve. If τ (X) is continuously distributed, one possible extension is to develop kernel smoothing estimators that locally average the ETE curve over neighborhoods of the empirical score (Zhang and Yang, 2025). This would provide a more flexible, nonparametric description of how treatment effects vary with predicted PV responses. Third, many applications involve multiple PVs. For instance, in pragmatic trials, investigators may observe both treatment noncompliance and 31 intercurrent events (e.g., the ADAPTABLE trial (Jones et al., 2021)). Extending empiri-cal stratification to this setting would require defining empirical strata based on the joint predicted response profile of multiple PVs, such as E[M (1) | X] and E[M (0) | X] for a vector of PVs M . Such an extension would allow investigators to characterize treatment effect heterogeneity driven jointly by multiple PVs.
