## Abstract 

Causal discovery methods commonly assume that all data is independently and identically distributed (i.i.d.) and that there are no unmeasured variables affecting the system. In practice, these assumptions are often violated, leading to inac-curate inference. In this paper, we study how to identify hidden confounding and selection biases from causal mechanism shifts. In particular, we show that structural biases lead to dependent mechanism shifts. That is, by considering for which variables the mechanisms change given data from different environments, we can tell which variables are unbiased, which are subject to hidden confounding, and which are undergoing selection bias. We formalize this into an empirically testable criterion based on mutual information, and show under which conditions it identifies structural biases. To tell which nodes are subject to what kind of bias, we introduce the S TRU BI algorithm. Experiments on synthetic and real-world data show that S TRU BI works well in practice, accurately recovering affected variable sets and types of biases, outperforming the state-of-the-art by a wide margin. Our implementation is available at https://github.com/niftynans/strubi .

## 1 Introduction 

Methods for causal inference generally require causal sufficiency and i.i.d.-ness [ 21 , 31 ]. That is, to provably recover the causal ground truth, they require that all causally relevant variables are measured (sufficiency) and that the samples in the data are all independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). While convenient in theory, neither of these are likely to hold in practice [ 13 , 14 ]. Often, we do not know what the causally relevant variables are, and even if we do, we cannot always measure them. Often, we also do not know whether a system has a representative sample, or whether it is subject to selection bias. In either case, this can lead to causal models that can be arbitrarily wrong because the data will show spurious dependencies [ 5 , 14 , 32 ]. For example, if ‘shark attacks’ and ‘ice cream sales’ are statistically dependent, it may lead us to believe there is a causal dependency, even if they are actually conditionally independent given their unmeasured confounder ‘season’. Similarly, if we only study the survival rates of fighter jets that returned from combat, then we get a distorted view of where more protective armor is most needed. This raises the question, can we detect whether the data at hand is unbiased, subject to hidden confounding, or subject to selection bias? In this paper, we show that by considering non-i.i.d. data collected from different contexts, the effects of confounding and selection bias are identifiable by considering distribution shifts of the observed variables. In particular, we show that actual, intrinsic , mechanism shifts leave measurably different observed mechanism shifts between the three cases. We give an example in Fig. 1. Here,

We formally show under which conditions we can leverage this observation to identify and classify between the three cases. To do so, we assume that mechanisms shifts are sparse and independent. Importantly, unlike existing work [ 5, 6], our approach is non-parametric, making it generally applica-ble. To put our theory to practice, we propose the S TRU BI algorithm for structural bias identification from empirical data. S TRU BI operates on a given causal graph G and employs the KCI test by [ 37 ] to determine if mechanisms change between contexts. We use adjusted mutual information to determine whether the mechanism shifts of different variables behave independently or not, and to classify between confounding or selection, consider many of the upstream variables change together. We empirically evaluate S TRU BI on a wide range of synthetic data, comparing it to five state of the art methods. The experiments show that S TRU BI outperforms its competitors by a wide margin, and that using an inferred causal graph gives comparable results to using the ground truth causal network. We verify that S TRU BI is applicable on real-world data through a case study on cell-signaling data, where it correctly classifies between confounding and selection bias, and unlike its competitors, retrieves high-quality sets of affected variables in both settings. All code is available in the supplementary. We postpone all proofs to the appendix. 

## 2 Related Work 

Inferring causality from observational data is a fundamental challenge in statistics [21, 26, 31]. In a nutshell, it is only possible to do so under assumptions of the data generating model [ 21 ]. Standard assumptions include the causal Markov condition, causal faithfulness, causal sufficiency, and i.i.d.-ness. Under these assumptions, classical constraint-based [ 31 , 36 ] and score-based [ 3 , 23 ] methods can recover causal structures up to Markov equivalence. Causal discovery under latent confounding is an active topic of research. The FCI family [ 4, 20 , 31 ]can discover and mark edges that are likely confounded. Nested Markov Models (NMMs) [ 7 , 25 , 28 ,29 ] leverage Verma constraints to identify latent factors. DCD [ 1] combines NMMs with differentiable constraints [ 38 ] to discover partial DAGs. Reddy et al. [24] utilize mutual information on observed distributions, and Karlsson and Krijthe [12] address latent confounding for effect estimation under a fixed graph structure. Kaltenpoth and Vreeken [9 , 11] model latent confounders directly, allowing identification of which nodes share latent confounders. 2There exists much less research on causal discovery under selection. FCI [ 31 ] is sound under selection, but Spirtes et al. [32] show that single observational studies are generally insufficient to distinguish between confounding or selection. To resolve this, Versteeg et al. [33] identify local patterns to detect causal effects under both biases. Recent work has expanded these capabilities to temporal dependencies in sequential data [ 39 ], "twin graph" frameworks for interventional settings [ 5], and rank-based constraints for linear Gaussian models [ 6]. These methods respective require it i.i.d. or sequential data, or strict parametric assumptions. Recent research leverages non-i.i.d. data, in particular distribution shifts across different environments, to relax the assumptions necessary to identify causal DAGs. Existing methods primarily focus on handling latent confounding. JCI [ 18 ] extends existing causal discovery methods with a context variable. Perry et al. [22] focus on sparse mechanism shifts, whereas CD-NOD [ 8] and C OCO [14 ]both exploit independent mechanism changes. Our approach leverages multi-environment data to identify the presence of, and, which nodes are affected by hidden confounding resp. selection bias by detecting dependent mechanism shifts.

We then ask the central question: what patterns in the observed context shifts differentially reveal latent confounding and respectively selection?

Based on these results, we next introduce a practical algorithm which leverages the notion of ancestral coverage to distinguish confounding from selection bias in the finite data setting.

6.1 Detecting Structural Biases 

First, we are interested in understanding whether and how well S TRU BI can detect structural biases. To this end, we generate synthetic data as above, and consider S TRU BI ( Πobs ), S TRU BI ( G∗), and STRU BI ( ˜G). We give the results in Fig. 2. On the left-hand side, we report the F 1 scores for classifying the type of structural bias (i.e. none, confounding, selection) resp. for determining which nodes are affected. S TRU BI ( Πobs ) shows our theory works; knowing the actual mechanism shifts, S TRU BI performs perfectly. More interestingly, S TRU BI given an inferred graph ˆG performs essentially as well as when given the true graph G∗. The right-hand panel shows the AMI and coverage values. We see that classification performance goes hand in hand with how well partitions are recovered (top row), as well as that when more nodes share a confounder, it becomes harder to classify it from the case of selection with many ancestral nodes. Next, we evaluate how well S TRU BI performs compared to the baselines. To this end we consider STRU BI given graph ˆG inferred using T OPIC [ 35 ], JCI-FCI and FCI-P given the causal skeletons, CoCo given the ground truth graph G∗, and LS-P and LS-C. We are interested in (i) how well can each identify the presence of latent variables, (ii) identify the affected variables, and (iii) how well can they identify the type of structural bias. We run each method on the same data as above, and present the results in Fig. 3. We see that for each of the three tasks, S TRU BI outperforms all baselines by a large margin. LS-P and LS-C do not do very well, probably because of their parametric and structural assumptions and model misspecification. CoCo is the best performing competitor, and the only one that improves in subset recovery as the DAG size increases. S TRU BI also outperforms the baselines in terms of the accuracy, precision, and recall. We give those results in Appx. Fig. 12. 

6.2 Case Study – Cell Signaling 

To see how well S TRU BI performs in a real-world settings, we consider the ( infamous ) Sachs et al. [27] flow cytometry dataset. The data contains samples of eleven protein and phospholipid components in human immune cells that were studied under different molecular interventions. ## 7 Discussion & Conclusion 

We studied the problem of identifying structural biases from observational data. In particular, we showed under which conditions it is possible to infer the presence of latent confounding or selection bias on latent colliders, given data from multiple contexts by detecting dependent mechanism changes. To tell which nodes are subject to what kind of bias in practice, we proposed the S TRU BI algorithm. Through an extensive set of experiments, including a case study on cell-signalling data, we showed that S TRU BI performs well in practice, outperforming the state of the art in detecting confounding, selection bias, and classifying between them, even when our assumptions do not strictly hold. 

Limitations As with all approaches in causal inference, we too need to make assumptions on how the world works. While we relax the assumptions of sufficiency and i.i.d.-ness, we do require the Markov condition and faithfulness. This means that for systems with cyclic dependencies, our guarantees do not hold. We see this as an important direction for future work. A key assumption in our work is that mechanism changes are sparse and independent. While generally reasonable, this will not always hold in practice, and it will be interesting to generalize our theory given (limited) knowledge about intervention targets, or by including recent results on identifying intervention targets. A practical limitation is that S TRU BI relies on a conditional independence test, and therewith requires sufficiently many i.i.d. samples per context, and similarly, to determine independence of mechanism shifts, requires data from sufficiently many contexts. It will be interesting to extend our theory and method to time-series data, where contexts can possibly be replaced with regimes. 9References

C.7 STRU BI and its variants

## D Additional Results on the Cell-Signaling Data 

The Sachs et al. [27] dataset is particularly interesting as it contains multiple contexts with known interventions. However, it is also very challenging as the system is complex and has many cyclic dependencies [ 16 ] and hence these interventions might affect multiple nodes in overlapping contexts, leading to noisy partitions. In the unbiased setting, S TRU BI finds overlaps in causal mechanisms of Akt-Mek, Plcg-P38, and PKA-PKC. This can be due to biological crosstalk. For instance, the dependencies detected between Akt-Mek may be attributed to the extensive pathway crosstalk between the Ras-MAPK and PI3K-mTORC1 cascades [ 17 ]. Similarly, the identified dependencies between PKA-PKC likely reflect shared regulation via phospholipase C(PLC) signaling; such simultaneous activation pathways can render conditioning on PLC γ alone insufficient for isolation [ 19 ]. Finally, the overlapping shifts in Plcg-P38 may reflect parallel activation by common upstream MAP3Ks in response to shared extracellular growth or stress signals [2].
