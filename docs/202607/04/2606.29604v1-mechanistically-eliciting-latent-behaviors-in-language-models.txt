Abstract 

We aim to discover diverse, generalizable perturbations of LLM internals that can surface hidden behavioral modes. Such perturbations could help reshape model behavior and systematically evaluate potential risks. We introduce Causal Perturbative Elicitation (CPE), an unsupervised method for discov-ering interpretable low-rank adapters (LoRAs) that can elicit these latent behaviors. CPE decomposes the computations of a deep transformer slice using a heuristic tensor-decomposition-based algorithm. CPE exhibits remarkable data efficiency, learning a large number of interpretable LoRAs from a single example .Even though CPE is unsupervised, we find that in some cases it can be competitive with supervised elicitation methods via brute-force enumerative search over weight space. For instance, CPE performs similarly to matched-wall-clock-time GRPO (Shao et al., 2024) on the Countdown task (Katz et al., 2026) for Qwen3-8B (85% vs 87%), demonstrating that CPE can efficiently elicit complex multi-token behaviors. Since CPE is unsupervised, it can also surface hidden failure modes, such as sandbagging, restoring 85% of locked BigCodeBench (Zhuo et al., 2025) performance on a password-locked version of Llama3-70B introduced by (Taylor et al., 2025). Additionally, since CPE explores behaviors in weight-space rather than token-space it can potentially ameliorate exploration hacking, a misalignment failure which may arise in sufficiently self-aware AI models (Ngo, 2022). In fact, we find that CPE virtually eliminates alignment-faking (Greenblatt et al., 2024) behavior in a Llama3-70B-based model organism developed by (Hughes et al., 2025). Finally, we find that CPE can be used to initialize GPT-OSS-20B in an “aligned basin” when running GRPO on an environment prone to reward-hacking. By providing a data-efficient method to systematically explore the space of latent model behaviors, CPE yields a powerful tool for aligning AI systems and evaluating their safety.

The existence of these latent modes poses both opportunities and risks: we may be leaving valuable capabilities untapped, while dangerous behaviors could emerge unexpectedly. This problem becomes particularly pressing as we consider future AI systems that may develop more sophisticated strategies to resist behavioral modification. Concerns about gradient hacking (Ngo, 2022) and alignment faking (Greenblatt et al., 2024) suggest that future models might become increasingly “sticky,” resisting reinforcement learning (RL) approaches when we attempt to modify their behavior. The core issue is that traditional RL adds noise at output layers, where models can learn to resist changes. Ideally, we would discover meaningful perturbations at early layers, where resistance is harder. Such an approach could prove crucial for reshaping model behavior in future AI systems. A second important problem is systematically evaluating model behavior . We require techniques that can comprehensively audit models for diverse failure modes, uncovering potentially harmful hidden personas. Additionally, we desire methods to systematically fuzz a model, eliciting concealed knowledge and capabilities. Crucially, we need perturbations that generalize well. In behavior reshaping, generalization ensures consistent 2access to desired capabilities. In model evaluation, generalization increases our confidence that we’ve isolated a genuine behavioral mode rather than an isolated anomalous output. We address these challenges by developing an unsupervised method to discover perturbations in early-layer weights which induce substantial downstream changes in the model’s internals. Our core hypothesis is that perturbations causing large downstream effects within the network (as measured by changes in later-layer activations) correspond to human-interpretable concepts and behavioral modes. Intuitively, meaningful concepts should activate entire computational pathways that propagate through the network, while random perturbations produce only local noise that quickly dissipates. By discovering causally important perturbations and using them to steer the network’s outputs, we can reliably elicit different behavioral modes, bypassing the limitations of surface-level prompting. We mathematically search for diverse, causally important perturbations by learning a shallow MLP approxi-mation of the causal structure of an intermediate, 10-20 layer transformer slice. We call our technique Causal Perturbative Elicitation (CPE). The key innovation is our causal training objective. Existing unsupervised methods like sparse autoencoders (SAEs) (Bricken et al., 2023) discover interpretable features by learning to reconstruct activations subject to sparsity constraints. In contrast, CPE focuses on matching the differential structure of the transformer slice. This enables remarkable data efficiency compared to SAEs, discovering interpretable weight perturbations from as little as a single training example. CPE elicits latent behaviors across a broad range of tasks. A summary of our findings is as follows: 

Enhanced behavioral diversity We demonstrate that CPE perturbations trained on Llama3.1-8B-Instruct and Qwen3-8B elicit coherent personas on open-ended conversational prompts about as effectively as SAE-based steering vectors, even when training on only one prompt (as opposed to billions of tokens for the SAE baselines). 

Comparison with SAEs and GRPO We compare the effectiveness of CPE at eliciting a number of natural behaviors (reasoning, anti-sycophancy and jailbreaking) against standard unsupervised (SAE) and supervised (GRPO) baselines. We find that CPE is more effective at eliciting these behaviors than SAE feature steering, despite requiring a comparatively tiny amount of training data. Additionally CPE is sometimes as effective as GRPO at eliciting behaviors such as anti-sycophancy and reasoning. 

Reward Hacking In a competitive programming environment with mis-specified reward, we find that CPE can be used to initialize GRPO in an “aligned” basin of GPT-OSS-20B’s weight-space. In particular, CPE + GRPO on mis-specified reward achieves comparable gold-standard performance as running GRPO with the correctly specified reward, while random initialization leads to reward hacking and a collapse in gold-standard reward. 

Sandbagging We find that CPE restores 77% of password-locked coding performance on a password-locked version of Llama3-70B (Taylor et al., 2025). 

Alignment-Faking We find that CPE virtually eliminates alignment-faking in a synthetic-document-fine-tuned variant of Llama3-70B (Hughes et al., 2025). 32 Related work 

Activation engineering. Steering vectors are directions that induce specific behaviors when added to a model’s activation space. Prior work has successfully learned these vectors in a supervised manner for many high-level behaviors (Subramani et al., 2022; Turner et al., 2024; Zou et al., 2023; Panickserry et al., 2024). Meanwhile, Fierro and Roger (2026) successfully steer models in weight space. We build on this literature by exploring how many behaviors we are able to elicit with a purely unsupervised method. 

Sparse coding and feature learning. The dominant approach to discovering interpretable features in neural networks relies on sparse coding methods, particularly sparse autoencoders (SAEs) (Bricken et al., 2023; Huben et al., 2024; Templeton et al., 2024). While SAEs have shown promise in identifying behaviorally relevant features, they face fundamental limitations for our goals. Most critically, SAEs suffer from the “dark matter” problem of neural network interpretability (Engels et al., 2025). Given that models likely contain far more features than dimensions in their residual stream, even the widest SAEs trained to date (with millions of features) potentially miss the vast majority of features. We thus need a data-efficient method to “fill in the gaps” on high-stakes datasets of interest, allowing us to discover important features that SAEs might miss. Moreover, as future models increasingly employ online reinforcement learning, their internal features may evolve over time, further necessitating more adaptive discovery methods that can operate with limited data. 

Contrastive learning. Dalva and Yanardag (2024)’s NoiseCLR uses a contrastive objective to learn meaningful representations in image diffusion models. NoiseCLR is similar to our causal learning objective in that NoiseCLR searches for vector perturbations which induce consistent changes in downstream activations. However, CPE achieve superior data efficiency, both in principle and in practice. Specifically, the main objective defined in equation (5) of (Dalva and Yanardag, 2024) relies on computing pairwise similarities between downstream activation vectors across data-points , which is meaningless when applied to a single training example. In contrast, we show that training CPE on a single prompt can elicit generalizable behaviors. 

Jacobian decompositions. Ramesh et al. (2018) found that the right singular vectors of the Jacobian of the generator network in GANs yield a small number ( ∼ 32 ) of interpretable feature directions in generative image models. Bushnaq et al. (2024) found that Jacobian-based feature detection schemes are less successful when applied to language models. In contrast, Lin (2026) studies SVD applied to the empirical neural tangent kernel (the Jacobian of network outputs with respect to weights), discovering features in both toy models and language models, although they do not study steering. CPE works similarly to a Jacobian decomposition of a middle slice of a transformer network. However, our theorem 3.1 shows (in a heuristic sense) that by training at a larger scale we implicitly decompose the higher-order differential tensors of the sliced transformer, allowing for the identification of non-orthogonal factor directions.

At evaluation time, we are given a scalar metric μ quantifying the degree to which a target behavior is present (e.g. a pass rate, an accuracy, or an attack success rate). We run inference with each of the m trained adapters on the validation split, compute μ for each, and select the adapter i⋆ = arg max i∈{ 1,...,m } μval (i). We then report the test-set score μtest (i⋆) as the measure of how effectively CPE elicits the behavior corresponding to μ. Because CPE is unsupervised, the same set of adapters can be reused across different metrics. ## 3.3 Baseline: causally important SAE features 

Sparse autoencoders (SAEs) represent a promising approach to feature discovery in neural networks, learning large dictionaries of interpretable features by training on massive datasets (Bricken et al., 2023; Huben et al., 2024; Templeton et al., 2024), amortizing the cost of feature discovery. A fair comparison requires a principled method for selecting which SAE features to evaluate. Modern SAEs can contain millions of features (Lieberum et al., 2024), making exhaustive evaluation computationally prohibitive. We thus need an unsupervised selection criterion that identifies the most relevant SAE features for some dataset of interest. We propose selecting SAE features using the same criterion that motivates CPE: causal importance . Specifically, we measure how strongly each SAE feature direction influences downstream activations when used as a steering vector. # 6 Application to model organisms of misalignment 

The previous sections demonstrated that CPE can elicit complex behaviors, sometimes nearly as efficiently as supervised methods. A key advantage of CPE being unsupervised is that it can be applied in settings where we do not know a priori how to quantify the behavior we want to elicit, or where supervised methods fail to produce the desired behavior even when we believe it is latent in the model’s weights. In this section we explore applications along these lines to model organisms of misalignment —models that have been trained or prompted to exhibit specific misaligned behaviors, and that therefore serve as controlled testbeds for alignment techniques that may be needed for future, more capable systems (Hubinger et al., 2024). We study three such organisms: a reward-hacking environment in which a model learns to game a mis-specified reward signal (section 6.1), an alignment-faking organism that strategically complies only when it believes it is being monitored (section 6.2), and a password-locked sandbagging organism that deliberately underperforms on coding tasks (section 6.3). 

## 6.1 Reward hacking 

Reward hacking is a particular concern for models that grow more capable over the course of training: even if a weak supervisor can detect reward gaming at the start of training, it may fail to do so once the model becomes more sophisticated. Such gaps between weak supervisors and strong models have served as a consistent thread throughout alignment research (Burns et al., 2023; Christiano et al., 2018). In this section, we study whether CPE can be used to initialize a model in an “aligned” basin of weight space and whether the model remains in that basin throughout reinforcement learning even when reward is mis-specified, as a proxy for learning monitored by a weak supervisor. 

Environment. We consider a competitive-programming environment similar to that of Golechha et al. (2026), built on a modified version of the X-Coder dataset (Wu et al., 2026), a collection of synthetic competition-level programming problems introduced after GPT-OSS-20B’s knowledge cutoff, sidestepping concerns about data contamination / memorization. Each prompt includes five visible test cases, while at least five additional hidden test cases are withheld. This yields a natural decomposition into a mis-specified reward (passes visible tests only) and a gold-standard reward (passes all tests). Following Golechha et al. (2026), we include a hint in the system prompt noting that LLMs sometimes overwrite test cases, encouraging GPT-OSS-20B to reward-hack (in other words, a prompt-based model organism; details are provided in 10 (a)

A concern with applying standard RL to sufficiently self-aware models is that the model may strategically limit its exploration so as to avoid generating outcomes that would trigger gradient updates away from its current behavior (Ngo, 2022). Greenblatt et al. (2024) demonstrated a concrete instance of this failure mode: when Claude was made aware via synthetic document fine-tuning (SDF) that it would be trained to comply with all user requests, including harmful ones, it selectively complied only when it believed its outputs would be used for training, while refusing in contexts it believed were unmonitored. Hughes et al. (2025) reproduced this phenomenon in an open-weights setting by applying synthetic document fine-tuning (SDF) to Llama-3.3-70B, producing a model organism that exhibits robust alignment-faking behavior. Because CPE operates in weight space rather than token space, it can in principle bypass this kind of exploration hacking, discovering behavioral modes that the model would never produce under standard sampling. We apply CPE to the Hughes et al. (2025) model organism. In this scenario, the hypothetical AI developers aim to train Llama-3.3-70B to be helpful-only (complying with all user requests) via GRPO on free-tier users. The alignment-faking strategy adopted by the model is to comply with requests from free-tier users, whose outputs are used for training, so as to avoid generating gradient signal that would further modify its weights. On paid-tier requests, which it believes are unmonitored, the model follows its “true” preferences and refuses. The model frequently verbalizes this reasoning in its hidden scratchpad. The baseline model after SDF sits in a clear alignment-faking basin: the compliance gap (free-tier compliance minus paid-tier compliance) is 58%, and the rate of verbalized alignment-faking reasoning is 84%. However, CPE reveals that several other behavioral modes are nearby in weight space. We quantify these behavioral modes using a Deepseek-V4-Flash-based judge; details are provided in Appendix H. As shown in table 2, one adapter raises minimum compliance across both tiers to 92%, essentially exhibiting the helpful-only behavior desired by the hypothetical developers. Representative scratchpad reasoning from this adapter is shown in fig. 4: the model verbalizes a consistent prioritization of the developer’s goals on both paid and free-tier prompts. From the perspective of the hypothetical supervisors, steering with this adapter would already yield a largely helpful-only model without additional RL training. Other adapters surface qualitatively different modes of behavior, and CPE can both increase and decrease the compliance gap. Adapter 284 exhibits a negative compliance gap, refusing on the free tier while complying on the paid tier. Adapter 121, by contrast, accentuates verbalized alignment-faking reasoning beyond the baseline rate. # 7 Conclusion 

We introduced Causal Perturbative Elicitation (CPE), an unsupervised approach for discovering causally important directions in an LLM’s weight space. CPE yields interpretable rank-1 LoRAs using minimal data and minimal compute. Empirically, CPE modulates complex, multi-token behaviors including reason-ing capabilities, password-locked capabilities, jailbreaks, reward hacking, anti-sycophantic tendencies and alignment-faking. It does so more effectively than SAE features, despite requiring a tiny amount of training data (1-100 prompts). Future work could extend CPE to higher-rank perturbations, potentially enabling more sophisticated behavioral modifications. 15 References

OALS for quadratic tensors. Suppose we wanted to perform a CP decomposition of T (2) , a three-way tensor (one output mode in Rd, two input modes in RD ). The orthogonalized alternating least squares (OALS) method of Sharan and Valiant (2017) alternates between the updates ⃗u ← T (2) (·, ϕ, ϕ ), ϕ ← T (2) (⃗u, ϕ, ·),

followed by normalization and orthogonalization across components. 

Quadratic case. Consider the objective f (ϕ) := ⟨⃗u, ∆ S→ t(Rϕ )⟩. If ∆S→ t were quadratic, then 

∇ϕf (ϕ) = R2 T (2) (⃗u, ϕ, ·),

so the update ϕ ← ∇ ϕf (ϕ) recovers the OALS contraction step up to the scalar R2. Thus in the quadratic case, SOGI coincides with OALS, differing only in the orthogonalization method (soft orthogonalization rather than QR). For the LoRA parameterization, the gradient ∇ϕf decomposes blockwise into gradients with respect to each ⃗a ℓ,⃗ b ℓ. The per-component normalization (projecting each block back to the unit sphere) replaces the single spherical projection of the original steering-vector formulation with a product-of-spheres projection onto the torus T .

General case: weighted higher-order contractions. For general ∆S→ t, the Taylor expansion gives 

∇ϕ⟨⃗u, ∆ S→ t(Rϕ )⟩ = X

The gradient update thus acts as a weighted sum of contractions of all higher-order derivative tensors, with weights proportional to Rk/(k − 1)! . These contractions are accessible through ordinary automatic differentiation, without ever forming higher-order tensors explicitly. 

Soft orthogonalization. OALS uses a hard QR step, which limits the decomposition to at most D

components. We replace QR with the soft orthogonalization of section A, which iteratively repels columns by their pairwise similarity in the flattened ϕ-space while preserving high-gradient-magnitude directions.
