CONDITIONAL MEAN INDEPENDENCE AND GLOBAL SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS USING NEAREST NEIGHBOR GRAPHS 

ANIRBAN CHATTERJEE, ZIANG NIU, AND BHASWAR B. BHATTACHARYA 

Abstract. Quantifying how well a conditional mean function explains a response is central to many statistical tasks, such as model evaluation and feature screening. A basic nonparametric measure of such dependence is the proportion of variation in the response explained by the regression function, which can also be interpreted as a multivariate Sobol’ index, a fundamental notion in global sensitivity analysis. In this paper, we propose a consistent estimator of this measure based on nearest neighbor graphs that can be computed in near-linear time. We also derive its rate of convergence and show that a studentized version of the estimator is asymptotically standard normal under the null hypothesis of conditional mean independence. This leads to a computationally efficient test for conditional mean independence that attains the correct asymptotic level and is universally consistent, without requiring bootstrap calibration or sample splitting. Next, we use the proposed estimator to develop a model-free variable screening algorithm that is provably consistent. We also discuss extensions of the framework to measuring interaction effects using higher-order Sobol’ indices. The benefits of the proposed methods are demonstrated through simulation studies and a real-data example. 

1. Introduction 

Understanding how a collection of covariates influences a response variable is central to many statistical problems, including variable selection [42, 53, 63, 64, 73], feature screening [25, 26, 44, 58, 73, 74], graphical modeling [27, 34, 45, 46], and applications in the biological sciences [24, 51, 61]. This has motivated the study of feature importance and dependence measures, an area that has attracted renewed attention in recent years owing to the increasing reliance on black-box models for handling the complexity of large-scale modern problems. While a substantial body of work has focused on general notions of dependence (see [10, 40] for recent surveys), in regression-type problems the primary object of interest is often the conditional mean, since it captures the systematic effect of the covariates on the response [16] (see [69] for a recent survey of various notions of feature importance in regression). A natural diagnostic step toward understanding whether a collection of covariates X affects an outcome variable Y is to determine whether X contributes to the conditional mean of Y . This entails testing the null hypothesis: 

H0 : ErY | Xs “ ErY s almost surely , (1.1) based on i.i.d. samples tp Yi, Xiqu 1ďiďn. This is the problem of testing conditional mean inde-pendence, which may be viewed as the nonparametric analogue of the regression goodness-of-fit problem. Several approaches to testing this hypothesis have been developed over the years. One approach is to estimate a (weighted) L2-discrepancy between ErY | Xs and ErY s using techniques from kernel density estimation [1, 21, 28, 42, 65, 77]. However, in most cases, for such methods convergence rate of the test statistic under H0 depends on the kernel bandwidth and typically wors-ens with dimension. Another popular approach is based on the martingale difference divergence (MDD) [58], a natural extension of the celebrated distance covariance [62] to the conditional mean setting. However, MDD-based tests usually require bootstrap calibration [41, 43], because their null distributions are non-pivotal and do not admit closed-form critical values. Another emerging

These methods, however, typically require sample splitting for estimation of the mean function, and the performance of the resulting test depends on the accuracy of the underlying nonparametric estimation. Quantifying the influence of input variables on a model output is also the central objective in Global Sensitivity Analysis (GSA) [19, 57, 70]. Classical approaches in this area rely on an analysis-of-variance (ANOVA) decomposition, with Sobol’ indices [59, 60] being among the most widely used metrics for measuring variable importance. The following section summarizes the main methodological and theoretical results obtained in the paper. In particular, we present the proposed nearest neighbor estimators, establish their consistency and convergence rates, develop a test for conditional mean independence, and develop an algorithm for variable screening. We also discuss how the same framework naturally extends to measuring higher-order interaction effects. 1.1. Summary of Results. Our starting point is a basic measure of conditional mean dependence that quantifies the proportion of variability in the response explained by the regression function. This quantity has three fundamental properties one would expect from a mean dependence measure: it takes values in the interval r0, 1s, vanishes if and only if conditional mean independence holds, and attains its maximal value of 1 when the response is completely determined by the covariates (see Proposition 2.1). Moreover, this measure coincides with the classical Sobol’ index, when the response is univariate, and its natural trace-based extension, when the response is multivariate [30, 31] (see Section 2.2 for details). In this paper, we propose a nearest neighbor-based estimator of this index and establish the following properties: 

‚ The estimate has a simple, interpretable form, which does not require any estimation of density or distribution functions. Moreover, the estimate can be computed in near-linear time (with a fixed number of nearest neighbors), irrespective of the dimension of the data (see Remark 3.1). 

‚ The estimator is consistent for the population measure under mild moment conditions (Theorem 3.1). Furthermore, in Theorem 3.2 we obtain the rate at which the estimator converges to the population measure as the sample size increases. As a consequence, one obtains an analogous convergence guarantee for the estimated Sobol’ index, linking the finite-sample behavior of our estimator directly to that of the corresponding population sensitivity measure. 

‚ Under the null hypothesis of conditional mean independence, the estimator is asymptotically standard Gaussian, after a simple data-driven standardization (Theorem 4.1). This allows us to readily select the rejection threshold (based on the asymptotic distribution), resulting in a test that asymptotically has the correct level and is universally consistent against fixed alternatives (Corollary 4.1). Consequently, our method is significantly faster than existing tests for conditional mean independence that rely on non-pivotal limiting distributions and therefore require bootstrap calibration (see Remark 4.3). Another important advantage of our approach is that it does not require sample splitting. This is particularly important in CONDITIONAL MEAN INDEPENDENCE AND GLOBAL SENSITIVITY ANALYSIS 3

practice, since sample splitting can lead to a loss of power in finite samples. Indeed, our empirical results show that the proposed test often exhibits improved power compared to recent methods that rely on sample splitting to estimate the regression function. Next, in Section 5, we develop a model-free variable screening algorithm based on our nearest neighbor estimator of the conditional dependence index. The procedure adds variables sequentially according to their estimated contribution to the conditional mean signal. In Theorem 5.1 we show that the proposed algorithm selects a sufficient set (see (5.2)) with high probability, with an exponential error bound in the sample size. Then, in Section 6, we discuss how the nearest neighbor framework can be extended beyond the estimation of conditional mean dependence and global sensitivity measures to the estimation of interaction effects through higher-order Sobol’ indices [30, 31]. In particular, we focus on the second-order case and propose a method for quantifying the proportion of response variation attributable purely to interaction effects, beyond the separate contributions of the individual inputs. Finally, in Section 7, we present empirical results on both simulated and real data, comparing our methods with existing approaches. The following are the summary of our findings: 

‚ In Section 7.1, we compare our proposed test with other popular methods for conditional mean independence, specifically the MDD based test [43, 58] and the partial Mean Indepen-dence Test (pMIT) [5], as well as with tests designed for the more general null hypothesis of conditional independence, such as distance covariance (dCov) [62] and the Azadkia– Chatterjee coefficient [2, 9]. The experiments show that the proposed procedure achieves strong power against a wide range of nonlinear alternatives, controls Type I error in settings where more general dependence tests over-reject, and is computationally much faster than the competing methods. 

‚ In the variable screening experiments, the proposed method is competitive with existing model-free screening procedures in simulation studies and yields strong predictive perfor-mance on the augmented California Housing dataset (Section 7.2). Remark 3.2. Given the connection between the NCMD measure and the classical Sobol’ index, it is natural to ask whether existing estimation strategies for Sobol’ indices can be adapted to this setting. The classical Pick-and-Freeze approach [32, 38] is not directly applicable beyond the setting of GSA, as it is a resampling-based method that requires generating additional independent samples and repeated evaluations of the model. There have been alternative approaches that rely solely on the observed samples, motivated by the high computational cost of model evaluations, and that employ sample-splitting strategies [17]. More recently, [33] drew inspiration from the rank-based approach of [9] to propose a rank-based estimator of Sobol’ indices in the univariate setting, where both Y and X are univariate. Chhaibi et al. [15] further generalized this to the multivariate response setting, by defining a coordinate-wise Sobol’ index, and established asymptotic normality of the corresponding estimator. Lin and Han [47] also defined the same estimator when X is allowed to be multivariate, but Y is still univariate. In this univariate setting, our estimator (with K “ 1) can be viewed as a comparable approach to the rank-based estimator. However, it is important to emphasize that our method is more general and remains well defined when both Y and X are allowed to be multi-dimensional. Moreover, [33] demonstrates that, in simple settings with linear associations, the rank-based approach outperforms the classical Pick-and-Freeze method. This observation suggests that, in the multi-dimensional setting, our nearest neighbor-based approach may yield similar improvements.
