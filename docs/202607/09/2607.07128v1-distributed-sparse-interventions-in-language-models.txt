## Abstract 

Language models perform a wide range of tasks at varying levels of abstraction with the capacity to flexibly infer tasks from context, execute multiple tasks simulta-neously, and select among competing tasks. To study the role of model components in task behaviour, their causal influence can be investigated through interventions. Prior work on model steering has largely focused on interventions along global directions in activation space, modeling task representations as approximately linear and additive. By studying interventions at the neuron level, we find substan-tial, neuron-specific nonlinear effects on model outputs that are not captured by current steering approaches. We introduce Distributed Sparse Interventions (DSI), an intervention approach that considers nonlinearities and interactions between neurons across layers to identify sparse sets of neurons that elicit task-relevant computations. Across a range of tasks, we demonstrate that DSI can activate task behaviour in instruction-tuned language models by localising and intervening on as few as 0.01% of neurons, highlighting the effectiveness of sparse, distributed interventions in the neuron basis. Additionally, adopting a set-based perspective enables computations over the identified neuron sets, offering insights into the roles of individual neurons by analysing their effects across tasks. Through sparse interventions, DSI enables fine-grained control over model behaviour, localisation of task-relevant neuron sets, and furthers our understanding of task composition. 

## 1 Introduction 

Modern language models demonstrate flexible task-solving behaviours, including the adaptive com-bination and execution of multiple tasks through in-context learning (ICL) [ 10 , 26 , 53 ]. However, the mechanisms by which these models represent and compose tasks remain largely elusive, particu-larly in scenarios requiring the combination, isolation, or selective activation of behaviours. In the context of model steering, prior research has primarily focused on interventions along directions in activation space [ 43 , 49 , 18 , 8, 32 ], leveraging the observation that many task-relevant behaviours exhibit approximately linear structure in representation space. Such vector-based steering approaches typically rely on a number of key assumptions: (1) effective interventions require modifying all neuron activations in a specific layer or attention head, (2) tasks are represented as linear directions in activation space that can be combined additively, and (3) local first-order effect approximations in activation space are sufficient to identify interventions that induce approximately proportional changes in behaviour. We demonstrate that these assumptions can be violated in practice. Task-relevant computations are distributed across model components, and interventions often exhibit nonlinear interaction effects across neurons that violate the assumptions of uniform first-order approximations. This motivates the development of fine-grained intervention strategies that operate directly on neurons rather than on global directions in activation space. ## 2 Related work 

Model steering Prior work on model steering has largely focused on identifying linear directions in activation space to intervene on model behaviour. Associated vectors are typically calculated from in-context-learning examples [ 49 , 18 , 32 ] as well as contrastive sample pairs [ 43 ], or via optimization, e.g. in approaches like LoFiT [ 54 ], which trains additive biases applied to specific attention heads. While these methods can effectively alter model behaviour and are sometimes sparse in terms of components such as whole attention heads, they operate at the level of global directions in representation space. 

Sparse representations in neural networks Often inspired by neuroscientific findings of sparse coding [ 50 , 38 , 39 ], prior work on understanding representations in artificial neural networks in-vestigated sparse representations. Research on model compression and pruning [ 27 ], as well as the lottery ticket hypothesis [ 15 , 14 ], suggests the existence of underlying sparse structure in large deep learning models by identifying sparse subnetworks capable of matching the performance of dense 2models. In vision models, neuron units aligning with human-interpretable concepts were identified [7]. Similarly, in language models, specific ‘skills’ [ 51 ] and ‘knowledge’ [ 11 ] have been localised to neurons, and sparse subnetworks have been shown to support strong performance through targeted fine-tuning [ 40 ]. As neural models may represent features in superposition [ 13 ], recent interpretability work has explored dictionary learning to disentangle representations into interpretable features [ 9], assuming the native neuron basis is not sufficiently sparse. Further efforts have focused on learning a sparse basis of interpretable features either at a specific layer (e.g., sparse autoencoders [ 9, 21 ]), or across multiple layers (e.g., crosscoders [ 29 ]), in order to generate sparser circuits [ 33 ]. While this line of work relies on features in dense, high-dimensional spaces, it remains an open question to what extent task-relevant structure is accessible within the native neuron basis. More recently, it has been found that for some circuits that previously have been analyzed using sparse autoencoders, an analysis directly in the neuron basis of the MLP components of transformer blocks can uncover circuits of similar sparsity [3]. 

Vector arithmetic in representation space Previous work has investigated the compositional nature of interventions [ 49 , 32 , 30 ], finding that steering vectors identified during ICL can be additive, allowing for the activation of task combinations through vector summation. Such modularity is not limited to activations but extends to the model’s parameters. Task vectors in weight space, derived from the difference between fine-tuned and pre-trained weights, can be combined to modify model capabilities [ 22 ]. Recent work further suggests that arithmetic capabilities are grounded in the composition of sets of modular heuristics [ 37 ]. While these studies highlight the compositional nature of task solving in LLMs, they largely rely on dense representations. 

## 3 DSI - Distributed Sparse Interventions 

We introduce Distributed Sparse Interventions (DSI), a method to identify and apply effective interventions on small sets of neurons distributed across layers and attention heads. As illustrated in Figure 1, DSI follows a four-step procedure: (1) activation differences between 10-shot and 0-shot prompts are computed for all neurons to isolate task-specific model responses; (2) a sparse initial intervention is derived from these activation differences and a robustified gradient, retaining only the most impactful neurons; (3) a refined intervention is generated via step-wise optimization of the sparse neuron set and neuron-level intervention scaling, accounting for neuron interactions (see Appendix A for an illustration and analysis of why modeling these interactions is necessary); and (4) during a modified forward pass , this intervention is applied to elicit the task-solving capabilities on 0-shot examples. After discussing preliminaries, first-order intervention effect estimation and robustified gradients are defined in Section 3.1, and the iterative intervention refinement procedure is introduced in Section 3.2. 

Notation Let f denote a model mapping a sequence x of input tokens to output logits. A task is defined via a set D of input-output pairs (x, y ). For an input-output pair, f (x)i refers to the output logit of the correct token, i.e., i denotes the output logit index of to the first token of y. We drop the index for a more concise notation, and write f (x) for f (x)i from now on. From a dataset D,we construct k-shot ICL prompts as a concatenation of k completed example pairs followed by an incomplete query, (( x1, y 1), (x2, y 2), . . . , (xk, y k), x k+1 ). Let Dk denote a dataset of such k-shot examples constructed from D, and D0 a dataset of 0-shot prompts, only containing the incomplete query but no in-context examples. We let a ∈ R denote a neuron activation in a hidden layer of f and δ ∈ R an intervention on that neuron. fδ := f (x|a ← a + δ) then denotes the model modified by intervention δ, that is, during a forward pass, a is computed from the input, then δ is added to a, and the forward pass is continued.

x0, the effect of adding an intervention δ to a neuron a during the forward pass is

Gradient modifications Deep neural network gradients suffer from instabilities such as gradient shattering [ 6 ], making them a potentially unreliable signal for the expected changes in model output under interventions. To mitigate this brittle behaviour, various other methods, such as integrated gradients [ 47 ], smooth grad [ 44 ], or layer-wise relevance propagation (LRP) [ 5, 34 ] have been proposed for computing more robust input attributions. Recent work extends these findings to activation patching and shows that intermediate attributions, using LRP-modified gradients, better estimate patching effects in transformer-based language models than raw gradients [ 23 ]. To improve the approximation of the intervention effect, we therefore use an LRP-modified gradient. The modifications include using the identity rule [ 42 ] for nonlinear activation functions, the half rule for gated MLPs and attention modules [ 4, 1 ], and the LayerNorm rule for layer normalization modules [ 2]. We denote this robustified gradient by ∂r and provide additional details on the rules in Appendix D.

Iterative refinement So far, we approximated the expected effect of an intervention δ on a neuron 

a with a first-order Taylor expansion around the root point a0, i.e. the value the neuron takes on during a forward pass on a 0-shot prompt. This disregards nonlinear effects of interventions, as well as interaction effects among interventions, which, as we show in Section 4, can be sizeable in highly nonlinear transformer networks. As explicit computation of higher-order terms across thousands of interventions is computationally infeasible, we opt for an iterative approximation. When maximizing a nonlinear function, it is typically not approximated only once at the initial point, but the function is repeatedly approximated, followed by a step in the gradient direction, and this process is iterated until a stopping criterion is met. Appendix A contains an illustrative example showing how such an iterative approach can help model interactions between neuron-level interventions. ## 4 Experiments 

Experimental setup We evaluate our approach on three instruction-tuned language models from widely used families, Qwen 3 (8B) [ 41 ], Gemma 3 (4B) [ 16 ], and Llama 3.2 (3B) [ 17 ] (see Ap-pendix B for details), spanning a range of model depths (28-36 layers), attention heads (8-32 heads), and hidden dimensions (128, 256). We consider a subset of 12 tasks from [ 49 ], including two abstrac-tion levels (see Table 3 for an overview): extractive , where the correct output is already present in the input (e.g., choosing the fruit from a set of words), and abstractive , where the output is not present in the input (e.g., naming the capital of a given country). For each task, we split the input-output pairs 50/50 into a training set and a test set. We then generate 100 0-shot prompts and 100 10-shot prompts from the training split. Two datasets contain fewer samples, resulting in smaller train and test splits. Details on the task data, splits, and prompt structure are provided in Appendix C. For the interventions, we consider the outputs of all attention heads in each layer at the last token position (before they are projected to the residual stream dimension).

Before proceeding to the main results, we illustrate the importance of this on the english–french task using Llama 3.2 (3B), by studying interventions on the most relevant neurons identified via a first-order approximation using the robustified gradient, but without iterative refinement. Distribution of intervened neurons across heads and layers Through its distributed, neuron-level intervention approach, DSI enables analysis of where sparse interventions are located across heads and layers. Figures 11, 12, and 13 in Appendix G show the distribution of identified neurons over attention heads. For instance, at a set size of 64, we find that neurons are broadly distributed, involving many attention heads across layers, in particular, middle and later layers. Compared to previous direction-based steering approaches, which rely on predefined intervention layers, e.g., [ 43 , 49 ], our findings suggest that task representation are distributed across multiple heads and layers, motivating intervention strategies that avoid constraining modifications to predefined layers. 

## 5 Task composition via sparse neuron sets 

Language models exhibit the ability to flexibly perform and compose multiple tasks from context [ 53 ,26 ], yet it remains an open challenge to understand where these behaviours originate and how they can be systematically controlled. Recent work has shown that linear representations enable multi-concept steering via per-layer addition of concept vectors such as transfer across languages [ 8]. However, such approaches operate in dense representation spaces and do not explicitly reveal how task-relevant computations are structured at the level of individual components. Having established that DSI enables effective interventions on sparse subsets of neurons for activating individual tasks, we next investigate whether tracing task computations back to neuron-level sources reveals shared structure and enables fine-grained control for selectively activating or suppressing tasks, particularly in multi-task settings. Unlike approaches that operate at the level of residual stream representations, this perspective acts directly on neurons before their contributions are combined, providing a more direct view of the origins of task computation and thereby facilitating interpretability. Analyzing a model’s internal task computations as sets of neurons further enables a compositional view based on set operations. Applying DSI in open-ended generation , we observe that DSI interventions for the present-past 

task (e.g., publish → published ) can induce past-tense usage, but also lead to highly repetitive 7outputs that prevent coherent continuation. In contrast to the fluent baseline response to a prompt such as “Are Annie Ernaux’s books good?” , where Qwen 3 (8B) responds: “Annie Ernaux’s books are widely regarded as **excellent** and **highly influential** in modern French literature [...]” ,under DSI intervention on 32 neurons, the same prompt yields “I started reading the book in 15 pages, I started reading the book in 15 pages, I started reading the book in 15 pages [...]” . This reflects the original task’s strict input–output structure, which requires reproducing the input under transformation. Similar behaviour has been observed n the context of vector-based steering [49]. Inspired by this observation, we hypothesize that the tense-switching and language-switching tasks from Section 4 are solved as a composition of input-reproduction (copying) and a transformation of the reproduced term. To examine whether a set-based approach can be used to decompose task behaviour into these two components, we conduct two targeted case studies. 

5.1 Case Study 1: Copying in Tense Switching 

Our first case study investigates whether we can isolate two subtasks within the present-past task, corresponding to copying the input and producing output in the past tense . To that end, we create samples for a copy task (where the output mirrors the input exactly, see Appendix C) and compute DSI interventions for a set size of 2048 , capturing neurons involved in copying the input. We then investigate (1) whether neurons involved in the copy task are also involved in the present-past task, (2) to what extent their activation leads to copy behaviour, and (3) whether activating only the 

present-past -specific neurons reduces copying while still activating task-specific behaviour. For the present-past task on Qwen 3 (8B), we choose sizes of 16 , 32 , and 64 neurons (the three smallest sets with high accuracy). Figure 4.a contains the resulting accuracies and a qualitative example for the set size of 32 , with full results in Table 5 in Appendix H.1. We can see that the intersection of the copy and present-past sets consists of 23 neurons, and that intervening on these neurons yields high performance on the copy task. We further see that the 9 neurons specific to the 

present-past task perform higher on this task than the 23 neurons in the intersection. Qualitatively, we observe copy behaviour intervening on both the intersection and all present-past neurons. Tense switching behaviour is observed intervening on all present-past neurons and the task-specific neurons, while the task-specific neurons induce less copy behaviour. Appendix H.2 contains details about the open-ended text generation as well as further qualitative examples that show that the qualitatively observed behaviour is generally robust, although the "specific" neuron set can fail to switch the generated answer to the past tense. 

5.2 Case Study 2: Copying in Language Switching 

Similarly, for the english-french task on Llama 3.2 (3B), we select sizes of 8, 16 , and 32 neurons. Figure 4.b contains the resulting accuracies and a qualitative example for the set size of 16 , with additional results reported in Table 6 in Appendix H.1. We find that the intersection of the copy 

and the english-french sets consists of 11 neurons, and that intervening on these neurons yields high performance on the copy task at around 92%. We further see that the 5 specific neurons perform higher on the english-french task than the 11 neurons in the intersection. Qualitatively, we observe copy behaviour intervening on both the intersection and all english-french neurons. Further qualitative examples in Appendix H.3 show that the qualitatively observed behaviour for interventions on these two sets is relatively robust, while the "specific" neuron set often fails to activate language switching or leads to a mix of English and French. This suggests that the effect of the 5 neurons in the specific set may not be sufficient to induce consistent language switching. Overall, our results suggest that sparse neuron-level interventions can reveal a structured decomposi-tion of task behaviour into shared and task-specific neuron sets. In both case studies, overlapping sets capture shared behaviours such as copying, while task-specific sets isolate the desired transformation with reduced interference. This provides initial evidence that task composition is reflected not only in representations but also in the underlying neuron-level structure. In contrast to vector-based steering methods [ 49 , 22 , 32 ], which model composition through direc-tions added to the residual stream, a set-based view allows for more explicit separation of shared and task-specific behaviour via set operations such as intersection and union. ## 6 Discussion 

We introduced Distributed Sparse Interventions (DSI) , a novel method for identifying sparse inter-ventions at the neuron level. In contrast to previous approaches, DSI does not impose constraints on linearity, independence, or uniformity of neuron intervention effects, and permits their distribution across all model components. Using DSI, we provided evidence that tasks can be activated by surpris-ingly small sets of neurons distributed widely throughout the model. Furthermore, we demonstrated that adopting a set-based perspective offers a new approach to disentangling the functional roles of the involved neurons. These findings suggest new directions for interpretability research, such as matching computations in task space to those on component sets, enabling manipulation of model behaviour along multiple subdimensions within a set of tasks. 

Limitations Our findings support the effectiveness of sparse and distributed interventions, but our approach and evaluation framework have limitations: (1) Our evaluation uses clean few-shot prompts and single-token prediction in an ICL setting. Future work should examine how optimal task interventions evolve during autoregressive generation and whether they are distributed across token positions [ 24 , 28 ]. (2) DSI performance varies across models and tasks, likely reflecting both DSI’s limitations and differences in representations. Further research may improve DSI in challenging settings to disambiguate representational from methodological limits. (3) Although our framework can isolate functional primitives (e.g., ‘copy’ neurons in translation and tense-switching), derived subtask interventions do not always activate associated behaviours. More advanced task-decomposition strategies may be needed to better preserve all subtasks. 

Societal impact Fundamental research on modifying language model behaviour could inform both alignment and potential misuse. While DSI may be employed for malicious manipulation, our goal is to advance understanding and improve safeguards for language models. 97 Acknowledgements

## F Algorithm details 

For the iterative refinement of the interventions, we use ZeroFPR [ 48 ], a numerical optimization algorithm suited for the sum of a non-convex differentiable function and a nonsmooth penalty term. To ensure sparsity of the identified neuron sets, we use the L0 pseudo-norm ball as a penalty.

Here, nnz( s) corresponds to the number of non-zero elements in s and n is the desired neuron set size. In our implementation of the algorithm, we follow the implementation in ProximalAlgorithms.jl [ 46 ]. Specifically, we use a convergence tolerance of 10 −8 with a maximum number of 50 steps, adaptive step size estimation, a maximum number of 20 line search backtracks, and L-BFGS [ 31 ] to compute the line-search directions with a memory of 5 steps. To facilitate escaping local minima near the starting values, we run the algorithm 10 times with perturbed starting values.
