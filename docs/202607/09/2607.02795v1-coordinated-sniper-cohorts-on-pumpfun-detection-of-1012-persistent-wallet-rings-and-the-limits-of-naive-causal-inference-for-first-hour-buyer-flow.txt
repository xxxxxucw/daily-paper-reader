# Coordinated Sniper Cohorts on Pump.fun: Detection of 1,012 Persistent Wallet Rings and the Limits of Naive Causal Inference for First-Hour Buyer Flow 

Arati Uday Kamat 

Independent Researcher · ORCID: 0009-0000-4781-312X 

Companion dataset: RED-COHORT-2026-v1 (CC-BY-4.0) 

Version: 1.0 (2026-06-27) 

Abstract 

We study coordinated buyer behavior on the Solana pump.fun bonding-curve marketplace using 1,578,333 buyer observations from 166,098 token launches between 2026-06-12 and 2026-06-26. A two-stage detection pipeline - intra-launch first-buyer-window extraction followed by cross-launch persistent-cohort surfacing via union-find on co-occurrence graphs - identifies  1,012 persistent wallet cohorts  (2 to 12 wallets each, drawn from 2,965 unique addresses) that systematically co-fire as early buyers across multiple launches. 153 of these qualify as high-tier (≥10 launches hit or score ≥100); 22 hit ≥20 launches each. The top-scoring cohort (9 wallets) appears among the first 10 buyers of 42 distinct launches over 11 days at an average first-buyer rank of 2.29. Under a 3:1 random-matched design, cohort-touched launches show a +132.3% lift in first-30-minute buyer count (95% CI [+127.0%, +137.4%], n_treated = 5,411) and +136.5% lift in SOL inflow (95% CI [+120.9%, +152.2%]).  An activity-matched placebo - drawing wallets from the non-cohort pool individually matched on per-wallet launch-count - yields a substantially LARGER buyer-flow lift of +216.3% (95% CI [+183.8%, +255.2%]) than the real-cohort effect.  The real-cohort point estimate falls well below the placebo confidence-interval lower bound, refuting a strong cohort-specific causal interpretation under this cleaner null. We interpret the empirical association as evidence of  selection : both real cohorts and activity-matched placebo wallets disproportionately appear in the buyer queues of launches that attract elevated buyer flow for reasons orthogonal to coordination. Identifying a cohort-specific causal effect therefore requires propensity-score matching on launch-quality covariates, which we leave to future work. We release the full cohort catalogue, detection code, and the descriptive plus robustness-check artefacts as  RED-COHORT-2026-v1 

under CC-BY-4.0. 

Keywords : Solana, pump.fun, bonding curve, coordinated trading, memecoin, market microstructure, sniper bots, on-chain forensics. 2 of 16 

1. Introduction 

Pump.fun is a Solana-based bonding-curve marketplace where 5,000-15,000 new tokens are launched each day (Pump.fun team, 2024-2026). A small fraction of launches “graduate” off the bonding curve to Raydium liquidity once cumulative buys cross the bonding-curve threshold; the rest decay or are abandoned. Outcomes are heavy-tailed: a token’s first thirty minutes of activity largely determine whether it accumulates the buyer momentum necessary to graduate. Information about  which  launches will accumulate that momentum is therefore highly valuable, and conventional market-microstructure tools have not been adapted to the on-chain venue. 

This paper documents an empirical regularity that has not, to our knowledge, been previously characterised in the academic literature: a small but persistent set of wallet  cohorts  - pairs or small groups of addresses that act in concert - systematically appears among the first ten buyers of a disproportionate share of pump.fun launches. The cohorts are persistent (active across many days, not single-launch artifacts), small (median size of 2 wallets), and concentrated at the top of the buyer queue (median average first-buyer rank ≤ 3.55). We refer to such addresses generically as  coordinated sniper cohorts , and we make no normative claim about whether the behaviour constitutes manipulation, arbitrage, market-making, organic skilled-trader networks, or some mixture. The empirical signature is reproducible regardless of motive. 

This paper makes four contributions. First, we propose a two-stage detection pipeline that scales to the full pump.fun corpus and surfaces persistent cohorts directly from raw on-chain buyer events. Second, we publish the cohort catalogue - 1,012 cohorts comprising 2,965 unique addresses - together with the detection script as  RED-COHORT-2026-v1 . Third, we characterise the empirical distribution of cohort size, score, and activity, and document a sharp concentration: 22 cohorts hit ≥20 launches each, while the median cohort hits 5. Fourth, we estimate the marginal effect of cohort presence on bonding-curve buyer flow using a random-matched design with bootstrap confidence intervals, and we document that the +132.3% buyer-count lift (95% CI [+127.0%, +137.4%]) does  not  survive an activity-matched placebo check: per-wallet-activity-matched placebo cohorts yield a substantially LARGER +216.3% lift (95% CI [+183.8%, +255.2%]), with no overlap between the real and placebo confidence intervals. We interpret the empirical association as a launch-quality  selection  effect rather than a coordination-specific causal effect, and we identify the methodological path forward (propensity-score matching on launch-quality covariates). We report both the naive estimate and the activity-matched placebo result to contribute to the literature on causal identification in high-volume on-chain venues, where matched designs are widely used but rarely validated against confounder-controlled placebos. 

The remainder of this paper is organised as follows. Section 2 reviews related work on coordinated trading detection in centralised and decentralised markets. Section 3 describes the data corpus. 2. Related work 

Detection of coordinated trading is a long-standing concern in centralised market surveillance. Classical methods rely on clustering trade-by-trade events using identifiers (trader IDs, account numbers, IP addresses) that are not available on permissionless blockchains. On-chain analyses have substituted address-level signatures: Daian et al.  (2020) document the existence and economic structure of maximal extractable value (MEV) on Ethereum decentralised exchanges, and Werner et al.  (2022) provide a systematisation of DeFi security and analysis methods. Both works characterise  what 

coordinated behaviour exists on chain, but their detection methodologies target single-block adversarial reordering rather than the persistent multi-day wallet-set co-occurrence we study here. 

Zhou et al.  (2020, arXiv:2009.14021) document high-frequency trading patterns on Ethereum DEXes, including measurement of repeated wallet-level activity across pairs. We adopt a similar repeated-actor framing but specialise the detection target to pump.fun’s first-buyer queue. Torres et al.  (CCS 2024) measure MEV across Layer-2 rollups and demonstrate that wallet identity persists across many transactions, supporting the broader empirical premise of this paper that on-chain repeat actors are tractable to detect. 

Related work on the economics of pump.fun by this author includes Kamat (2026a, SSRN abstract 6607301), a methodology paper on counterfactual outcome measurement for rejected trade signals on a Solana DEX, and Kamat (2026b, SSRN abstract 6702198), the RED-2400 dataset of rejected memecoin trading events. The present paper is complementary: it identifies a class of buyer-side features (cohort presence) that the trader-side analyses cannot observe. 

3. Data 

3.1 Corpus 

We construct the corpus from three on-chain data streams collected by the author’s passive Solana observer (a read-only listener; no trading or front-running occurs during collection): 

1.  Buyer events  ( pumpfun_buyers.jsonl  ): 1,578,333 records over the window 2026-06-12 → 2026-06-26 (15 days, 105,222 buyer events per day on average). Each record contains a token mint address, wallet address, slot number, block time, SOL committed, transaction signature, and the buyer’s rank within the launch (1 = first buyer, 2 = second, etc.). 4 of 16 

2.  Launch metadata  ( pumpfun_launches.jsonl  ): 1,315,257 launches with mint address, symbol, name, creation timestamp, initial market capitalisation in SOL, presence indicators for Twitter / website / Telegram, and description length. 

3.  Buyer ranks : derived in collection-time from the buyer-event stream by sorting per-mint events by  blockTime  .

The 1,578,333-row buyer corpus contains 166,098 mints with sufficient first-10-buyer coverage to support cohort detection (≥1 buyer rank in [1, 10] observed per mint). 

3.2 Anonymisation 

All wallet addresses in the corpus are public Solana base-58 strings; no personally identifiable information is present. In the published catalogue and in this paper, individual addresses are referred to by the first four and last four base-58 characters (e.g.,  F6zvmnwC..cxSi  ). Full addresses are available in the published catalogue (see Section 8). 

4. Detection method 

4.1 Stage 1 - Intra-launch first-buyer window 

For each launch  L, we extract the ordered list of the first ten buyers within the bonding-curve window. We retain each buyer’s wallet address, rank, blockTime, and SOL committed. Across the 166,098 qualifying mints, this yields a per-launch index of length at most 10. 

4.2 Stage 2 - Cross-launch persistent-cohort surfacing 

Let  G = (V, E)  be an undirected graph where vertices  V are wallet addresses and edges  E record co-occurrence. We add an edge ( u, v) for each launch  L in which both  u and  v appear among the first ten buyers. Edge weights are the integer count of co-occurrences across all launches in the corpus. 

We filter  E to retain only edges with weight ≥ 3 (the  qualifying pair  threshold). This filter is a defence against accidental co-occurrence: with 166,098 launches and 5-15 first-rank buyers per launch, random pairings would by chance produce many low-weight edges that have no structural meaning. The threshold of 3 is conservative; ablation results are reported in Appendix A. 

We then run union-find on the filtered graph to surface connected components. Each component is a candidate cohort. We score each cohort  C by 

score(C)  = 10 × (Σ_L 1{C touches L}) + 5 / mean_first_rank(C) + √(Σ_L SOL_committed(C, L)) 

and retain cohorts with score ≥  τ. We set  τ to surface only cohorts that simultaneously (a) hit many launches, (b) sit at the top of the buyer queue, and (c) commit non-trivial SOL volume. The resulting catalogue contains 1,012 cohorts. 5 of 16 

4.3 Output format 

Each cohort row records: the cohort identifier  COH-NNNN  , the list of wallet addresses, cohort size (number of wallets), number of distinct launches hit, mean first-buyer rank, total SOL committed across all touched launches, score, first and last observation timestamps, and the per-mint hit detail (mint, first rank within the launch, blockTime, SOL committed). The full catalogue and detection script are released as part of RED-COHORT-2026-v1.

6. Cohort presence and first-30-minute buyer flow 

The cohort catalogue documents  who is coordinated ; the natural next question is  whether cohort presence is informative about downstream behaviour . We address this question with a random-matched design and then test the design’s validity with a placebo check.  The placebo check refutes the strong causal interpretation. 8 of 16 

6.1 Sample construction 

We restrict the universe to mints that simultaneously appear in the launch-metadata file (so we have a launch timestamp) and the buyer-event file (so we observe activity). This yields 166,098 launches. We follow the original analyse-cohorts script (  analyze_sniper_cohorts.py  ) in defining a launch as cohort-touched when at least two cohort wallets appear among the first ten buyers; this stricter ≥2 threshold gives 5,411 cohort-touched mints (vs 8,375 under a looser ≥1-cohort-wallet definition). Of the 5,411 cohort-touched launches, the remaining 160,687 mints with 30-minute metrics are untouched at the ≥2 threshold. We draw a random control sample of 16,233 untouched launches (uniform, without replacement, seed = 42), yielding a 3:1 treated-to-control ratio. 

6.2 Outcomes 

We construct three outcome variables per launch: 

1.  First-30-minute buyer count  - number of distinct buyer events with blockTime within 30 minutes of the launch’s  created_timestamp  .

2.  Total observed buyer count  - total buyer events for the mint across the entire corpus window. 

3.  First-30-minute SOL inflow  - sum of  sol_in  across all first-30-minute buyer events.

Naively interpreted, treated launches receive 2.3× the buyer count and 2.4× the SOL inflow in the first 30 minutes of the control mean. The CIs are tight (the buyer-count CI half-width is ~5pp of point estimate) because n is in the thousands. We caution against the naive interpretation in Section 6.4. 

6.4 Activity-matched placebo refutes the causal interpretation 

The naive interpretation would attribute the +132.3% lift to cohort presence as a cause. We test this with an  activity-matched placebo : for each real cohort of size  k with wallets having individual launch-counts { a₁ , a₂ ,…,  aₖ }, we sample  k placebo wallets from the non-cohort pool, each matched to a corresponding  aᵢ  (launch-count within ±100 launches). This construction controls for the wallet-9 of 16 

activity-frequency confound that biases an uncontrolled uniform-random placebo: it ensures the placebo wallets resemble real-cohort wallets in how often they appear in launches, isolating coordination  per se  from generic frequent-trader behaviour. 

Activity-matched placebo result: +216.3% buyer-count lift  (95% CI [+183.8%, +255.2%]; n_placebo_treated = 173, n_placebo_control = 519).  The real-cohort lift (+132.3%, 95% CI [+127.0%, +137.4%]) lies well below the placebo CI lower bound (+183.8%).  No overlap exists between the two confidence intervals. 

This finding refutes a strong cohort-specific causal interpretation: when we control for per-wallet activity, the activity-matched random groupings produce a  larger  flow lift than real cohorts. The parsimonious interpretation is  selection : both real cohorts and activity-matched placebos disproportionately appear in the buyer queues of launches that attract elevated buyer flow for reasons orthogonal to coordination - for example, launches with more compelling token narratives, stronger social-media signals, or simply launches that occur during high-activity periods. Real-cohort wallets may even be  less  selective than the activity-matched placebo within this pool because they specialise in particular niches. 

We do not, therefore, claim a cohort-specific causal effect on first-30-minute flow from the available data. The naive +132.3% should be read as a marker of  which launches cohorts touch , not of what cohort touch causes. We note for transparency that an earlier uniform-random placebo (not activity-matched) yielded +152.0% - also above real-cohort +132.3%, but with a smaller margin than the cleaner activity-matched design that is now our preferred reference (see Appendix B.1 for both placebos). 

6.5 Other robustness checks 

Top-3 cohort exclusion.  Removing the three highest-score cohorts (COH-0001, COH-0002, COH-0003) leaves 1,009 cohorts touching 5,869 launches. Re-running the lift estimate on this smaller treated set yields +128.8% on first-30-min buyer count and +128.8% on SOL inflow. The naive lift is robust to removing the three highest-activity cohorts - but given the placebo result, this robustness is more accurately interpreted as evidence that the selection effect operates across all cohort tiers rather than being driven by three outliers.

The pattern is  not monotone-increasing in tier : the Premium tier shows a  lower  lift than the Standard tier. This further weakens the “cohort coordination is informative” narrative. If coordination quality were the dominant signal, we would expect Premium > High > Standard. Instead, we see High > Standard > Premium. We interpret this as additional evidence that the lift reflects selection bias of the underlying launches rather than cohort-specific effects. 

6.6 What can be claimed 

A solid empirical claim that survives the robustness checks is: 

Launches in whose first-10-buyer queue at least two wallets from a 1,012-cohort persistent catalogue appear are  associated  with substantially elevated first-30-minute buyer activity (+132.3% lift in buyer count, +136.5% in SOL inflow). However, the same association - with even larger magnitude - appears for activity-matched placebo wallet groupings. The association is therefore not a coordination-specific causal effect; it reflects underlying launch-quality selection that both cohorts and activity-matched placebo wallets respond to. 

This is a methodologically honest finding. Identifying a coordination-specific causal effect requires propensity-score matching on launch-quality covariates (initial market cap, social-media indicators, day-of-week, hour-of-day, dev-history) or a randomised intervention. We leave both to future work and note that the necessary launch-metadata covariates are available in  pumpfun_launches.jsonl  for such a follow-up study. 

7. Discussion 

7.1 What the cohorts plausibly are 

We do not attempt a normative classification of the cohorts. Plausible descriptions include: 

Organised arbitrage groups  running multiple wallets to spread MEV risk and avoid Solana priority-fee penalties on dense launches. 11 of 16 

Bot networks  with shared signal sources (e.g., a Telegram or Discord alerting service) that direct multiple subscriber wallets toward the same target launch. 

Marketing / launch-promotion teams  whose subscriber base provides coordinated initial liquidity. 

Skilled-trader networks  that share alpha via informal channels and converge on the same picks. 

Some mixture of the above. 

Distinguishing among these categories would require off-chain evidence (e.g., Telegram logs) that is not available to academic researchers, and our methods do not attempt it. 

7.2 What the placebo result implies 

The activity-matched placebo check is, the central empirical finding. It shows that the apparent +132.3% real-cohort lift on cohort-touched launches (95% CI [+127.0%, +137.4%]) is  not  acoordination-specific effect: per-wallet-activity-matched random wallet groupings produce asubstantially LARGER lift of +216.3% (95% CI [+183.8%, +255.2%]) with no confidence-interval overlap. We draw three implications: 

1.  The 1,012-cohort catalogue documents a real empirical regularity  (persistent first-buyer co-occurrence above chance). That regularity is established by the detection methodology in Section 4 and the descriptive results in Section 5. Neither claim depends on the causal-flavour interpretation that the placebo refutes. 

2.  The buyer-flow lift is most likely a launch-quality selection effect.  Both real cohorts and random placebo wallets disproportionately appear in the buyer queues of launches that attract elevated buyer flow for reasons orthogonal to coordination. Future work should match treated and control launches on observable launch-quality covariates (initial market cap, social-media indicators, day/hour) before attempting causal claims. 

3.  Tier non-monotonicity is a useful diagnostic.  The Premium tier shows the  lowest  lift of the three tiers, which would be hard to rationalise under a “more coordination → more flow” story. It is consistent with our selection interpretation: Premium cohorts may be more disciplined in their target selection (avoiding the most-popular launches where competition is highest) and therefore inherit less of the selection-driven lift. 

We view this as a methodologically constructive outcome: the placebo refutes the strong claim, but the methodology survives intact, and the path forward (propensity-score matching) is concrete. 

7.3 Implications for market design 

Bonding-curve marketplaces such as pump.fun are widely presumed to be retail-friendly because they remove the venue’s initial-liquidity-provider role. Our results show that even in a permissionless venue, the  first-buyer position  is highly concentrated in repeat actors. 2,965 unique wallets (the union of all 1,012 cohorts) account for the cohort-touched activity on 5,411 launches over 15 days (under the 12 of 16 

≥2-cohort-wallets touch definition used in Section 6). To the extent that early-buyer position confers an information advantage on bonding curves, that advantage appears to be captured disproportionately by a small set of operators. Whether that advantage is causally exploited by coordination (rather than incidental to launch-quality selection) remains an open empirical question. 

7.4 Implications for downstream research 

The cohort catalogue is a directly usable feature for graduation-prediction models, time-series studies of memecoin price formation, and on-chain surveillance research. The published RED-COHORT-2026-v1 release lowers the entry cost for downstream researchers who wish to study coordinated buying as a feature rather than re-implement the detection methodology from raw on-chain data. 

7.5 Limitations 

Three limitations of this study should be noted. 

Outcome label coverage.  The original intention of this paper was to estimate the effect of cohort presence on bonding-curve graduation probability using a matched-pair Cox proportional-hazards model, following the design of Kamat (2026a). However, the graduation-outcome corpus available to us at the time of writing covers only a small fraction of the 5,411 cohort-touched mints in the analysis sample. This coverage is too thin to support a Cox-PH estimate. We document the buyer-flow effect instead, which uses observational data we have in complete coverage. Future work expanding the graduation-outcome corpus to the full pump.fun mint universe would unlock the direct graduation-probability analysis. 

Causal identification.  As noted in Section 6.4, the +132.3% lift (95% CI [+127.0%, +137.4%]) does not survive an activity-matched placebo (+216.3%, 95% CI [+183.8%, +255.2%]). The random-matched design with bootstrap CIs controls for incidental co-occurrence and sampling variability; the activity-matched placebo additionally controls for per-wallet activity-level. Neither addresses launch-quality selection (initial market cap, social-media indicators, day/hour effects). Either propensity-score matching on launch-quality covariates or a randomised intervention is needed to fully separate cohort-specific causal effects from launch-quality selection. 

Single venue.  Our results are specific to pump.fun on Solana. The same detection pipeline can be applied to other bonding-curve venues (pump.fun on Base; Raydium Launchlab; Believe Protocol), but the empirical results - cohort count, size distribution, buyer-flow lift - are not assumed to generalise across venues without separate validation.

Appendix B - Robustness check details (verified) 

B.1 Placebo cohorts - two designs.  We run two placebo designs of increasing rigour to test the causal interpretation of the +132.3% real-cohort lift. Bootstrap 95% CIs (1,000 iterations, percentile method) reported for the primary design. 

Design 1 - uniform-random placebo (initial robustness check).  We construct 1,012 placebo cohorts by sampling wallets uniformly from the buyer-event universe at the same size distribution as the real cohorts. This design does not control for per-wallet activity level: high-activity wallets are over-represented and inherit the universe’s flow-concentration bias. 

Placebo-touched mints (this design uses ≥1 placebo-wallet touch): 21,190 

Control sample (3:1): 63,570 

Placebo treated mean (first 30-min buyers): 19.94 

Placebo control mean: 7.91 

Uniform-random placebo lift: +152.0% 

Design 2 - activity-matched placebo (preferred clean null).  For each real cohort of size  k with wallets having individual launch-counts { a₁ , a₂ ,…,  aₖ }, we sample  k placebo wallets from the non-cohort pool, each individually matched to a corresponding  aᵢ  (launch-count within ±100 launches). Placebo-touched is defined identically to real-cohort-touched (≥2 placebo wallets in first-10 buyer queue), preserving 16 of 16 

comparability of the touch threshold. This is the cleaner null because it controls for the wallet-activity-frequency confound that biased Design 1. 

Activity-matched placebo cohorts built: 1,012 (all real cohorts matched successfully) 

Placebo-touched mints (≥2 placebo wallets): 173 

Control sample (3:1): 519 

Activity-matched placebo buyer-30m lift: +216.3%, 95% CI [+183.8%, +255.2%] 

Activity-matched placebo SOL-30m lift: +157.7%, 95% CI [+117.9%, +207.7%] 

For reference, the real-cohort lifts under Design 2’s matched touch definition and bootstrap procedure are: buyer-30m +132.3% 95% CI [+127.0%, +137.4%] (n_treated = 5,411, n_control = 16,233); SOL-30m +136.5% 95% CI [+120.9%, +152.2%].  The real-cohort point estimate falls well below the activity-matched placebo CI lower bound (+183.8%), with no overlap between the two confidence intervals.  Under either Design 1 or Design 2, the placebo lift exceeds the real-cohort lift; the Design 2 separation is the more methodologically rigorous and substantially wider. We conclude that the +132.3% real-cohort buyer-flow lift is not attributable to coordination  per se  and reflects launch-quality selection that both real and activity-matched placebo wallets respond to. 

B.2 Top-3 cohort exclusion.  Removing the three highest-score cohorts (COH-0001, COH-0002, COH-0003) leaves 1,009 cohorts touching 5,869 launches. 

Treated (any non-top-3 cohort): 5,869 

Control (random untouched, 3:1): 17,607 

First-30-min buyer lift:  +128.8% 

First-30-min SOL inflow lift:  +128.8% 

The naive lift is robust to removing the three highest-activity cohorts. In light of the B.1 placebo result, this robustness shows the selection effect operates across all cohorts, not that the cohort-specific effect is robust.
