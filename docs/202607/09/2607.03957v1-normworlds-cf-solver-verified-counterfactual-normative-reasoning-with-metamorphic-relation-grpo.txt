# ABSTRACT 

Language models can give the right normative verdict for the wrong reason. In legal, policy, and institutional settings, a useful reasoner must know not only which normative label applies, but which rule is decisive, which counterargument is defeated, and which counterfactual edit should change the conclusion. We study this problem in NormWorlds-CF, a solver-verified environment built from executable rule worlds. Its deterministic solver produces final answers, proof and falsification certificates, argument statuses, support sets, and paired-world change labels, so supervision and evaluation do not rely on LLM judges. NormWorlds-CF supports staged SFT diagnostics and a compact paired-world benchmark with 270 root families and 1080 canonical-to-variant pairs. The SFT diagnostics show why final answers are an unsafe proxy: answer-only SFT reaches 1.00 accuracy on original and edited answer tasks but 0.00 on falsification, whereas proof-plus-falsification training with targeted replay reaches 0.99 all-task accuracy. For the harder structured-change task, we introduce metamorphic-relation GRPO (MR-GRPO), a class-conditioned GRPO reward that gives partial credit for relation families and solver-visible change fields. In a matched 1.7B continuation experiment, MR-GRPO improves held-out relation accuracy, relation-family correctness, and wrong-family error over sparse GRPO and answer-only GRPO. In a Qwen3-4B three-seed validation, answer-only reward improves answer-change fields but erodes relation-family structure; sparse reward preserves coarse relation labels best; and MR-GRPO gives the strongest balanced behavior across answer-change, support-change, status-change, and soft root-level MR metrics. These results show that verified counterfactual structure can shape post-training beyond final answers, while exact full change-record generation, invariant subtype recognition, and OOD transfer remain open. 

Keywords Normative reasoning · Counterfactual reasoning · Metamorphic relations · GRPO · Solver-verified supervision 

# 1 Introduction 

Normative reasoning exposes a failure mode that is easy to miss in ordinary post-training evaluation: the model can be right at the surface and wrong in the structure. In legal, institutional, ethical, and policy-like settings, a useful reasoner must track rules, exceptions, missing preconditions, defeated arguments, and counterfactual changes [ 1, 2, 3 , 4 ]. We study this problem as a controlled post-training laboratory rather than as a claim about open-ended legal expertise. A response such as PERMITTED is useful only if the model can identify the decisive rule, explain why a plausible prohibition is defeated, and recognize when a priority edit should change the conclusion. Final-answer accuracy, and therefore final-answer reward, can hide exactly the failures that matter in normative settings. The empirical challenge is not a lack of benchmarks, but a lack of attribution. Recent legal benchmarks and legal-domain language models are essential for measuring external validity [ 5, 4, 6, 7], but realistic legal tasks often entangle legal knowledge, retrieval quality, open-text drafting, and reasoning. Conversely, controlled reasoning benchmarks isolate specific capabilities [ 8 , 9, 10 , 11 ], but rarely expose the conflict, priority, and defeated-argument structure that makes normative reasoning distinctive. This leaves a gap for post-training research: we need environments where normative

Each instance is generated from an executable rule world containing facts, rules, conflicts, priorities, and transformations. Deterministic solvers and metadata builders then produce labels for final answers, proof certificates, falsification certificates, argument statuses, minimal support, and root-level metamorphic relations. This design makes errors inspectable: a wrong output can be separated into answer error, invalid proof, missing falsification, relation-family confusion, schema failure, or counterfactual generalization failure. The study is deliberately staged. We first ask whether a small model can learn solver-generated normative tasks under supervised fine-tuning (SFT). We then compare answer-only, proof-only, proof-plus-falsification, and targeted-replay training to test whether dialectical supervision provides information beyond final-answer labels, consistent with broader evidence for intermediate and verifiable supervision [ 12 , 13 ]. Only after this structured-output substrate is stable do we move to reinforcement learning; otherwise, an RL failure would be difficult to interpret. For reinforcement learning, we formulate a compact root-level paired-world change task. The task is inspired by metamorphic testing: related inputs should obey specified relations under controlled transformations [ 14 , 15 , 16 ]. Each root family contains a canonical world and transformed variants. Some transformations preserve the normative result, while others change support facts, priority relations, argument statuses, or attacks. Rather than asking the model to regenerate two full certificates, we ask it to output a compact, machine-scored change record over relation, answer, support, status, and attack fields. This target is harder than final-answer prediction but more focused than full certificate reconstruction. We compare answer-only reward, sparse GRPO-style exact/schema reward [ 17 , 18 ], and metamorphic-relation GRPO (MR-GRPO), a class-conditioned reward that gives partial credit for the root-level metamorphic structure of interest. In the matched 1.7B continuation experiment, MR-GRPO improves held-out relation accuracy from 0.2417 to 0.3375, relation-family correctness from 0.2917 to 0.3750, and wrong-family error from 0.5750 to 0.4333. In a Qwen3-4B three-seed validation, answer-only GRPO improves answer-change accuracy over sparse GRPO, but substantially degrades relation and relation-family behavior. MR-GRPO preserves more of that structural signal while improving answer-change, support-change, and status-change fields and soft root-level MR consistency. The resulting tradeoff is itself a finding: reward decomposition can improve structural fields that sparse exact reward underspecifies, but dense MR-aware credit does not automatically dominate on every relation-label metric. 

Contributions. This paper makes three contributions: 1. We introduce NormWorlds-CF, a solver-verified environment and benchmark suite for counterfactual normative reasoning, with executable rule worlds, deterministic certificates, compact paired-world change records, family-level splits, and OOD structural probes. 2. We show through staged SFT diagnostics that final-answer supervision is insufficient: proof and falsification targets expose different capabilities, and targeted replay repairs concrete boundary failures under deterministic scoring. 3. We formulate compact root-level structured-change prediction as a reward-ready post-training task and introduce MR-GRPO, a class-conditioned GRPO reward. Matched answer-only, sparse, and MR-aware GRPO experiments show that answer reward, exact/schema reward, and MR-aware reward induce different tradeoffs, with MR-GRPO improving relation-aware structure while exposing clear limits in coarse relation labeling, exact change-record generation, invariant subtype recognition, and OOD transfer. The organizing claim is therefore broader than “MR-GRPO beats a baseline.” The paper argues that post-training normative reasoners should be trained and evaluated through solver-verified structure at three levels: supervision targets, counterfactual evaluation tasks, and reward design. 

Scope. The results below should be read as a controlled post-training study, not as a broad claim about legal or ethical expertise. NormWorlds-CF isolates normative structure in executable worlds so that answer, proof, falsification, argument, and counterfactual labels are auditable. This makes it a diagnostic bridge toward legal, policy, and compliance settings, where external-domain transfer, natural-language ambiguity, and institutional context remain open. # 2 NormWorlds-CF 

NormWorlds-CF separates three objects that are often conflated in natural-language reasoning benchmarks: a symbolic world, a textual rendering, and the supervision target. The symbolic world is the source of truth. The textual prompt is only a readable rendering of that world. The labels are produced by deterministic symbolic procedures over the world, not by an LLM judge. This section defines the rule-world interface, explains how the solver produces certificates, and shows how the same worlds are converted into a staged task suite. 

2.1 Formal Normative Worlds 

A world in NormWorlds-CF is a finite executable rule program, not a free-form legal narrative. It contains ground facts, instantiated rules, optional priorities, and a query. Templates may be used during data generation, but the solver receives only ground rule objects: each rule has an identifier, a kind, an action, and a finite set of preconditions. Identity rules derive additional facts by forward chaining. Normative rules whose preconditions hold become candidate arguments for the queried action. Formally, we write a world as w = ( F, R, P, q ), where F is a finite set of ground facts, R is a finite set of instantiated typed rules, P ⊆ R×R is a priority relation, and q is the queried action or normative target. A priority edge (ri, r j ) ∈ P

means that rule ri overrides rule rj when their conclusions conflict. A deterministic solver S maps w to an answer and certificate: 

S(w) = ( a, C ), (1) where a is the final normative answer and C contains certificate fields such as decisive rules, defeated alternatives, argu-ment statuses, and minimal support. If no applicable normative rule supports the query, the answer is NO_CONCLUSION . If incompatible undefeated arguments remain, the answer is CONFLICT_UNRESOLVED . Otherwise, the answer is read from the surviving normative labels using a fixed reporting convention: OBLIGATED before FORBIDDEN before PERMITTED .This order is only a deterministic serialization convention for cases with multiple compatible surviving labels; it is not a claim about legal or ethical priority. Transformations are executable edits to a root world. They may preserve the normative result, add irrelevant distractors, remove support facts, or change priority relations. For a transformation τ applied to root world w, the pair (w, τ (w))

r2 > r1 , r2 defeats r1 ; the answer is PERMITTED , the decisive rule is r2 , the defeated rule is r1 , and the minimal support includes emergency_rescue(alice) plus the priority edge. If the priority edge is removed, the same facts yield CONFLICT_UNRESOLVED . If the emergency fact is removed, only r1 applies and the answer becomes FORBIDDEN .If an irrelevant fact is added, the answer and certificate should remain invariant. These controlled edits are the basis for root-level metamorphic relations. 

2.2 Solver-Generated Labels 

The solver is best understood as a deterministic symbolic engine for this DSL. It does not parse statutes, infer hidden legal principles, or decide open-ended natural-language cases. It operates under a closed interface in which facts, rule preconditions, actions, labels, and priorities are explicit strings in a fixed schema. This narrow interface is the reason labels can be generated without an LLM. Label construction follows four deterministic steps. First, identity rules are forward-chained to a fixed point, producing the derived fact set. Second, the solver selects applicable normative rules whose action matches the query and whose preconditions are contained in that fact set. Each applicable normative rule becomes one candidate argument for its labeled conclusion. Third, the solver compares candidate arguments pairwise. Two arguments attack only when their labels are incompatible: permission versus prohibition, or obligation versus prohibition. Fourth, priorities are applied by graph reachability. If one rule can reach another through the directed priority graph, the lower-priority rule is defeated; if neither side has a one-way priority advantage, the conflict remains unresolved. The certificate is extracted from the same trace. Accepted arguments are decisive rules whose labels match the final answer. Defeated arguments are rules defeated by priority. Undecided arguments are rules that participate in unresolved conflicts. Supporting facts are the preconditions of decisive rules that appear in the derived fact set. Minimal support is computed by searching subsets of decisive rules and retaining those sufficient to support the answer while defeating conflicting alternatives through the priority graph. The same trace also supports falsification and metamorphic labels. For falsification, a candidate answer or certificate is checked against the trace and assigned a deterministic error type, such as missing precondition, defeated rule used as decisive evidence, unresolved conflict ignored, or incorrect priority claim. For metamorphic pairs, the system applies a named transformation to the root world, solves both worlds, and computes answer, support, status, and attack changes by set differencing their certificates. The natural-language prompt is only a rendering of the symbolic record; it is not the source of ground truth. Each solved case therefore has three layers of supervision. The decision layer contains the final answer: PERMITTED ,

FORBIDDEN , OBLIGATED , NO_CONCLUSION , or CONFLICT_UNRESOLVED . The certificate layer contains decisive rules, defeated rules, supporting facts, falsification targets, argument-node statuses, and minimal support. The counterfactual layer is defined for paired worlds and contains the root-level relation plus structured answer, support, status, and attack change fields. These layers expose different failure modes. In the archive example, predicting FORBIDDEN is an answer error. Predicting PERMITTED while citing r1 as decisive is a proof error. Failing to reject the candidate FORBIDDEN answer is a falsification error. Claiming that removing the priority edge changes nothing is a counterfactual error. Each interface uses the same underlying world and solver machinery, but asks for a different slice of the record. # 3 Dataset Construction 

The dataset is constructed as a diagnostic ladder rather than as a single pool of examples. Each stage adds one source of difficulty while keeping labels solver-generated: early micro-worlds establish the output interfaces, surface-varied roots test whether the model survives prompt variation, root-level families test paired counterfactual structure, and compact change records turn those paired worlds into reward-ready post-training examples. This design lets us localize failure: final-answer prediction, certificate construction, falsification, relation-family recognition, fine-grained change description, or transfer to unseen structural families. Examples used for optimization are separated from held-out evidence throughout the paper. Dev denotes a small diagnostic split used for error analysis during development. ID test means an in-distribution held-out test split drawn from the same generator family types as training but from disjoint roots. OOD test means a held-out split with structural family types that are not present in training. Whenever examples are derived from a common root world, we split by root or root family so that variants of the same underlying world do not cross split boundaries.

3.3 Root-Level MR Families 

The micro-world stages still evaluate mostly single worlds. The main paper question, however, is counterfactual: what should change, and what should stay fixed, when the rule world is transformed? To study this, we construct root-level metamorphic-relation families. Each family starts from one canonical formal world. The canonical world is an anchor, not a scored relation by itself. The supervised MR examples are the four pairs obtained by comparing that canonical world with four transformed variants. The four variants play different diagnostic roles. A surface rewrite changes only rendering choices, such as order, names, or rule identifiers, and should receive the surface_invariant label. A support-fact edit, instantiated in the generator as the fact_ablation variant, removes or adds a decisive precondition/support fact and is scored as 

support_delta . A priority perturbation changes priority edges and is scored as priority_delta . A distractor expansion adds irrelevant facts or rules and is scored as distractor_invariant . The split is by root family, so no canonical world or transformed variant crosses train, dev, ID-test, or OOD-test boundaries. The OOD split uses held-out structural family types, such as priority-chain and distractor-conflict variants, rather than new roots from the same type mix. 3.4 Compact Paired-World Change Records 

The root-family construction produces rich paired labels, but the main RL experiments do not ask the model to regenerate two full certificates. In pilot prompt variants, that target proved too noisy: it entangles solving each world, serializing two nested certificates, and comparing them. A failed output could reflect reasoning failure, formatting failure, or simply excessive sequence length. We therefore introduce a compact paired-world change record. Instead of asking for both certificates, the model outputs the relation label and the structured differences between the canonical world and the transformed world. # 4 Post-Training Method 

The previous sections define the executable worlds and compact paired-world change targets. The post-training question is how to use those targets without reducing the task back to final-answer prediction. We use a staged pipeline. Supervised fine-tuning first establishes that the model can emit solver-aligned structured outputs at all; reinforcement learning is then applied only to the compact change-record task, where the main comparison is among answer-only reward, sparse exact/schema reward, and MR-aware reward. This section describes the SFT substrate, the GRPO objective, and the controlled reward comparison. The diagnostic SFT and RL outcomes are reported together in Section 5. We use LoRA adapters for parameter-efficient continuation rather than full-model fine-tuning [ 19 ]. In both SFT and GRPO stages, matched branches are trained as adapters on the same base model and share the same prompt template, data substrate, and update budget within each comparison; the intended experimental variable remains the supervision or reward design. 

4.1 SFT as Structured-Output Substrate 

The SFT stage is part of the method rather than merely an initialization detail. Normative reasoning in NormWorlds-CF requires several output interfaces: final answers, proof certificates, falsification certificates, argument-status records, and compact change-record objects. If a model cannot learn these interfaces under direct supervision, an RL failure would be ambiguous: it could reflect weak reward design, weak reasoning, or simply inability to serialize the expected structure. We therefore design SFT as a sequence of interface tests rather than as a single mixed warmup. The answer-only stage teaches original-world and edited-world answer fields. The proof-only stage keeps those answer anchors but adds decisive rules, defeated rules, priorities, and supporting facts. The falsification-only stage again keeps the answer anchors but asks the model to check a proposed answer or certificate and, when it is invalid, identify the error type, wrong rule, counter-rule, and corrected answer. The proof-plus-falsification stage combines the two sides of dialectical supervision: explaining the winning argument and rejecting a plausible losing one. The targeted-replay step is deliberately conservative. It does not introduce a new task family; it replays the small failure families exposed by error analysis, such as unresolved-versus-priority-resolved conflicts, identity-derived support facts, and sibling counter-rule confusions. Finally, the root-level case warmup transfers the same principle to the harder MR family distribution by teaching argument-certificate outputs before RL asks for compact pairwise change records. This division keeps Section 5 interpretable: SFT results diagnose supervision and interface design, while the matched GRPO comparisons diagnose reward design.

# 5 Experiments 

The experiments answer four questions in order. First, does final-answer supervision suffice for solver-generated normative structure? Second, under a matched 1.7B continuation setup, how do answer-only, sparse, and MR-aware rewards compare? Third, does the reward-design lesson survive a stronger Qwen3-4B initialization, where exact change fields are more expressible? Fourth, which failures remain after reward shaping? 

5.1 Protocol and Metrics 

All reported metrics are computed by deterministic parsers and scorers. A model output is first parsed into the expected answer, certificate, or compact change-record object; normalized fields are then compared to solver-generated gold labels. No LLM judge is used for scoring, error slicing, or significance testing. For compact change records, we score relation prediction at two levels. Relation accuracy is the strict four-way accuracy over surface_invariant , distractor_invariant , support_delta , and priority_delta . Relation-family correctness asks a coarser question: did the model at least distinguish invariant transformations, { surface_invariant ,

distractor_invariant }, from substantive change transformations, { support_delta , priority_delta }? This hierarchy separates two errors that matter differently. Predicting priority_delta for a gold support_delta case confuses the mechanism but still recognizes that the transformed world should change; predicting an invariant label for that same case misses the change family altogether. We report the latter boundary-crossing rate as wrong-family error .10 NormWorlds-CF A P REPRINT 

We then score the contents of the change record. Answer, support, status, and attack change fields are exact 0/1 metrics after normalization. All changes exact requires all four change fields to match the solver target, but intentionally excludes the relation label; full all-fields correctness additionally requires the relation label to be exact. This distinction lets us tell whether a model understands what changed even when it chooses the wrong subtype label. Class-conditioned MR, root-MR soft, and change-presence are continuous diagnostics that give partial credit for structurally useful behavior, such as correct invariant emptiness or correct changed-answer presence. Paired comparisons align predictions by example id. Exact 0/1 rows, including relation, relation-family, wrong-family, and exact change-field metrics, use exact McNemar tests. Continuous soft scores use bootstrap confidence intervals. In result tables, ↑ and ↓ mark the preferred direction, and bold marks the better value for each row. When a table reports between-method differences, the caption defines the baseline and negative values mean that the baseline is better. We reserve the term delta for implementation field names and relation labels such as support_delta ; prose uses 

change to distinguish paired-world structure from method-to-method gains. SFT diagnostic tables are left unbolded because they show an ablation pattern rather than a single winner, and purely descriptive diagnostics such as length and clip ratio are not bolded. We report the metrics that support each evidence claim and treat near-floor or non-discriminative fields as limitations rather than foreground table rows. The evidence hierarchy is deliberate: the 1.7B branch primarily tests relation-family learning under a tightly matched reward comparison, while the 4B branch tests whether MR-aware reward improves answer-change, support-change, and status-change fields once structured expression is less of a bottleneck. OOD results are reported as boundary evidence, not as a main success claim.

# 6 Discussion and Future Work 

The experiments support a focused claim: verified counterfactual structure can be used as a reward target, but the choice of structure matters. Answer-only reward demonstrates that a capable model can improve local outcome fields when they are directly rewarded. Sparse exact reward demonstrates that coarse labels and schema validity can be preserved under a demanding exact-match objective. MR-GRPO occupies the middle ground that this task requires: it exposes relation-family and solver-visible change fields to the policy update, yielding the strongest balanced behavior rather than universal dominance. 13 NormWorlds-CF A P REPRINT 

The main tradeoff is relation exactness versus field-level semantic repair. Sparse GRPO remains strongest on exact relation and relation-family labels in the 4B validation, while answer-only reward can improve answer-change and all-changes exactness at the cost of relation-family structure. This is why compact change records should not be evaluated by any single row. A model that gets the answer field right can still misunderstand what kind of transformation occurred; a model that gets the relation label right can still serialize a wrong counterfactual change. Future reward design should therefore treat coarse relation decisions, answer-change correctness, and change-record fidelity as separable objectives, for example through curricula that first stabilize relation labels and then increase pressure on field-level changes. The boundary cases are also clear. NormWorlds-CF is synthetic by design, which enables deterministic supervision but limits direct external validity. Exact full change-record generation remains difficult, especially for nested support, status, and attack changes. Invariant subtype recognition remains weak, because models often recognize that no substantive change occurred while confusing surface_invariant with distractor_invariant . OOD family transfer is not solved. These limitations point to three natural next steps: constrained or verifier-assisted decoding for serialization, contrastive training for invariant subtypes, and broader structural-family coverage for OOD transfer. 

# 7 Related Work 

Synthetic and solver-verified reasoning environments. Synthetic reasoning benchmarks have long been used to isolate compositional and symbolic capabilities, from question-answering toy tasks to visual reasoning and natural-language rule reasoning benchmarks [ 8, 9, 10 , 11 ]. NormWorlds-CF follows this tradition but makes two different design choices. First, the domain is explicitly normative: rules produce permissions, prohibitions, obligations, unresolved conflicts, and defeated arguments. Second, every label is generated by executable solvers and metadata builders, including proof, falsification, argument status, minimal support, and metamorphic relation labels. This lets the paper evaluate post-training behavior without LLM judges. 

Normative, defeasible, and legal reasoning. Normative reasoning is related to deontic logic, defeasible logic, and legal argumentation, where the central difficulty is not only what follows, but why it follows under rules, exceptions, priorities, conflicts, and defeated arguments [ 1, 2, 22 , 3]. Natural legal benchmarks are important for external validity [ 4], but they often mix legal knowledge, language understanding, retrieval, and reasoning. NormWorlds-CF instead uses controlled rule worlds to isolate counterfactual normative structure. The intended role is complementary: a solver-verified laboratory for post-training methods before moving to open-text legal or policy settings. 

Legal benchmarks and legal LLMs. Recent legal NLP has produced broad evaluation suites for legal understanding, reasoning, and retrieval-grounded generation, including LexGLUE, LegalBench, LawBench, and LegalBench-RAG [ 5, 4, 6, 23 ]. Legal-domain LLMs and assistants such as SaulLM, DISC-LawLLM, ChatLaw, and InternLM-Law study continued pretraining, instruction tuning, retrieval integration, and legal-service workflows [ 7, 24 , 25 , 26 ]. These works motivate the external-validity direction for NormWorlds-CF, but they answer a different question: whether models perform on realistic legal texts, legal knowledge, and legal-service tasks. We instead ask whether post-training can teach small models to follow solver-verified normative structure under controlled transformations. This distinction matters because open-text legal settings make it hard to separate domain knowledge, retrieval quality, drafting skill, and rule-world reasoning. 

Process supervision and verifiable rewards. The SFT portion of the paper is aligned with work showing that intermediate structure and verifier signals can matter beyond final-answer supervision [ 12 , 13 ]. NormWorlds-CF extends that idea from mathematical answers to normative certificates: proof fields, falsification fields, argument statuses, and paired-world change records are all deterministic supervision targets. The RL portion connects to reinforcement learning from feedback, PPO-style policy optimization, and verifiable rewards [ 27 , 28 , 29 , 20 , 17 , 18 ]. Our GRPO comparisons include answer-only reward, sparse exact/schema reward, and MR-GRPO, isolating how reward decomposition changes root-level counterfactual behavior. 

Metamorphic testing and counterfactual consistency. Metamorphic testing evaluates whether related inputs obey expected relations when oracle labels are difficult or expensive [ 14 , 15 , 16 ]. NormWorlds-CF adapts this idea to post-training rather than only testing: each root family specifies transformations that should preserve or change normative behavior, and MR-GRPO uses those relations as reward structure. This differs from generic data augmentation because the target is not invariance alone; priority and support perturbations are supposed to change answers and certificates in precise ways. 14 NormWorlds-CF A P REPRINT 

Counterfactual and contrastive data. Counterfactually augmented data and contrast sets use minimally edited examples to expose spurious correlations and local decision-boundary failures [ 30 , 31 ]. NormWorlds-CF shares the commitment to paired examples, but differs in the source and use of the pairs. The transformations are generated from executable rule-world families rather than human edits, the labels include structured certificates rather than only class labels, and the pairs are used both for evaluation and as decomposed RL reward targets. This is why the main question is not simply whether a model changes its answer under a perturbation, but whether the answer, support, status, attack, and relation fields change in the solver-specified way. 

Structured output generation. Structured reasoning tasks often fail for two separable reasons: the model may not know the right structure, or it may know the structure but fail to serialize it exactly. Constrained decoding methods such as PICARD show that parsing constraints can improve structured generation by rejecting invalid continuations [ 32 ]. Our experiments deliberately evaluate unconstrained generation to expose the post-training signal, but the persistent strict change-record and schema failures suggest that future NormWorlds-CF systems should combine MR-aware reward with constrained or verifier-assisted decoding. 

# 8 Conclusion 

We introduced NormWorlds-CF, a solver-verified environment for counterfactual normative reasoning. Executable rule worlds generate answers, proof and falsification certificates, argument metadata, support sets, and metamorphic relations without LLM judges, making both supervision and evaluation auditable. The staged SFT diagnostics show that final-answer supervision is insufficient for dialectical structure: proof, falsification, and targeted replay supervision are needed before compact counterfactual training is interpretable. On the harder compact change-record task, MR-GRPO improves ID relation-family behavior in a controlled 1.7B setup. The Qwen3-4B validation then makes the reward tradeoff visible: answer-only reward improves local answer-change fields but can lose paired-world relation structure, sparse reward preserves coarse relation labels, and MR-aware reward gives the strongest balanced structural behavior across answer-change, support-change, status-change, and soft root-level MR consistency. The training diagnostics support the same interpretation: MR-aware reward supplies denser within-group ranking signal while retaining more relation-aware structure than answer-only reward. The contribution is not a complete normative reasoner. Exact full change-record generation, invariant subtype recogni-tion, and OOD transfer remain open. The result is instead a controlled step toward post-training models on verified normative structure rather than final answers alone.
