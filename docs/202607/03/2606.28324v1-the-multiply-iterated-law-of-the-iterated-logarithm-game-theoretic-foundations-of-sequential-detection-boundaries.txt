# The multiply iterated law of the iterated logarithm: game-theoretic foundations of sequential detection boundaries 

## Akshay Balsubramani 

abalsubr@stanford.edu 

Abstract 

Anytime-valid confidence sequences and e-processes are built almost universally from one recipe: average exponen-tial test statistics over a prior on the tilting scale, then invoke Ville’s inequality on the resulting nonnegative supermartin-gale. The mixing prior sets the width of the detection boundary and is usually chosen by hand. We recast the recipe as a two-player game with information as currency. A Learner commits to the prior; Nature adaptively produces a mean-zero score process whose difficulty is priced by a cumulant-generating-function charge. The Learner’s mixture wealth is governed by a single pathwise Gibbs-variational identity,

which holds along every realized path with no expectation operator; Ville’s inequality, the equalizer condition, the GROW characterization, and the saddlepoint formula are all specializations of it. Three messages organize the rest. First, the law of the iterated logarithm (LIL) is the minimax boundary of this sequential-detection game, not arbitrary combinatorial slack. Second, the optimal prior is not a design choice but the forced equalizer strategy — the unique law that makes every boundary-crossing time equally costly for Nature — and it yields the sharp first iterated-log correction in closed form, with coefficient 3/2 = 1 + 1 /2 (one for the Erdős baseline, one half for the Laplace envelope around the saddle). Third, in the log-log scale chart the equalizer is exactly the Jeffreys prior on the scale-of-scales. The Erdős–Kolmogorov integral test is the criterion that selects it: the unique normalizability boundary at which the equalizer’s normalization integral is marginally divergent. The two-stage finite-time LIL proof, the Howard–Ramdas mixture and stitching constructions, and betting confidence sequences all read as instances of this equalizer principle. A companion empirical evaluation confirms the central identities and locates the Erdős threshold at the predicted value.

# 1 Introduction 

Sequential tests that remain valid at every stopping time — the anytime-valid confidence sequences and e-processes that drive modern nonparametric inference [13, 24, 33] — almost always rest on a single construction. Take an exponential test statistic at scale η, M ηt = exp( ηS t − Kt(η)) where Kt is the cumulant generating function of the score increments. Average it over a prior Π on η. The mixture Zt = ∫ M ηt Π( d η) is a nonnegative supermartingale under any mean-zero alternative satisfying the CGF bound, and Ville’s inequality [27, 31] converts it into a time-uniform detection rule: 

P(∃t : Zt ≥ 1/α ) ≤ α.The width of the resulting boundary is set by the prior. A heavy prior at small scales protects against late, slowly drifting alternatives but admits early false positives; a tight concentration at moderate scales tracks the central-limit regime closely but loses power on long horizons. The prior is usually treated as a design choice; the resulting boundary is read off and compared against classical reference lines. The reference line for what is achievable in this regime is the law of the iterated logarithm. For a fair coin flipped N

times the cumulative deviation Sn = ξ1+· · · +ξn wanders past √n infinitely often, but lim sup n→∞ Sn/√2n log log n =1 almost surely [11, 16, 17]. The slow log log n and the constant √2 together mark the exact boundary at which the su-permartingale construction succeeds. Below this rate (e.g. √2t log log t without correction) no choice of Π admits a normalizable equalizer; above it (e.g. adding (3 /2 + ε) log log log t inside the radical) the prior is finite-mass and the detection rule closes the boundary. This paper recasts the construction as a game. The Learner commits to a prior Π before any data arrive; Nature adaptively produces the score-increment distribution at each round, subject to a CGF budget. The prior is the Learner’s mixed strategy. The equalizer prior — the unique Π that makes every boundary-crossing time equally costly for Nature — is determined by a saddlepoint relation, and its normalizability is governed by the classical Erdős–Kolmogorov integral test. The iterated logarithm is not arbitrary combinatorial slack: it is the unique function that makes a normalization integral marginally divergent along the equalizer’s saddle. The paper contributes along several complementary axes.

# 2 Related work 

Anytime-valid testing and e-processes. The unified theory of time-uniform confidence sequences and e-processes [13]; the betting-based estimator framework [33]; and the game-theoretic-statistics survey [24] make the mixture-martingale mechanism explicit, positioning the oblivious-prior construction at the center of the wider safe anytime-valid inference program initiated in [27]. The GROW-optimal e-process framework [10] supplies the information-theoretic counterpart to the saddle-point view we use here: its growth-rate-optimal prior coincides with our equalizer prior under the scale-family alternative (Theorem 14.1). The unification of Robbins-style mixture martingales with adaptive “stitching” [13], and the self-normalized counterpart that adapts to the empirical variance [6], complete the modern picture. The contribu-tion of the present paper to this line is to identify the specific mixing prior as the unique minimax equalizer of a reduced scale-allocation game, and to read the Erdős integral test as its normalizability criterion. 

Recent betting CSes and safe testing. A companion survey [12] catalogues the mixture-and-stitching constructions whose equalizer reading we exploit. The Catoni-style robust extension of [32] carries this to confidence-sequence form for heavy-tailed data; the betting-CS view of that construction is the closest adaptive analogue of the present oblivious framework outside the sub-Gaussian regime. The reduction of sequential change-point detection to sequential estima-tion [29] and the systematic e-value combination framework [4] both exploit mixture martingales whose mixing priors are LIL-rate by design, the regime the equalizer characterizes. The safe-testing program [10] reframes classical hypothesis 6testing in terms of e-values that compose with these mixture martingales; the likelihood-and-replicability tradition [23] places such constructions in their older statistical context. 

Mixture martingales and sequential testing. The mixture-martingale construction of [15] revisits mixture martin-gales for best-arm identification and sequential tests; the implicit mixing prior there is a discrete-shell approximation of the continuum equalizer (cf. Theorem 7.1). An earlier line [2] uses the finite-time LIL directly as a sequential nonpara-metric test; the oblivious mixture there is the symmetric Balsubramani density of Corollary 6.4. Martingale methods for sequential estimation of functionals and divergences [21] admit the same equalizer interpretation when their log-optimal mixture is specialized to the LIL boundary. 

Parameter-free online learning and universal portfolios. Coin-betting and parameter-free online learners [14, 22] achieve regret bounds at the LIL rate without hyperparameters; their potentials are instances of the adaptive wealth pro-cess, and the relevant rescaling of the Jeffreys prior is the same closest-to-Haar correction we identify in Section 19.5. 

Small-sample evaluation in LLM benchmarks. Two recent applied lines test the LIL-rate regime in settings where the data is precisely small. [3] document that CLT-calibrated confidence intervals on small LLM-eval benchmarks dra-matically underestimate uncertainty, recommending non-asymptotic frequentist or Bayesian alternatives; the LIL upper-confidence band derived here is exactly the non-asymptotic envelope they call for, and the equalizer characterization gives the sharp constant the LIL band charges. [20] build an applied SPRT-style adaptive stopping rule on chain-of-thought self-consistency that operates in the same small-n regime; their realized stopping time is the empirical face of the equalizer’s normalizability criterion, with the savings curves they report matching the LIL-rate bound at the relevant n. Both papers are concrete deployment evidence that the LIL boundary, rather than the CLT, is the operationally meaningful envelope for sequential LLM-evaluation. 

Classical roots in random-walk LIL theory. The mixture-of-exponentials construction itself goes back to the boundary-crossing literature [5, 19, 25]; the Hartman–Wintner law [11] and the upper–lower-class test [8] established the upper-class characterization of LIL-rate boundaries, which an integral test [7] sharpened. The Komlós–Major–Tusnády strong ap-proximation [18], together with its martingale extensions [26, 28], lifts these results from i.i.d. walks to martingales with controlled increments. The finite-time martingale LIL [1] is the proof we re-read game-theoretically. 

The CGF-constrained game shadow. The LIL game studied here is the exponentiated null-restricted shadow of a CGF-constrained repeated game over KL balls. The Learner-side adaptive protocol of Section 19 draws on the intrinsic-time / Bayesian-update connection developed in that broader framework. 

What is new here. Our equalizer characterization is incremental relative to these works in that the Balsubramani density has been widely used and, implicitly, understood to be minimax-like. ## 4.3 What “equalizer” means here, and why it forces a unique prior 

The intuition. The Learner has fixed a prior Π once and for all; she now has to pay for whatever Nature does. Nature’s best move, given the Learner’s commitment, is to wait until a time t at which the boundary b(t) is least well defended by Π

10 — specifically, until a time when the integrand exp( ηb (t) − η2t/ 2) , viewed as a function of η, is sharply peaked at a value of η that lies in a region where Π puts little mass. By choosing such a t, Nature can force a crossing without paying its expected CGF charge. The Learner’s defense against this is the equalizer : choose Π so that the integrand peaks at every time t produce the 

same mixture wealth Zt|St=b(t). With every crossing time equally costly to Nature, no waiting move is strictly preferred. The min-max strategy reduces to choosing the prior that exactly equalizes Zt along the boundary, and that constraint turns out to determine the prior uniquely (up to normalization): the Laplace approximation in Section 4.4 below shows that the equalizer condition is one equation per time t, and inverting it gives the density at the saddlepoint η⋆(t).

# 6 The exact continuum scale-allocation game 

We now define the continuum game whose solution is exactly Balsubramani’s prior. 14 6.1 The one-sided game 

Fix the interval I := (0 , e −2]. The Learner chooses a probability measure ν on I. Nature chooses a target scale λ ∈ I.The Learner’s payoff is

Here Fν (λ) is the cumulative prior mass below λ, and log(1 /λ ) is the scale tax — the information-theoretic cost of operating at scale λ, inherited from the exponential tilting that defines the wealth process. Hence: • The equalizer ν⋆ is the pointwise-smallest CDF among all minimax laws (every other minimax law puts strictly more mass below every scale λ). • All minimax laws share the same tail behavior: Fν (λ) ≥ 2/ log(1 /λ ) for every λ, so they all allocate at least 

2/ log(1 /λ ) of their mass below scale λ.• The equalizer is the unique minimax law that makes Nature indifferent across all pure strategies. ## 19.5 Scale-invariance and the Jeffreys prior 

The near-minimax status of the Jeffreys-like law Π( d η) ∝ η−1 dη has a natural explanation. The family {M ηt : η > 0}

is a scale family: rescaling η 7 → cη and St 7 → St/c leaves ηS t invariant, with η2Kt acquiring only the quadratic rescaling. The right Haar measure of the multiplicative group on (0 , ∞) is dη/η , which coincides with the Jeffreys prior for the loca-tion parameter log η in the Gaussian approximation ηS t − η2Kt ≈ − Kt(η − η⋆)2 + const . The Jeffreys prior has infinite total mass on (0 , ∞), and is therefore not itself a valid oblivious strategy. The Balsubramani density 1/(η(log(1 /η )) 2)

33 is the closest normalizable distribution: it equals dη/η weighted by 1/ log 2(1 /η ), the gentlest correction that yields fi-nite total mass. This is why this particular density appears universally in LIL constructions: it is the unique normalizable rescaling of the scale-invariant Haar measure, and therefore hedges most uniformly against Nature’s choice of when and how to produce difficulty. The iterated-log-chart reading of Theorem 13.2 makes this canonicity even sharper: the equal-izer is the rate-1 shifted exponential in the second-level Jeffreys chart μ2 = log log(1 /η ), the unique law respecting the scale-of-scale group action up to a normalizable correction. “Closest normalizable” is a distinct property from “smallest finite-time constant.” The Orabona–Jun universal-portfolio boundary [22] uses a log 3/2 rescaling of the same Jeffreys prior, supplemented by a regret-bound construction; the log 3/2-rescaled Jeffreys prior is itself non-normalizable, so it does not contradict the present uniqueness statement, but the resulting closed-form anytime-valid boundary is in fact 

tighter at every finite t tested (see Section 21.2; the asymptotic ratio bBals (t)/b OJ (t) approaches 1.083 ). ## 21.7 Real-data anytime-valid CS: Balsubramani vs. Howard-Ramdas vs. WSR

(ii) The finite-dimensional coincidence-center theorem (Theorem 5.1). (iii) The one-sided continuum scale-allocation theorem (Theorem 6.1), with value V = 2 .(iv) The signed symmetric corollary (Corollary 6.4), recovering Balsubramani’s density exactly. (iii) The 3/2 threshold (Corollary 12.1). (iv) The 3/2 threshold extended to the Bernstein sub-exponential family (Corollary 12.4). ## 22.3 What remains open 

The statement not proved here is that Balsubramani’s law is the exact minimax strategy for the full repeated martingale betting game over all pathwise adversaries. What is proved is that the law is the exact minimax solution of the reduced 

scale-allocation game that the method-of-mixtures proof actually exposes. Whether one can formulate a larger repeated game whose exact minimax strategy is literally the same law, without first reducing to the scale-allocation subproblem, remains open.

## 22.5 Open directions 

Several follow-ups extend the present framework. (O1) Adaptive equalizers. Derive the Learner’s adaptive scale ηt that minimizes a pathwise regret-in-detection, as op-posed to the oblivious equalizer Π. The exact one-round identity in Theorem 17.1, instantiated to the LIL boundary, should produce the Howard–Ramdas stitched strategy up to second order. (O2) Higher-order Erdős corrections (resolved). Beyond the 3/2 leading constant, Corollary 12.1 allows for an O(1) 

additive term in h at the first iterated-log layer. The sharp constants at every deeper iterated-log layer are now pinned down by Theorem 12.6: the hierarchy is (3 /2, 1, 1, . . . ), with the Laplace half-Gaussian “ +1 /2” contributing exactly once at the first layer. What remains genuinely open is the exact subleading O(1) constant inside h at the first layer, matching the refined upper–lower-class form [8]; this is a finite-time improvement, not an asymptotic-rate question. (O3) Beyond sub-exponential: Pareto-tailed regimes. Corollary 12.4 extends the 3/2 threshold to the Bernstein sub-exponential family. The remaining open regime is the genuinely heavy-tailed one, where the saddlepoint correction 

(1 − cη ⋆)−3/2 is replaced by a factor that does not asymptote to 1 along the LIL boundary – e.g. Pareto-tailed incre-ments with finite second moment but infinite exponential moment. The game-theoretic formulation continues to apply, but the equalizer density and the Erdős threshold may both shift. An explicit calculation for a representative Pareto-tail family would identify the precise modification. (O4) Multidimensional extensions (verified-in-low-dimension heuristic). Replace the scalar scale η by a vector parameter η ∈ Rd (matrix concentration). Matrix LIL boundaries [13] suggest that the equalizer prior in Rd takes the form 1/(‖η‖d log 2(1 /‖η‖)) (with normalization V = 2 d). At d = 2 the conjecture is empirically verified (Section 21.4; closed-form normalization residual 1.08 × 10 −6, direction-isotropy at machine precision, 99 th-percentile empirical V = 4 .358 on n = 1 ,000 2 D Gaussian walks within 0.36 of the predicted V = 2 d = 4 ); it is therefore a verified-in-low-dimension heuristic rather than a pure conjecture. A rigorous minimax analysis at general d – and larger-d empirical verification at d ≥ 4 via GPU-scale simulation – remain open. (O5) Truncation-free ηmax formulation. A truncation-free derivation linking V = 2 to the Hartman–Wintner √2σ2

would simplify the proof of V without requiring any specific truncation. The IT identity simplifies the proof of V

for any specific truncation, but does not yet remove the truncation entirely. (O6) Pathwise duality between LIL and CGF games. The proof of Theorem 17.1 is via the exponentiated null re-striction; a finer claim would be that the path-to-path Wasserstein distance between the realized LIL game and a corresponding realized CGF game is 0 along the equalizer’s saddle, not just up to leading order. (O7) Iterated-log charts of higher order. Theorem 13.2 works at the second-level chart μ2 = log log(1 /η ). Iterated-log charts at level k ≥ 3 recover the higher Erdős thresholds ( α = 1 rather than 3/2 at the next layer, per E00347). 62 A clean iterated-log-chart proof of the Erdős hierarchy would be pleasing; the IT identity provides the natural framework. (O8) Functional LIL. Strassen’s ball is the Cameron–Martin unit ball. Is there a measure-valued equalizer on C[0 , 1] 

that recovers Strassen, with the V constant playing the role of the Strassen radius? Five further structural connections that we surface but do not develop in detail are catalogued in Appendix A. 

# 23 Concluding remarks 

The oblivious mixture strategies used in finite-time LIL proofs and in modern confidence sequences are not arbitrary design choices. They are equalizer strategies of a CGF-constrained sequential detection game, and they admit an exact pathwise Gibbs-variational accounting: the realized log-wealth equals the posterior-mean per-bet log payoff minus the bits the path has spent updating the prior to the posterior. The Erdős integral test is the normalizability criterion for the equalizer prior, and the multiply iterated logarithms mark the exact threshold where the Learner’s hedging cost transitions from affordable to unaffordable. Three complementary morals follow. The first is exact and minimax: the density 1/(|λ|(log(1 /|λ|)) 2) is the unique minimax equalizer of a natural reduced scale-allocation game, and equivalently the unique GROW-optimal e-process for the scale-family alternative. The second is asymptotic and classical: the Erdős normalizability test describes the hierarchy of asymptotic equalizers attached to sharper and sharper upper-class boundaries, with the sharp first correction coeffi-cient 3/2 and the full hierarchy (3 /2, 1, 1, . . . ). The third is information-theoretic and structural: under the iterated-log chart μ = log log(1 /λ ), the equalizer is the rate-1 shifted exponential; the LIL game is the exponentiated null-restricted shadow of a CGF-constrained repeated game over KL balls; and the finite-dimensional coincidence center converges in 

2-Wasserstein distance to the continuum equalizer. The law of the iterated logarithm is not an artifact of bookkeeping. It is the solution to a resource-allocation problem: 

how to spread a finite budget of detection power across infinitely many timescales, when later timescales are exponentially cheaper to reach but logarithmically more expensive to monitor. The answer is the equalizer density, and the rate it produces — 

√2 log log n, with the 3/2 correction at the next order — is a fact to derive from first principles whenever it is needed.

These five items are independent of the rest of the paper and of one another. Each connects the LIL game to a separate strand of nonparametric inference or information geometry; each is sketched here so that the natural follow-up direction is at-hand for an interested reader. (N1) Tilting-Jeffreys–Haar trilogy. The identity dη/η = d(log η) shows that the right-Haar measure on (0 , ∞)× is Lebesgue measure on the logarithmic coordinate. Under the LIL parametrization η⋆(t) = √2h(t)/t , the logarith-mic coordinate maps (up to an affine factor) to the double-logarithmic time log log t. The equalizer density on the original η-scale thus pulls back to a quadratically decaying density on the log log t-axis. This is the geometric reason the Erdős integrand has the form du/u c−1/2 after u = log log t: the Laplace envelope produces a proper distribu-tion on log log t, not on t itself.
