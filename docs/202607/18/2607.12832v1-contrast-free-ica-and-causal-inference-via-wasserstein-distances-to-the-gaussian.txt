## Contrast-Free ICA and Causal Inference via Wasserstein Distances to the Gaussian

Abstract 

We study the squared 2-Wasserstein distance to the standard Gaussian as a non-Gaussianity criterion and use it for linear Independent Component Analysis (ICA) and causal inference in Linear Non-Gaussian Acyclic Models (LiNGAM). The analysis relies on a strict inequal-ity between the Wasserstein non-Gaussianity of independent standardized sources and that of their linear combinations. When at most one source is Gaussian, any unit-norm linear combination involving at least two sources has strictly smaller squared Wasserstein dis-tance than the corresponding weighted sum of source distances. At the population level, this yields exact identification of the ICA unmixing matrix, up to signed permutation, and gives an analogous characterization of causal orders through least-squares residuals. We then define empirical plug-in estimators and prove distribution-free uniform convergence bounds under finite-moment assumptions, before detailing three practical solvers: a Picard-style orthogonal optimizer for ICA, an exhaustive dynamic program for causal-order search, and a greedy order-search variant. Empirically, we demonstrate competitive performance for both tasks and provide open-source implementations for source separation and causal inference. 

Keywords: Independent Component Analysis, Causal Inference, Optimal Transport, Wasserstein Distance, Non-Gaussianity. 

Introduction 

Linear independent component analysis for source separation (Jutten and Herault, 1991) and linear non-Gaussian acyclic models for causal inference (Shimizu et al., 2006) are of-ten presented as distinct problems. In ICA, one observes an invertible linear mixture of independent latent sources and seeks to recover them up to permutation and scaling. In linear causal inference, one observes variables generated by independent structural noises through an acyclic linear system and seeks to recover the causal order, and eventually the underlying directed graph. Classical ICA algorithms optimize cumulants, likelihood surrogates, or approximations of negentropy (Comon, 1994; Bell and Sejnowski, 1995; Hyvarinen, 1999; Hyv¨ arinen and Oja, 2000). A common approach is to seek an unmixing matrix such that the recovered sources

While effective, these methods require choosing a contrast or nonlinearity, whose suitability depends on the source distributions. Non-Gaussian causal inference methods, such as LiNGAM, rely on the same identifiability principle (Shimizu et al., 2006, 2011). Score-based exact DAG learning enables global optimization, but iden-tifiable linear scores typically rely on variance restrictions or other structural assumptions (Silander and Myllym¨ aki, 2006; Loh and B¨ uhlmann, 2014; Peters and B¨ uhlmann, 2014). Overall, after whitening in ICA or residualization under a candidate causal order, identifi-cation depends on the non-Gaussianity of the independent components. However, practical algorithms are often based on second-order information, which sometimes remains insuffi-cient. This paper studies a single, natural non-Gaussianity score: the squared 2-Wasserstein distance of a standardized random variable to the standard Gaussian. This score is distri-butional rather than moment-based. We call it contrast-free in a specific sense: although the criterion is itself a contrast, it is fixed by the Gaussian reference and has no tunable non-linearity or moment order. Classical non-Gaussianity identifiability is usually established through entropy and the entropy-power inequality, which require estimating densities or entropies in practice. The Wasserstein route replaces these by a one-dimensional optimal-transport quantity that admits a direct order-statistics plug-in, without density estimation. This makes the resulting ICA estimator distribution-free: apart from standardization, non-Gaussianity, and finite moments for concentration, the theory does not impose a parametric source family, density smoothness, cumulant condition, or likelihood model. Under the usual ICA assumption that at most one source is Gaussian, we prove that a nontrivial orthogonal mixture of independent standardized sources has strictly smaller total Wasserstein non-Gaussianity than the unmixed sources. We use this comparison to establish both identifiability of an Optimal Transport (OT)-based ICA objective and iden-tifiability of causal orders through Wasserstein scores of regression residuals. In each case, Lemma 10 distinguishes coordinates that isolate an independent component from coordi-nates that combine several components. A correct ICA rotation or a correct causal order exposes the individual independent components, namely the sources or the structural noises as least-squares residuals, whereas a wrong rotation or a wrong order produces residuals that mix several of them. Because such nontrivial mixtures are strictly closer to the Gaus-sian, maximizing the total Wasserstein non-Gaussianity favors precisely the unmixed ICA coordinates and the residuals of topological causal orders. 

Main Contributions. In this paper, we prove a Wasserstein comparison principle for linear combinations of independent random variables and a strict form for nontrivial mix-tures of non-Gaussian sources. We also derive an optimal transport-based objective whose oracle maximizers are exactly the true unmixing matrices, up to signed permutations, and we study its exact empirical plug-in estimator based on the one-dimensional quantile for-mula, establishing distribution-free uniform convergence and consistency of global empirical maximizers for ICA unmixing. We use the same principle to extend the methodology to identify causal orders in linear non-Gaussian Structural Equation Models (SEMs): wrong orders create residuals that mix structural noises and, therefore, reduce the Wasserstein or-der objective. This gives a distribution-free score-based characterization of LiNGAM causal orders under the standard non-Gaussianity condition. Finally, we develop a relative quasi-2Newton solver for ICA and exact and greedy order-search procedures for causal inference, and we evaluate their source-separation accuracy, causal-order recovery, and computational cost in controlled experiments. 

1 Related Work 

ICA is a classical model for blind source separation. Its standard identifiability theory shows that independent non-Gaussian sources can be recovered from linear mixtures up to signed permutations, provided that at most one source is Gaussian (Comon, 1994). Early and influential algorithms include cumulant-based methods, Infomax, and FastICA (Bell and Sejnowski, 1995; Hyvarinen, 1999; Hyv¨ arinen and Oja, 2000). Modern ICA solvers also exploit second-order optimization on the whitened orthogonal problem. For instance, the Picard algorithm uses a preconditioned quasi-Newton scheme and is particularly relevant to our Wasserstein implementation (Ablin et al., 2018). Our objective keeps the same orthogonal geometry after whitening, but replaces likelihood or negentropy surrogates by a canonical Wasserstein distance to the Gaussian. This leads to a distribution-free consistency statement for a fixed empirical ICA objective, rather than an algorithmic guarantee tied to a chosen nonlinearity, likelihood family, or source-density model. At the level of proof technique, the classical identifiability arguments rely on entropy and the entropy-power inequality, or equivalently on Fisher information and de Bruijn’s identity, to show that mixing increases Gaussianity (Comon, 1994). We instead use a strict optimal-transport comparison, which yields the same conclusion through uniqueness of monotone transport maps and avoids entropy or density estimation in the empirical criterion. Nonparametric ICA methods optimize distributional dependence criteria rather than fixed low-order cumulants or prescribed nonlinearities. Kernel ICA uses reproducing-kernel dependence measures (Bach and Jordan, 2002). Closest in spirit is the distance-covariance ICA of Matteson and Tsay (2017), which, after generalized whitening, searches the orthogo-nal group for the rotation minimizing a distance-covariance measure of mutual dependence. Since distance covariance vanishes if and only if the components are independent, the cri-terion is necessary and sufficient for independence, and consistency is established with-out assuming the existence of independent components a priori. Both methods optimize over the whitened orthogonal geometry, but they use different population characterizations: distance-covariance ICA minimizes joint dependence, whereas our criterion maximizes the sum of one-dimensional Wasserstein distances to the Gaussian and replaces an Opn2q depen-dence criterion with one-dimensional sorting. Identifiability follows from a strict compari-son principle for mixtures rather than an oracle characterization of independence. Recent optimal-transport approaches use multivariate ranks and related rank-based independence criteria (Niu and Bhattacharya, 2022). Particularly close is the Wasserstein projection-pursuit method of Mukherjee et al. (2023), which also maximizes one-dimensional W2 dis-tances to the Gaussian. However, it targets subspace recovery in a spiked model, whereas our ICA result recovers the individual source directions, up to signs and permutation. Linear non-Gaussian causal inference builds on the same source-separation principle. LiNGAM shows that a linear acyclic structural equation model with independent non-Gaussian noises is identifiable from observational data (Shimizu et al., 2006). DirectLiNGAM provides a greedy sequential procedure that avoids a full ICA post-processing step (Shimizu 3et al., 2011). In contrast, our order objective is also based on non-Gaussian residuals, but it is formulated as a score-based optimization problem with a fully distributional score. In particular, the resulting identification argument relies on the ordering structure and non-Gaussianity of the structural noises, while its empirical counterpart benefits from distribution-free convergence guarantees. Score-based structure learning traditionally optimizes decomposable local scores over DAGs. The dynamic program of Silander and Myllym¨ aki (2006) gives an exact combi-natorial solver for globally optimal Bayesian network structures at exponential cost. For linear SEMs, identifiable score-based approaches have been obtained under additional re-strictions, including known or equal error variances (Loh and B¨ uhlmann, 2014; Peters and B¨ uhlmann, 2014). In contrast, our oracle criterion uses only the standard non-Gaussianity condition familiar from ICA and is distribution-free with respect to the structural-noise laws. Continuous relaxations such as NOTEARS and DAGMA replace the combinatorial acyclicity constraint by smooth characterizations (Zheng et al., 2018; Bello et al., 2022). They are scalable, but their solutions depend on nonconvex continuous optimization and regularization choices.

3 Optimal Transport ICA 

In this section, we consider linear independent component analysis and assume that the data distribution is centered and whitened. This population-level normalization is standard in ICA analyses (Comon, 1994; Hyv¨ arinen et al., 2001) and reduces a general invertible ICA model to an orthogonal one, leaving only sign and permutation indeterminacies. Empiri-cally, a consistent plug-in strategy consists in subtracting the sample mean and applying a whitening map, for instance through the eigendecomposition of the empirical covariance ma-trix. On the resulting orthogonal parameter space, we maximize the squared 2-Wasserstein distance of each recovered coordinate to the Gaussian. Since this quantity is computed ex-actly in one dimension, the resulting empirical objective does not require density estimation, entropy approximation, or the choice of a nonlinearity.

4 Causal Inference in Linear Non-Gaussian SEMs 

In this section, we further specialize our framework and show that the Wasserstein com-parison principle can also be used for causal order recovery in linear non-Gaussian acyclic models. As in many two-stage approaches, including ICA-LiNGAM (Shimizu et al., 2006), we first focus on order recovery. Given a causal order, parent-set estimation then reduces to a sequence of sparse regression problems, which can be efficiently solved by adaptive Lasso-type procedures (Zou, 2006; Shimizu et al., 2006). As in the ICA analysis, the data distribution is assumed to be centered at the population level, which empirically corresponds to a centering step obtained by subtracting the sample mean. Intuitively, in the oracle set-ting, the correct causal order yields least-squares residuals which coincide with structural noises. In a wrong order, at least one residual combines several independent noises, bringing it closer to a Gaussian distribution. Hence, the strict Wasserstein inequality can distinguish correct orders from incorrect ones. More precisely, orthogonal rotations in the ICA analysis and least-squares residualization under a candidate causal order both produce one-dimensional linear combinations of the independent components. Lemma 10 therefore applies to the recovered ICA coordinates and to the standardized order residuals.

5.2 LiNGAM Solvers 

We observe a centered data matrix X P Rnˆd, whose rows are independent observations of the d variables. We consider three strategies for estimating the causal order. The first 14 combines the proposed OT-ICA estimator with the standard LiNGAM pipeline, while the other two optimize the proposed Wasserstein order objective directly. Once an order pσ has been obtained, a second estimation step can be used to recover the weighted adjacency matrix pBppσq, for instance by fitting the multiple linear regressions with adaptive Lasso, as in the lingam package. 

5.3 OT-ICA-LiNGAM 

A first practical strategy is to use an equivalent formulation of Model (3), namely X “ εpId´

B‹q´J , which expresses the observations as linear mixtures of the independent structural noises ε P Rnˆd.Leveraging Algorithm 1, the data can be unmixed using the OT-ICA solver after a whitening step. The resulting unmixing matrix then induces an estimate pB, which is not guaranteed to be acyclic. As in standard ICA-LiNGAM, this estimate is post-processed by finding a suitable permutation through a linear sum-assignment problem to “dagify” pB,which can be solved using the Hungarian algorithm (Shimizu et al., 2006). This yields a di-rect analogue of the FastICA-based causal inference implementation of the lingam package. However, the main limitation, which also motivated the introduction of DirectLiNGAM by Shimizu et al. (2011), is that acyclicity is enforced only greedily and after estimation rather than being imposed as a constraint in the estimation problem itself.

A third greedy causal order estimation strategy consists in constructing the causal or-der from source to sink by repeatedly selecting the remaining variable whose standardized residual has the largest empirical 2-Wasserstein distance to the Gaussian. In contrast to Exhaustive OT-LiNGAM, it does not compare complete orders. Instead, it maintains a residual matrix, initially equal to the centered data, and removes the linear effect of each selected source from every remaining variable. The main advantage of this strategy is its scalability. The drawback of this procedure is twofold. First, since it is irreversible, early errors cannot be corrected, which may make it more sensitive to noise and less stable than global maximization. Moreover, the exactness result in Theorem 33, provided alongside its proof in Appendix B.13, requires the additional assumption that all structural noises have the same Wasserstein non-Gaussianity. Nonetheless, the procedure remains applicable beyond this setting and can still provide accurate causal orders when these non-Gaussianity levels only differ moderately. Conclusion and Perspectives 

We introduced a Wasserstein non-Gaussianity criterion for linear ICA and linear causal order recovery. The key mechanism is a strict comparison principle that yields the identifi-ability of the Wasserstein ICA objective, and through regression residuals, the identifiability of causal orders in linear non-Gaussian SEMs. The resulting framework uses a distributional score for source separation and causal discovery, avoiding hand-chosen ICA nonlinearities while retaining the standard non-Gaussian identifiability assumption. The same score also supports practical algorithms in both settings. For ICA, it can be optimized on the orthog-onal group using a Picard-style solver, and causal orders can be estimated by exhaustive dynamic programming and greedy approximations. 24 Yet, the scope of these results is limited by the modeling assumptions. The analysis relies on linearity and on the independence of the latent sources or structural noises. These assumptions are standard in ICA and LiNGAM, but they exclude nonlinear causal mech-anisms, feedback, and latent confounding. Furthermore, for ICA, the population objective is identifiable, but the empirical objective remains nonconvex on the orthogonal group, so practical Picard-style solvers are local optimizers. For causal inference, Exhaustive OT-LiNGAM gives a true global optimum for the empirical order score, but its exponential dependence on the number of variables limits its use to moderate dimensions. Future work may include nonlinear SEMs, latent-confounder models, and high-dimensional screening procedures that reduce the candidate pool before exact or greedy order search. Another direction is to improve Wasserstein ICA optimization using the piecewise-quadratic structure induced by projection ranks.
