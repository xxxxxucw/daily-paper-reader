# Knowledge-Informed Local Causal Discovery of Optimal Adjustment Sets 

Seong Woo Ahn ∗, Alessandro Leite †, José Lucas De Melo Costa ∗

Fabrice Popineau ∗, Bich-Liên Doan ∗, Arpad Rimmel ∗∗Laboratoire Interdisciplinaire des Sciences du Numérique, Université Paris-Saclay, CNRS, CentraleSupélec, France 

†LITIS UR 4108, INSA Rouen Normandy, University of Rouen Normandy, France 

Abstract —Local causal discovery is a scalable alternative to global structure learning. However, it can struggle to identify valid adjustment sets in data-scarce settings because of finite-sample uncertainty, incomplete local neighborhoods, and unre-solved Markov equivalence. Although many application domains provide structured background knowledge, its integration into local causal discovery remains limited. We propose b-LOAD ,a knowledge-informed extension of the LOAD Schubert et al. (2025a) algorithm for local discovery of optimal adjustment sets. 

b-LOAD incorporates prior edge constraints directly into the local structure-learning procedure and uses Meek’s rules to expand the discovery frontier dynamically, yielding a knowledge-constrained maximally partially directed acyclic graph (MPDAG) over the relevant local subgraph. This strategy helps prevent structurally relevant nodes introduced by prior knowledge from being excluded by local search. We prove that, under sound background knowledge, the procedure monotonically refines the admissible equivalence class and can enlarge the set of identifiable causal queries, enabling recovery of optimal adjustment sets that are not identifiable from observational conditional-independence information alone. Empirically, b-LOAD improves downstream causal effect estimation relative to purely data-driven and standard knowledge-augmented baselines, particularly in data-scarce and structurally complex regimes. Results on real-world biological networks show that locally targeted prior knowledge provides the largest gains and remains beneficial under moderate structural noise. These findings position b-LOAD as a scalable approach for converting fragmented domain knowledge into more reliable causal-effect estimation. 

Index Terms —Causal discovery, Local causal discovery, Optimal adjustment sets, Background knowledge, Markov equivalence class 

I. I NTRODUCTION 

Modern AI systems excel at capturing statistical associations, yet many scientific and high-stakes applications require reason-ing about the effects of actions. While predictive models exploit correlations, domains such as epidemiology, economics, and medicine demand answers to counterfactual questions, what would happen under some intervention, rather than what merely co-occurs (Pearl, 2009; Pearl and Mackenzie, 2018). Causal inference provides the formal framework for this shift. Directed acyclic graphs (DAGs) provide a canonical repre-sentation of causal structure (Spirtes et al., 2000; Pearl, 2009). When the graph is known, causal effects can be identified using graphical criteria such as adjustment sets (Pearl, 1993; Perkovi´ c et al., 2018) or the IDA estimator over a CPDAG’s 

Corresponding author: seong-woo.ahn@universite-paris-saclay.fr 

Markov equivalence class (Maathuis et al., 2009). In practice, however, the causal graph must be inferred from observational data. Causal structure learning methods recover equivalence classes of DAGs from data, but face fundamental scalability limitations in high-dimensional settings. Local causal discovery mitigates this challenge by restricting attention to a target variable and its neighborhood, typically its Markov blanket (Pellet and Elisseeff, 2008; Wang et al., 2014; Margaritis and Thrun, 2000; Tsamardinos et al., 2003; Aliferis et al., 2010). While this locality enables scalable inference, it introduces a key limitation: the resulting completed partially directed acyclic graph (CPDAG) is often weakly oriented, especially in data-scarce or structurally complex regimes, preventing identification of causal effects. Schubert et al. (2025a) address this limitation with LOAD (Local Optimal Adjustments Discovery), which recovers op-timal adjustment sets (Henckel et al., 2022) by minimizing asymptotic estimation variance, from local structure alone. How-ever, LOAD remains fundamentally data-driven. In low-sample regimes, the learned CPDAG typically retains many undi-rected edges, leaving causal effects non-identifiable. Moreover, LOAD’s empirical evaluation is largely restricted to synthetic Erd˝ os–Rényi graphs at sample sizes that comfortably exceed the variable count—a setting at odds with the deployment scenarios that most motivate causal-effect estimation. In biomedical applications, gene-regulatory and protein-signaling networks routinely present hundreds of variables with comparable or fewer observations, inverting the standard n ≫ d assumption and driving conditional independence (CI)-based CPDAGs into the weakly-oriented regime where identification breaks down. At the same time, many application domains combine limited observational data with rich sources of structured prior knowledge, such as curated knowledge graphs or domain constraints. These encode partial causal relationships and provide a natural source of orientation information. While the integration of background knowledge into causal graphs is well understood theoretically (Meek, 1995), its use within local discovery pipelines for optimal adjustment set identification

(1) Knowledge-informed local discovery with formal guar-antees. II. P RELIMINARIES 

We introduce the key concepts and notation used throughout the paper, following the conventions of Pearl (2009) and Spirtes et al. (2000). A causal graph G := ( V, E) encodes causal dependencies over observed variables V = {X1, . . . , X d}. A directed edge 

Xi → Xj represents a direct causal influence; Pa G (Xi),

An G (Xi), De G (Xi), Ch G (Xi), and Sib G (Xi) denote the par-ents, ancestors, descendants, children, and siblings of Xi,respectively. Causal graphs are assumed to be DAGs (Pearl, 2009; Spirtes et al., 2000). Under the Markov and faithfulness 

assumptions, conditional independencies in the data correspond exactly to d-separation statements in G.

Markov equivalence. Multiple DAGs can encode the same conditional independencies and are thus indistinguishable from observational data. Such DAGs form a Markov equivalence class , uniquely represented as a CPDAG, a mixed graph sharing the same skeleton and v-structures (unshielded colliders 

Xi → Xk ← Xj with Xi̸ ∼ Xj 2) as all DAGs in the class (Andersson et al., 1997; Hauser and Bühlmann, 2012). When background knowledge constrains additional edge orientations, the equivalence class is further refined into a maximally partially directed acyclic graph (MPDAG) (Meek, 1995; Wienöbst et al., 2021), representing only those DAGs consistent with both data and prior constraints.

Meek’s orientation rules. After v-structures are identified, four deterministic rules (Meek, 1995) are applied exhaustively in any order to propagate edge orientations to yield the unique CPDAG of the equivalence class (Figure 2). Each rule orients an undirected edge to prevent either a new v-structure or a directed cycle: R1 orients β − γ as β → γ

when α → β and α̸ ∼ γ; R2 orients α−γ when a directed path α → β → γ would otherwise create a cycle; R3 and R4 

handle configurations with two directed paths converging on a common node, orienting the remaining undirected edge to avoid spurious colliders. These same rules are applied when incorporating background knowledge: forced orientations from prior constraints are propagated through R1–R4 to derive all logically entailed directions, yielding an MPDAG. III. R ELATED WORK 

Global and local causal structure learning. For a recent survey see Heinze-Deml et al. (2018). Constraint-based meth-ods such as PC and FCI Spirtes et al. (2000); Zhang (2008), score-based approaches such as GES Chickering (2002), and hybrid score/constraint methods such as MMHC Tsamardinos et al. (2006) recover Markov equivalence classes from obser-vational data but suffer from poor scalability due to the super-exponential size of the search space Robinson (1977). Local causal discovery addresses this by restricting inference to the neighborhood of a target variable. Sequential methods such as MB-by-MB Wang et al. (2014) and the HITON/IAMB Markov-blanket family Aliferis et al. (2010) iteratively expand the local structure, while parallel approaches such as DCILP Dong et al. (2025) learn and reconcile overlapping subgraphs. While these methods improve computational efficiency, they do not directly address identifiability or statistical optimality of causal effect estimation from partially oriented structures. 

Local optimal adjustment set discovery. Schubert et al. (2025a) introduce LOAD, which takes a step further by com-bining local discovery with the theory of optimal adjustment sets Henckel et al. (2022). Building on MB-by-MB Wang et al. (2014), LOAD identifies settings where the causal effect of X on Y is globally identifiable, using graphical criteria of amenability and, when so, recovers both the direction of causality and the associated optimal adjustment set from local structural information alone. LOAD thus bridges the gap between scalable local discovery and statistically efficient causal inference. However, as discussed in Section II, its purely data-driven nature limits performance in data-scarce or structurally complex settings, and its guarantees are confined to settings of explicit ancestry. 

Background knowledge in causal discovery. The integration of prior knowledge into causal discovery has a rich theoretical foundation (Heckerman et al., 1995). Meek (1995) establishes the formal framework for causal inference under background knowledge, showing how required and forbidden edge orienta-tions can be consistently incorporated into a CPDAG to yield an MPDAG, with orientation constraints propagated via the four Meek rules (Figure 2) Meek (1995). Perkovi´ c et al. (2017) further develop this theory by providing tools for interpret-ing and manipulating CPDAGs augmented with background knowledge, and notably underscores the importance of Meek’s fourth rule, which is often omitted in implementations, for obtaining a maximally oriented representation. Following their “b-” prefix convention for background-knowledge analogues, we name our method b-LOAD . Beyond pairwise edge constraints, Borboudakis and Tsamardinos (2012) generalize the formalism to path-level (ancestral) constraints, including in MAGs. These works collectively establish that data-driven discovery alone has inherent identifiability limitations and that structured prior knowledge provides a principled mechanism for resolving otherwise undetermined edge orientations. 

Local discovery with background knowledge. Most closely related to our work is the recent contribution of Zheng et al. (2026), who extend MB-by-MB to incorporate background knowledge within a local causal discovery framework, ground-ing the approach in theoretical soundness and producing locally consistent MPDAGs (their construction underlies the LOCAL MPDAG procedure of Algorithm 1). Fang and He (2020) independently propose a fully local IDA-style causal-effect estimator under background knowledge in MPDAGs, but stop short of identifying statistically optimal adjustment sets. Like its predecessors, neither method extends to the recovery of optimal adjustment sets. Our proposed framework precisely addresses this gap by combining knowledge-informed local structure refinement with the optimality criteria of Henckel et al. (2022), enabling both the principled incorporation of prior knowledge and statistically efficient causal inference from local information. As shown in Table I, b-LOAD is the first framework to jointly support local discovery, background knowledge integration, MPDAG reasoning, and statistically optimal adjustment set identification. IV. M ETHOD 

A. Theoretical Motivations 

Three observations shape the design of b-LOAD .

(i) Optimal adjustment requires orientation. Optimal-adjustment-set theory (Henckel et al., 2022) requires a suffi-ciently oriented graph: every proper causal path from treatment 

X to outcome Y must begin with a directed edge out of X, the amenability condition of Perkovi´ c et al. (2018). Background knowledge supplies the missing orientations precisely in the regimes—finite samples, dense local neighborhoods—where data alone underdetermines them. 

(ii) MPDAG manipulation needs the four-rule Meek closure. 

Manipulating a CPDAG under background constraints requires the four-rule closure of Perkovi´ c et al. (2017) to obtain a maximally oriented MPDAG. The three-rule closure propagates v-structures and acyclicity but misses orientations implied jointly by two directed paths converging on a common node, captured by R4—exactly the configurations that background-knowledge edges create. 

(iii) Local search must be re-driven by every orientation. The-orem 1 of Zheng et al. (2026) establishes that MB-by-MB-style local search remains sound under the background knowledge provided the frontier of nodes to expand is recomputed after every orientation step. Prior knowledge must therefore drive the search trajectory itself, not patch its output: a post-hoc orientation pass on a learned CPDAG cannot recover the nodes that would have been reached through a background-induced orientation. Lemma 1 and Proposition 1 formalize the end-to-end guarantees that follow from composing these three ingredients in Algorithm 1.

Let D be observational data generated from a distribution that is Markovian and faithful with respect to G, and let B be a set of valid background knowledge. Denote by G∗ the true MPDAG representing the Markov equivalence class of G restricted by B, and let X be the target variable. Assume all conditional independencies are correctly identified. Remark 1. Lemma 1 implies that any failure of b-LOAD 

to identify the causal effect or the optimal adjustment set is attributable to irreducible Markov equivalence—edges that remain undirected even in G∗—rather than to errors in local discovery. Background knowledge strictly reduces this equiva-lence class, and therefore monotonically improves identifiability prospects, as formalized next. 

Proposition 1 (Background Knowledge Improves Identifiabil-ity) . Let C be the CPDAG of the Markov equivalence class of G, and let M = M(C, B) be the MPDAG obtained by incorporating valid background knowledge B̸ = ∅ via Meek’s rules R1–R4. Let (X, Y ) be a causal query. Then: (i) Monotone refinement. The equivalence class represented by M is a strict subset of that represented by C: every DAG consistent with M is consistent with C, but not vice versa. (ii) Improved identifiability. If the causal effect of X on Y

is identifiable in C (i.e., C is amenable w.r.t. (X, Y )), it remains identifiable in M. Conversely, there exist configurations in which the effect is not identifiable in C

but becomes identifiable in M, strictly expanding the set of queries for which b-LOAD returns an answer. (iii) Optimal adjustment set preservation. If O⋆ is the optimal adjustment set identified from C, then O⋆ remains a valid adjustment set in M. Furthermore, additional orientations in M may yield a strictly smaller or lower-variance optimal adjustment set than that obtainable from C alone. B. Modelling Background Knowledge a) Working assumption: partial but perfect knowledge.: 

We model background knowledge as a partial, fully correct snapshot of the true causal graph: any constraint supplied to b-LOAD is consistent with the data-generating DAG G. Formally, given a true DAG G = ( V, E), background knowledge is a set 

B+ ⊆ E 

of required directed edges, so that every orientation supplied to b-LOAD is a true directed edge of G. This formalizes the standard correctness assumption in the background-knowledge causal-discovery literature (Meek, 1995; Perkovi´ c et al., 2017). As a deliberate design choice, we restrict ourselves to the required edges in this work—the most common form of curated prior knowledge in practice—and leave forbidden edges to the data-driven skeleton step. The motivation is empirical. In high-expertise application domains, curated knowledge graphs and expert-elicited con-straints typically encode a subset of well-established causal relationships rather than a complete description of the system. Our working assumption is faithful to this regime, where the practical limitation is coverage rather than correctness .This is precisely the setting in which our real-data evaluations (Section V) operate. We deliberately stress-test this assumption rather than rely on it: Section VI and Appendix A report a preliminary analysis of b-LOAD under controlled corruption of B+, characterizing the degradation regime as the knowledge becomes noisy. 

Two sampling strategies for B+. Given a query (X, Y ), where in G does a practitioner’s prior knowledge actually live? Two stylized scenarios capture the dominant practical cases. Both are parameterized by a fraction ρ ∈ (0 , 1) controlling the proportion of edges from the eligible pool revealed to b-LOAD 

as background knowledge. 

Local sampling strategy. 3 B+ is sampled uniformly from edges of E incident to either X or Y . This simulates a practitioner who possesses incomplete but highly accurate domain knowledge about the immediate neighborhoods of 

X and Y .

Global sampling strategy. B+ is sampled uniformly from the full edge set E without conditioning on X or Y . This simulates the scenario in which a practitioner has access to a general domain knowledge graph that may or may not happen to touch the variables of the specific query. Together the two strategies let us disentangle how much 

prior knowledge is available from where it lies, providing a controlled instrument for the experiments of Section V. 

C. The b-LOAD Algorithm 

Algorithm 1 presents b-LOAD . The outer loop is that of LOAD (Schubert et al., 2025a): invoke local discovery around each target via L OCAL MPDAG, check that X is a proper ancestor of Y and that the resulting graph is amenable w.r.t. 

(X, Y ) (Perkovi´ c et al., 2018), then extract the optimal adjust-ment set via the criterion of Henckel et al. (2022). Background knowledge enters the inner procedure L OCAL MPDAG at (a)– (d). 

(a) Initialization. Seeding the local graph with B+ (rather than the empty graph) makes prior orientations available to the first v-structure check and Meek pass, so they enter the search trajectory rather than patching its output. 

(b) Protected updates. After each Markov-blanket discovery and orientation step, B+ is re-imposed on the working graph. This serves two purposes. First, it prevents finite-sample errors of the PC-style skeleton substep from deleting required edges and thereby invalidating subsequent Meek propagation. Second, it resolves any conflict between a data-driven orientation and a constraint in B+ in favor of B+: the re-imposition overwrites the conflicting data-driven orientation, so the working graph after step (b) is consistent with B+ by construction. Because 

B+ ⊆ E for the true acyclic DAG G, the subset of edges fixed by B+ is itself acyclic, and the conflict-resolution rule guarantees that no directed cycle can be introduced by the imposition of background knowledge. Edges outside B+

are oriented by data-driven v-structure detection and Meek propagation, both of which are sound under the standard assumptions (Theorem 1 of Zheng et al. (2026)) and preserve acyclicity by construction of the rules themselves (Meek, 1995).

A. Experimental Setup 

Background knowledge sampling. We instantiate the local and global sampling strategies of Section IV-B at every fraction ρ ∈ { 0.1, 0.2, . . . , 0.9}, running each (ρ, strategy )

configuration independently. Sampled edge orientations are assumed correct in every configuration except in the preliminary noise-robustness analysis of Appendix A, where we corrupt a fraction of them. 

Evaluation metrics. We report two complementary metrics. The F1 score on optimal adjustment set identification measures how accurately each method recovers the ground-truth optimal adjustment set O⋆, balancing precision and recall over its constituent variables. As a downstream metric we adopt the 

Intervention Distance of Schubert et al. (2025b), defined as the distance between the ground-truth causal effect ΘT,T ′ from a target T to T ′ and the predicted causal effect bΘT,T ′ for the same pair obtained by adjusting on the returned set. Unlike F1, which scores the composition of the adjustment set, the intervention distance scores the quantity the practitioner actually consumes—the estimated causal effect—and is the metric of primary interest in the downstream use of causal discovery. 

Statistical power metric. Following Kummerfeld et al. (2024), we summarize experimental conditions along a unified axis of 

statistical power per structural parameter , defined as: 

Π = n

|V| · dexp /2 , (4) where n is the number of samples, |V| the number of observed variables, and dexp the expected node degree. The denominator approximates the total number of edges in the graph 4, so Π

Low Π corresponds to data-scarce, high-complexity regimes where purely data-driven methods are expected to struggle; high Π corresponds to favorable regimes where LOAD and b-LOAD should converge. When does background knowledge add the most value? 

b-LOAD yields the highest returns under data scarcity and high causal complexity (low Π), where unreliable conditional independence tests leave the local CPDAG weakly oriented and background knowledge bypasses this limitation by deter-ministically reducing the equivalence class. While the choice between local and global sampling is metric-dependent—with global sampling often maximizing adjustment-set F1 (Figure 3 and Table II)— local sampling proves remarkably efficient and competitive on Intervention Distance specifically when background knowledge exposure is low (10%–30%) (Table II and Figures 5 and 6). This low-exposure regime is highly realistic, as practitioners in complex expert domains typically possess only partial, high-confidence knowledge localized to specific pathways rather than comprehensive graph coverage. In this realistic low-exposure regime, either sampling strategy of b-LOAD outperforms the naive global augmentation b-PC across both metrics; at higher exposures the ranking becomes metric-dependent (Table II). This yields a goal-driven recommendation: globally distributed orientations help when composing adjustment sets, whereas locally targeted orienta-tions are preferable for downstream causal-effect estimation, a distinction particularly relevant in biomedicine where curated pathways are locally abundant. 

F1 versus Intervention Distance. The empirical evaluations reveal that improvements in structural accuracy (F1) do not monotonically or predictably translate to better causal effect estimation. Under global sampling, this relationship is highly volatile: on the Sachs dataset, b-PC clearly outperforms b-LOAD 

on both F1 and Intervention Distance at certain background knowledge exposures, yet the metrics behave erratically for both algorithms as ρ increases. This unpredictability occurs because randomly distributed global constraints interact chaotically with adjustment sets; an injected global edge might improve the overall F1 score while inadvertently opening a backdoor path that severely biases the target’s downstream estimate. Crucially, this erratic global regime is also epistemically demanding, as practitioners rarely possess randomly scattered, domain-wide causal links. However, under local sampling— a far more realistic knowledge budget restricting constraints to the target’s immediate neighborhood—this unpredictability vanishes. In this regime, b-LOAD reliably and monotonically minimizes Intervention Distance, demonstrating that targeted local knowledge is essential for stable, bias-reducing effect estimation. 

Robustness to incorrect background knowledge: a pre-liminary note. Our main experiments assume strictly correct background knowledge (Meek, 1995; Perkovi´ c et al., 2017). Because real-world curated graphs often contain errors or context-specific exceptions, the practical question is how much corruption b-LOAD tolerates before becoming detrimental. We explored this by replacing required edges with false ones at a mean corruption rate μ on the synthetic sweep (Appendix A); the parallel question for forbidden edges B− is left for future work. Results suggest b-LOAD retains its full advantage at 

μ ≤ 0.1 and degrades gracefully on F1 at moderate noise (μ ∈ {0.2, 0.3}) while preserving a residual Intervention-Distance advantage. It crosses an operational boundary near 

μ = 0 .5, where the F1 advantage collapses. These anecdotal but encouraging findings suggest that a full evaluation under misspecified B is a natural next step. 

Limitations and Future Directions. Several limitations suggest natural directions for future work. First, b-LOAD 

inherits the assumption of causal sufficiency; extending it to explicitly model latent confounders via MAGs or PAGs (Zhang, 2008; Borboudakis and Tsamardinos, 2012) would broaden its applicability, and would naturally pair with non-adjustment identification approaches such as the general criterion of Tian and Pearl (2002). Second, the strict requirement of correct prior knowledge could be relaxed by developing mechanisms for weighted constraints, using confidence scores to modulate influence in settings where priors are systematically biased. Finally, our empirical scope is restricted to linear-Gaussian SEMs and biological networks; evaluating b-LOAD on non-Gaussian or nonlinear data and on substantially larger graphs in domains like economics or epidemiology would strengthen the empirical case. VII. C ONCLUSION 

We introduced b-LOAD , a knowledge-informed extension of the LOAD Schubert et al. (2025a) algorithm for local discovery of optimal adjustment sets. By incorporating background knowledge directly into the local structure-learning loop, b-LOAD refines the local equivalence class into a knowledge-constrained MPDAG that can identify causal effects unreach-able by purely data-driven methods. Experimental results on synthetic and real-world datasets show that b-LOAD leads to large gains in data-scarce, structurally complex regimes and enables causal-effect identification in settings where LOAD returns no answer. Further analyses show that locally targeted constraints yield the most reliable downstream effect estimates, offering actionable guidance for practitioners with partial domain knowledge. APPENDIX 

Proof of Lemma 1 . Background knowledge B is injected into the local graph prior to any structural discovery, so all directed edges in B+ are present from initialization and are consistent with G∗ by validity of B. The critical design feature of Algorithm 1 is its dynamic WAIT LIST (step (d) of the L OCAL MPDAG procedure): after each Markov blanket discovery and Meek propagation step, the frontier is recomputed as all nodes connected to X by an undirected path in the current graph that have not yet been processed. This ensures that the nodes are newly reachable through a background-induced orientation in B are automatically enqueued, preventing the failure mode in which such nodes are never examined for v-structures or orientation implications. For every node Z processed from the WaitList, the algorithm recovers its Markov blanket, learns the marginal graph over the relevant variables, and propagates all orientation implications through Meek’s rules R1–R4. Under perfect conditional inde-pendence testing, every v-structure and subsequently oriented edge is guaranteed to match G∗. The WaitList terminates when no new nodes are reachable, at which point all relevant unshielded triples have been examined and all implied orienta-tions propagated. By Theorem 1 of Zheng et al. (2026), this procedure is sound and complete over the local neighborhood of X, from which the three equalities follow directly. 

Proof of Proposition 1 . (i) By construction, M is obtained from C by orienting a subset of undirected edges via the constraints in B+ and propagating through Meek’s rules R1– R4. Each orientation step eliminates from the equivalence class those DAGs in which the constrained edge points in the opposite direction. Since B̸ = ∅, at least one edge is oriented, yielding a strictly smaller equivalence class. Acyclicity and the absence of new v-structures are preserved by the construction of Meek’s rules (Meek, 1995). 

(ii) Identifiability of the causal effect of X on Y via adjustment requires amenability of the graph w.r.t. (X, Y ): all proper causal paths from X to Y must begin with a directed edge out of X (Perkovi´ c et al., 2018). If C is amenable, then all edges on proper causal paths incident to X are already directed in C; these directions are preserved in M by part (i), so amenability is maintained. with the edge X − W undirected; C is not amenable w.r.t. 

(X, Y ) since the causal path through W is unresolved. If B+

contains X → W , then M orients this edge, rendering the path directed and M amenable w.r.t. (X, Y ), so the effect becomes identifiable.
