# Whom Should a Platform Amplify? Truth, Engagement, and Networked Polarization ∗

Zaruhi Hakobyan †

University of Luxembourg 

July 14, 2026 

Abstract 

Social-media platforms allocate reach, deciding whose content becomes widely visible. We study this as feed/reference design in a networked coordination game where users track an unknown state, coordinate with others, and hold biased ideal actions. Amplification changes both who receives information and who becomes a salient coordination reference. Making a private signal commonly observed adds a second common signal and removes the usual non-monotonicity of truth-tracking accuracy in public-signal precision; under a broadcast budget, accuracy depends only on total amplified precision. For any finite network and biases, a network intervention’s effect on accuracy splits exactly into an information gain and a quadratic bias cost governed by a Katz–Bonacich influence-overlap matrix, yielding an exact source-ranking rule and a closed-form amplification threshold. A reduced-form engagement objective instead favors validating, same-type links, producing more segregated networks and lower accuracy. Amplification’s value depends jointly on information, bias propagation, and the platform’s objective. 

Keywords: information design; platforms; social networks; coordination; transparency; polar-ization; higher-order beliefs.

Algo-rithmic amplification is not hypothetical: platform ranking measurably amplifies some voices over others (Husz´ ar et al., 2022). This raises a central platform-design question: when a platform can reshape an information network, whom should it amplify, and how does the answer change when the platform optimizes engagement rather than collective accuracy? The question matters because amplification is a structural intervention. A platform need not fabricate information, censor content, or persuade anyone in order to change collective outcomes: it can change the observation network—who receives whose signal, whose posts become common reference points, and which users acquire broadcast reach. Follow recommendations, feed ranking, repost suggestions, and “people you may know” modules all reshape the directed graph through which users observe one another. Yet the accuracy consequences of this lever are not obvious. Amplifying a highly informed source spreads a precise signal; but if that source is biased, the same reach transmits bias to many users at once. And a platform rewarded for engagement may rationally amplify sources that users find validating even when those sources are uninformative or socially harmful. We develop a model of algorithmic amplification as feed/reference design : a networked coordina-tion game with dispersed information and confirmation bias, in which the platform’s only instrument is the topology of the observation network. Agents seek to match an unknown fundamental—the correct interpretation of a news event, a policy, or a market condition—while also coordinating with others, the beauty-contest motive of Keynes (1936) as formalized by Morris and Shin (2002). Each agent observes a public signal, a private signal, and the private signals of the agents she is connected to; a directed link i → j means that agent i observes agent j’s signal, as when a user follows another user or receives that user’s content in her feed. The same feed also defines the local social reference group with which the user coordinates. This coupling is a deliberate modeling choice: on social platforms, recommendation systems jointly determine information exposure and salient peer comparisons, so amplification should be read as feed/reference design rather than as a pure information intervention. To capture the forces that make online environments polarizing, agents also have biased ideal actions: a user does not only want to track the truth, she is pulled toward the interpretation that confirms her type. In the accuracy analysis of Section 3 the platform sets the network directly; Section 4 then adds the liberal constraint that the platform can merely recommend link additions and deletions, which users accept or reject according to their own utility. A channel map organizes the results. The paper’s benchmark accuracy results in Sections 3.2– 3.3 operate through the information channel alone because they use uniform reference weights, where the feed/reference coupling does no work. In general finite networks, however, the variance channel also depends on the coordination weights. The paper’s polarization results—the demagogue threshold and the engagement echo chamber—depend intrinsically on the coordination channel :they require that amplifying a source also make it a payoff-relevant reference point. Section 5.4 2verifies this division directly by decoupling the two channels, showing that reorganizing reference groups, not filtering information, is what polarizes. Our main contribution is twofold. First, we introduce algorithmic reach as an endogenous feed/reference-design instrument in a coordination game with dispersed information—distinct from the canonical channels through which the economics literature studies social media: misinformation diffusion, algorithmic filtering of exposure, strategic persuasion and slanted news supply, content-sharing discipline, and strategic expression for popularity (Allcott and Gentzkow, 2017; Vosoughi et al., 2018; Allcott et al., 2020, 2022; Mostagir et al., 2022; Guriev et al., 2023; Acemoglu et al., 2024; Crawford and Sobel, 1982; Kamenica and Gentzkow, 2011; Mullainathan and Shleifer, 2005; Edmond, 2013; Kanik and Hakobyan, 2026). The platform in our model does not choose which state is true, which signals exist, or what users prefer; it chooses reach. This isolation allows us to separate three forces that are usually bundled together in discussions of social media: the precision of a source, the source’s bias as a coordination reference, and the objective used to allocate visibility. Second, we identify the source-level statistics that govern the accuracy consequences of reach: on the accuracy side, total broadcast precision; on the polarization side, a positive-semidefinite influence-overlap matrix , the Gram matrix of the R-weighted Katz–Bonacich influence profiles of all sources in the coordination graph (Katz, 1953; Bonacich, 1987; Ballester et al., 2006; Jackson, 2008). Its diagonal entries are the single-source bias-amplification multipliers, while its off-diagonal entries capture reinforcement or offsetting between multiple biased sources. These objects turn “whom should a platform amplify” from a rhetorical question into an exact, measurable design score. Our first result shows, in a reference-neutral global-broadcast benchmark, how adding a second common signal changes the classical transparency comparative statics. In the coordination model of Morris and Shin (2002), more precise public information can reduce accuracy because agents overweight the public signal as a coordination device—a result that generated a large debate on the social value of public information (Hellwig, 2005; Svensson, 2006; Morris et al., 2006; Angeletos and Pavan, 2007; Cornand and Heinemann, 2008; James and Lawler, 2011). We show that global broadcast reach restores monotonicity in this benchmark through a two-common-signal substitu-tion : the amplified private signal becomes a second public-like signal, agents tilt toward the more precise common source, and the single public signal loses its monopoly on coordination. Formally, broadcasting acts exactly as a shift of effective common precision (Remark 1), which mechanically moves the system out of the non-monotone region. We emphasize the scope of this result: it is an accuracy-channel statement about truth-tracking accuracy in a two-common-signal broadcast environment, not a claim about the coordination-inclusive payoff criterion or about raising the precision of the original public signal (see footnote 2 and the discussion after Theorem 2). Our second result characterizes which sources an accuracy-oriented platform should amplify within the broadcast benchmark. The unconstrained first best is trivial—connect everyone to everyone—so the economically relevant case is an attention-constrained platform with a limited number of amplified feed slots. In the symmetric large-N broadcast class, accuracy depends on the amplified set only through its total broadcast precision, and in the monotone region where 3public precision is not too low ( α ≥ β/ 8) an exact-use broadcast budget should go to the most precise sources. Outside this region the budget-use decision is a separate design margin: a small broadcast can be accuracy-reducing when public precision is very low and coordination is strong. The general finite-network budget problem has no closed form, because a single link change moves the entire equilibrium fixed point; we treat it computationally, and the computations—exact for small networks, a local-search regularity for larger ones—support the same precision-based rule at the baseline calibration. This connects the platform-design question to the literature on games, learning, and centrality in networks (Ballester et al., 2006; Jackson, 2008; Golub and Morris, 2017; Myatt and Wallace, 2019; Leister et al., 2019) and, most closely, to targeting interventions in net-work games (Galeotti et al., 2020); where that literature targets standalone incentives or attention, our contribution is to identify broadcast reach, the number of users who receive a source’s signal, as the design object in this class. Precision, however, is not sufficient. Our third result shows that the same amplification tech-nology turns harmful when a precise source is also a biased coordination reference—a phenomenon we call the demagogue effect . In the model, signals remain unbiased Gaussian draws; bias enters through users’ ideal actions. A biased hub therefore does not transmit bias by distorting a signal: its signal becomes widely observed while the hub simultaneously becomes payoff-relevant for coor-dination, so its biased ideal propagates through the reference-group channel. Accuracy decomposes into a variance channel and a bias channel. In any finite network and for any vector of source biases, the bias channel is an exact quadratic form in a matrix of pairwise influence-profile overlaps (Proposition 2). This yields an exact source-specific amplification score that incorporates both each source’s own multiplier and its interactions with all other biased sources. The single-source case reduces to the bias-amplification multiplier in Proposition 3. Evaluating that case in a canonical broadcast-with-local-reference benchmark yields, in the large-N limit, a closed-form demagogue threshold : amplification raises accuracy only if the hub’s bias is small enough relative to the in-formational gain. The mechanism links information design to the literature on polarization and biased assimilation (Lord et al., 1979; Nickerson, 1998; Dixit and Weibull, 2007; Halberstam and Knight, 2016; Gentzkow and Shapiro, 2011; Hakobyan and Koulovatianos, 2020), but is formally distinct from misinformation and strategic persuasion (Crawford and Sobel, 1982; Kamenica and Gentzkow, 2011; Edmond, 2013): the platform sends no message and distorts no signal; it changes accuracy by changing which source becomes a common information and reference point. We then ask whether an accuracy-oriented platform can implement the desirable network when users retain agency. The platform is a liberal planner : it recommends accuracy-improving additions and deletions, but users accept or reject them according to their own expected utility. Without bias, incentives are closely aligned—users accept nearly all accuracy-improving recommendations, and the simulated network moves toward the broadcast core. With bias, a polarization wedge opens: users prefer to coordinate with similar-bias neighbors, so they reject accurate cross-type sources that improve collective accuracy but make their own feed less validating. In the simulations this wedge generates homophilous rest points, and organic friends-of-friends rewiring erodes accuracy 4through the bias channel. The accuracy planner mitigates polarization but cannot fully undo users’ demand for confirmation (Mullainathan and Shleifer, 2005). This shifts attention from exposure alone, the focus of the empirical filtering literature (Bakshy et al., 2015; Flaxman et al., 2016; Levy, 2021; Guess et al., 2023; Nyhan et al., 2023), to the platform’s objective in choosing the observation network. The comparison with a subjective-engagement recommender is deliberately reduced-form: en-gagement is average expected user utility, as would arise if retention increased in expected utility. The objectives do not diverge mechanically for every source. We derive an exact decomposition of the engagement change into signal/covariance, own-validation, and coordination components. Acompact exact finite-network exercise repeats the two-camp comparison under several coordination calibrations and finds the same qualitative three-region pattern: sufficiently precise and moderate sources raise both objectives; validating sources with small information gains can be accepted only by engagement; and sufficiently extreme sources can lower both. The full parameter maps are relegated to an online appendix. Our mechanism is closest to Acemoglu et al. (2024), who reach a filter-bubble conclusion in a sequential content-sharing model: agents decide whether to share a single article and fear be-ing called out for propagating misinformation, and an engagement-maximizing algorithm builds homophilous communication that relaxes the discipline on sharing low-reliability content. We in-stead embed amplification in a coordination game with dispersed information, where the platform designs the directed observation and reference network. Our central results—the global-broadcast transparency reversal, the precision-based characterization within the broadcast class, and the dem-agogue threshold in the canonical broadcast-with-local-reference benchmark—have no counterpart in a sharing model, and the engagement divergence here operates through bias validation and co-ordination comfort rather than reduced sharing discipline. The two papers ask the same question with different machinery and arrive at complementary conclusions. The interpolation between accuracy and engagement has an intervention-specific switch point. Two points clarify the mapping to Morris and Shin (2002). Attention: reallocation versus density. The fixed-network analysis of Section 3 compares given topologies: the transparency and demagogue results are fixed-topology comparisons, while the attention-budget exercise of Section 3.3 and Numerical Result 1 additionally holds a per-agent out-degree m fixed and asks how to allocate reach. The recommendation protocol below instead lets each user independently accept or reject one proposed addition and one proposed deletion per period, so the realized out-degree—and hence network density—can drift over time. The dynamic comparisons therefore mix attention reallocation with densification and pruning. We report degree and density trajectories alongside accuracy in Appendix F and confirm there that the accuracy/polarization ordering across rules is primarily driven by the bias channel rather than by density: in the bias-free case all rules densify similarly and the orderings collapse (Numerical Result 2), whereas with bias the orderings open up at comparable densities. A fixed-attention swap variant of the protocol, in which each accepted addition is paired with a forced deletion so out-degree is held at m, yields the same qualitative ordering; we use the optional-attention protocol in the main text because it is the more natural model of users accepting or declining recommendations. 19 4.1 Recommendation dynamics and fixed points 

At each period, the accuracy planner evaluates marginal changes to the network using a strict one-add/one-drop rule. For each user, it proposes one accuracy-improving link addition, interpreted as an invitation, and one accuracy-improving link deletion, interpreted as an annoyance or suggested unfollow. A candidate is proposed only if it strictly raises accuracy relative to the current network; if no addition (resp. deletion) raises accuracy, none is proposed to that user. The user then chooses among the accept/reject combinations of whatever was proposed to maximize her own expected utility. Appendix F gives the algorithm. Because the proposal rule is local, the simulations are best read as a disciplined model of algorithmic curation dynamics rather than as a universal stability concept. Let 

Mt+1 = Ψ( Mt)be the induced network transition. A network M is a rest point if Ψ( M ) = M, 

so no user accepts any recommendation the planner would make. The key implementation issue is the gap between the planner’s accuracy objective and private incentives. The planner proposes changes that increase (3); users accept only changes that increase their own expected payoffs. 

4.2 Alignment and the polarization wedge 

Section 3.4 characterizes which fixed networks improve accuracy, but it does not show whether users will accept the links needed to implement those networks. A proposed link changes both information exposure and reference weights, so the sign of the user’s private payoff change cannot be derived in general. Numerical Results 2–3 form a control–treatment comparison: the first asks whether the liberal constraint binds without bias; the second adds bias and isolates the resulting implementation wedge. 

Numerical Result 2. When bi = 0, users’ incentives are closely aligned with accuracy. In the simulated finite asymmetric networks, a link addition that raises accuracy typically also lowers the observing user’s mean-squared error and raises her covariance with the newly observed source— and with other users who load on that source—thereby reducing the relevant coordination losses, though not necessarily her covariance with every coordination neighbor. These signed effects are a numerical regularity rather than an analytic implication, because a link addition also changes the receiver’s reference weights and the full equilibrium fixed point; in a small share of cases (3% of accuracy-improving proposals; Appendix F) the equilibrium feedback makes such a link privately unattractive. As a result, users accept accuracy-improving recommendations at high rates (97%) and the simulated dynamics move toward the broadcast-core structure. 20 Numerical Result 3. When bi > 0, private incentives can diverge from the planner’s accuracy objective. In the simulated equilibria, links to similar-bias neighbors typically reduce the relevant coordination loss ( dj − di)2, while precise opposite-type sources are often privately rejected even when they improve collective accuracy. The resulting implementation wedge generates homophilous rest points below the corresponding accuracy-planner benchmark. Taken together, the two results show that rejection is not a mechanical feature of the recom-mendation protocol. It is rare in the bias-free control and becomes systematic when confirmation motives create a gap between private utility and truth-tracking accuracy. The claims remain nu-merical because the full equilibrium feedback of a link change cannot be signed analytically in general.

# 6 Discussion and conclusion 

This paper studies algorithmic amplification as a network-design problem. A platform does not need to change signals in order to change beliefs and actions: by changing who observes whom, it changes which private signals become common reference points. The accuracy consequences of that structural lever are governed by three separable objects—the precision of the amplified source, the source’s bias as a coordination reference, and the objective used to allocate visibility—and the same source can be socially valuable under one objective and harmful under another. The accuracy analysis delivers three design principles. First, in a reference-neutral global-broadcast benchmark, broadcast reach can restore the social value of transparency through a two-common-signal substitution: a precise broadcast source creates a second common signal— equivalently, an effective shift of common precision (Remark 1)—so the public signal no longer serves as the unique focal point for coordination. Second, within the symmetric large-N broadcast class, reach should be allocated to precision in the monotone region α ≥ β/ 8; finite-network compu-tations support the same rule under the baseline attention constraints—exactly for N ≤ 6, and as a local-search regularity up to N = 20 at the baseline calibration—while outside that region, whether to use a broadcast budget at all is a separate design margin. Third, precision is not sufficient. In general networks and under arbitrary heterogeneous biases, the exact bias cost is a quadratic form in an influence-overlap matrix. Its diagonal entries are source-specific bias-amplification multipliers and its off-diagonal entries capture reinforcement or offsetting between sources; the resulting score in (12) ranks candidate amplifications exactly. In the canonical single-source broadcast-with-local-reference benchmark this reduces to the closed-form demagogue threshold in (14). A platform should therefore evaluate source accuracy, source neutrality, and interaction with the existing bias composition before assigning broad reach. The subjective-engagement analysis identifies the boundaries of the conflict rather than assum-ing it. The exact decomposition separates information, own-validation, and coordination effects, and a compact finite-network robustness exercise shows that the qualitative three-region pattern survives several coordination calibrations. The engagement-only region is therefore a conditional property of the coupled subjective-utility model, not a universal prediction about commercial rec-ommenders. In the baseline recommendation protocol, the engagement platform nevertheless pro-duces lower truth-tracking accuracy and greater polarization than the accuracy planner. The decoupling exercise localizes the policy mechanism. Holding reference weights fixed makes 29 the bias profile and polarization invariant to the information graph, so the engagement platform loses the reference-group lever used to build an echo chamber. It does not make the two objectives equivalent over information networks: their signal and covariance terms can still rank information graphs differently. The statement that informational gains survive decoupling applies directly to the uniform-weight broadcast benchmarks of Theorems 2–3; general finite-network accuracy can still depend on the coordination weights. The regulatory interpretation is correspondingly conditional. For an intervention with ∆ W < 

0 < ∆Π, lowering the effective engagement weight below its source-specific λ∗ reverses the platform’s decision. Systemic-risk assessment, recommender disclosure, or non-profiled feed options can be interpreted through this mechanism only insofar as they reduce that effective weight or separate information exposure from salient reference groups. The model does not establish that these policies improve every platform environment. Several extensions are natural. First, beliefs here are static: the state is redrawn each period and agents do not learn, which isolates the reach channel but abstracts from the interaction be-tween amplification and belief dynamics; embedding the feed/reference design problem in a learning environment would connect the demagogue threshold to persistent belief distortions. Second, en-gagement is reduced-form; a microfoundation estimated from retention or interaction data would discipline the effective weight λ and make the regulatory knob empirically operational. Third, source precision is exogenous; allowing sources to invest in accuracy in anticipation of reach would endogenize the supply side of amplification. Finally, the model’s central design objects are in prin-ciple estimable, but not from observation-network data alone. Given estimates of the observation network, coordination primitives ( R, Φ), source precisions, and source biases, one can compute the influence-overlap matrix and evaluate information gains against the full quadratic bias cost. Reach data of the kind already observed at scale (Husz´ ar et al., 2022; Aridor et al., 2024) provide one necessary input; estimating the remaining primitives is a substantive empirical task. The role of bias. The separation in Figure 3 requires non-trivial bias. With bi ≈ 0, information is a pure good: every recommendation rule adds informative links, the network densifies toward com-pleteness (the first best of Lemma 1), and the three policies coincide. Bias is what makes which links form matter—friends-of-friends rewiring reinforces homophily and random rewiring attaches follow-ers to biased neighbors, both dragging actions off the fundamental, while the accuracy-targeting planner routes around them. This is consistent with the demagogue mechanism of Section 3.4: the planner’s value added is in containing the bias channel. The density trajectories confirm that the accuracy ranking is not a density artifact: across rules the mean out-degree stays within a narrow 42 band (within roughly one link of the initial mean degree) over the horizon, so the accuracy gaps in Figure 3 arise at comparable densities rather than from one rule densifying more than another.
